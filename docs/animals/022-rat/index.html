1. Opening Summary

The brown rat (Rattus norvegicus) is one of the most consequential and most maligned animals in the human world, and one of the most important in the history of science. Familiar as a sewer-dwelling pest and a shorthand for treachery and disease, the brown rat is in reality a highly social, cooperative, and cognitively rich rodent whose behavior has repaid decades of close study. Ethologically it matters for three reasons that rarely coincide. First, it is a colonial burrowing mammal with structured dominance relationships, rich communication, and a striking capacity for prosocial behavior — helping and food-sharing that some researchers describe, cautiously, as "empathic." Second, it possesses formidable learning ability, spatial memory, and behavioral flexibility that underpin its success as an urban commensal and its resistance to control. Third, as the origin of the laboratory rat, it has shaped modern neuroscience, psychology, and medicine, which makes what we know about its mind both extensive and ethically charged. Studying the brown rat forces a confrontation between a caricature and a complex, feeling, learning animal.

2. Scientific Identity

The brown rat is commonly called the Norway rat, common rat, sewer rat, or street rat; a female is a doe, an intact male a buck, and the young are pups or kittens. Its scientific name is Rattus norvegicus. It belongs to the order Rodentia, the family Muridae, and the genus Rattus. The domesticated form kept as a pet or used in laboratories is the same species, sometimes labelled Rattus norvegicus domestica. The common name "Norway rat" is a historical misnomer; the species most likely originated in northern China and eastern Asia and spread globally with human trade, not from Norway.

Brown rats are among the most widely distributed mammals on Earth, found on every continent except Antarctica and on many islands, almost always in association with people. They are opportunistic omnivores, eating grain, seeds, fruit, invertebrates, human food waste, and, when available, small animals and carrion. In the wild, most brown rats live under a year owing to predation, disease, and control; in protected captivity they commonly live two to three years. As a widespread commensal, the brown rat is listed by the IUCN as Least Concern. It is distinct from the black rat (Rattus rattus), the other major commensal Rattus, discussed later in this book.

3. Evolutionary Background

Brown rat behavior is shaped by the ecology of a burrowing, ground-dwelling omnivore that evolved in the riverbanks and grasslands of eastern Asia. Selection favored a generalist able to exploit patchy, unpredictable food, to dig extensive burrow systems for shelter and breeding, and to live at high density alongside relatives. This produced the species' defining traits: powerful exploratory drive, fast learning, excellent spatial memory for navigating complex tunnels, and a strongly social temperament built around living in colonies of related and familiar individuals.

Two evolutionary pressures are especially important. Living in dense colonies against a background of predation selected for cooperation, individual recognition, graded communication, and the reciprocal exchanges that make helping behavior adaptive among kin and familiar partners. And eating a broad, shifting, sometimes dangerous diet selected for caution toward novelty (neophobia) and for a powerful capacity to learn which foods make an animal sick (learned taste aversion). These two adaptations — sociality on one hand, dietary caution on the other — explain much of what makes the brown rat both scientifically fascinating and famously difficult to poison. The commensal relationship with humans is recent in evolutionary terms but has spread the species worldwide almost entirely on the back of these pre-existing capacities.

4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Brown rats are primarily nocturnal, emerging around dusk and being most active in darkness, which reduces both predation risk and encounters with humans; where they are undisturbed they may extend activity into daylight. Their lives are organized around a home burrow system dug into banks, foundations, and waste ground, with multiple entrances, nesting chambers, and food stores. From this base they follow established runways to foraging sites, and they are strongly neophobic about changes along these routes.

They are colonial and social rather than solitary. Brown rats live in colonies centered on burrow systems, made up of overlapping family groups. Within and between these groups there are dominance relationships, with dominant males in particular defending territory and access to resources and mates. Rats are also notably organized in their use of space, keeping distinct areas for nesting, feeding, and defecation. A rat's day, then, is a cycle of resting communally in the burrow, cautious nocturnal foraging along known routes, scent-marking, grooming, and social interaction with familiar colony members whom it recognizes as individuals.

5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

The brown rat's perceptual world is dominated by smell and touch, with vision playing a comparatively minor role. Olfaction is exceptional: rats detect and discriminate a vast range of odors, use scent to recognize individuals, kin, colony membership, reproductive state, and stress, and lay scent trails that structure movement through the environment. A specialized vomeronasal organ detects pheromones important in social and reproductive behavior. Much of rat social life is, in effect, conducted through the nose.

Touch is equally central and is dominated by the vibrissae, the long facial whiskers. Rats sweep their whiskers back and forth several times a second in a behavior called whisking, actively palpating surfaces, gaps, and objects to build a tactile picture of their surroundings in the dark. This active-touch system is a major model for how brains process sensory information. Hearing extends well into the ultrasonic range, and rats both hear and produce ultrasonic vocalizations inaudible to humans (see Section 8). Vision is relatively poor, adapted for a nocturnal, near-ground life: rats are thought to have limited, dichromatic color vision and rely little on fine visual detail. This sensory profile — nose and whiskers first, ears next, eyes last — shapes everything from foraging to social recognition.

6. Feeding Behavior

The brown rat is an opportunistic omnivore and a generalist forager, and its feeding behavior is organized around two themes: exploiting almost any available food, and doing so cautiously. Rats forage along established runways, drawing on strong spatial memory to relocate productive sites, and they cache food, carrying items back to store near the burrow. This flexibility is a large part of why the species thrives in human environments, where waste and stored food are abundant.

Caution is the other half of the picture and has important practical consequences. Brown rats are markedly neophobic toward new food and new objects in familiar places, often approaching a novel item repeatedly and sampling only a small amount before waiting. If that small sample later makes the animal ill, it forms a powerful, long-lasting learned taste aversion and avoids the food thereafter — a phenomenon central to the classic work of John Garcia on conditioned taste aversion. This combination of neophobia and one-trial taste-aversion learning is precisely what makes rats so difficult to poison with acute baits: a sub-lethal dose teaches the colony to avoid the bait, a problem known to pest managers as "bait shyness." Rats can also acquire food preferences socially, being drawn to foods whose odor they detect on the breath and fur of colony-mates that have eaten safely.

7. Social Behavior

Brown rats are fundamentally social animals that live in colonies built on individual recognition, kinship, and familiarity. The basic fabric is a set of overlapping family groups sharing a burrow system, within which rats recognize colony-mates by scent and treat familiar individuals very differently from strangers. Cooperative behaviors — huddling for warmth, allogrooming (grooming one another), communal nesting, and shared use of burrows — knit the colony together, while unfamiliar intruders are typically met with aggression.

Superimposed on this cooperative core are dominance relationships. Dominant males, in particular, establish and defend territories and priority of access to food, shelter, and mates, and status is negotiated through displays, chasing, boxing, and, in escalated cases, fighting, with a characteristic subordinate posture used to signal submission. Once established, these relationships reduce continual conflict. Rats also learn a great deal socially, acquiring information about food and the environment from colony-mates, which is why social context is central to rat behavior and cognition. This blend of cooperation among the familiar and competition over rank makes the brown rat a rich subject for the study of mammalian social organization.

8. Communication

Brown rats communicate through an integrated system of scent, vocalization, posture, and touch, with scent and ultrasound especially prominent. Olfactory signals carry a great deal of information: scent marks and body odors advertise individual identity, sex, reproductive state, dominance, and even stress, and rats deposit urine and gland secretions along runways to structure the social and spatial world.

Vocally, much of the rat's repertoire lies in the ultrasonic range, above human hearing, and different call frequencies map onto different emotional states. Adult rats emit around 22 kHz calls in situations of distress, fear, and defeat — an alarm and negative-affect signal — and around 50 kHz calls in positive contexts such as play, anticipation of reward, mating, and, notably, when tickled by a familiar human. This 50 kHz "chirping," studied extensively by Jaak Panksepp and colleagues, has been described as a possible analogue of the antecedents of human laughter and is discussed further in Section 11. Pups emit ultrasonic isolation calls that summon the mother when they are cold or separated from the nest. Alongside these signals, rats use body postures, piloerection, tooth-chattering, and physical contact such as nudging and grooming to negotiate encounters. This multimodal system supports the individually structured colonies described above.

9. Reproduction and Courtship

Brown rats are highly prolific breeders, which is central to both their ecological success and their pest status. Where conditions allow they breed year-round, and females are polyestrous, cycling roughly every four to five days when not pregnant. Courtship centers on chemical signals: a female in estrus advertises her state through scent and behavior, and males detect and pursue receptive females, with mating involving characteristic approach, following, and repeated brief copulations. Females may mate with several males, and dominant males typically gain more matings, so male competition over status translates into reproductive advantage.

Gestation is short, about 21 to 23 days, and litters are large, commonly around six to twelve pups and sometimes more. A female can enter estrus again very soon after giving birth, so reproduction can be nearly continuous under good conditions, and a single pair's descendants can multiply rapidly — the biological basis of the rat's reputation for explosive population growth. This reproductive strategy of many, fast-developing young, produced frequently, is a classic fast life-history pattern suited to an animal facing high predation and exploiting unpredictable, human-linked food supplies.

10. Parenting and Early Development

Rat parenting is centered on the mother, who prepares a nest, gives birth, and cares intensively for the litter. Pups are born altricial — blind, deaf, hairless, and helpless — and depend completely on maternal care. The mother nurses, retrieves straying pups back to the nest in response to their ultrasonic isolation calls, and performs extensive licking and grooming. This maternal licking is not incidental: a celebrated line of research associated with Michael Meaney and colleagues found that natural variation in how much mothers lick and groom their pups is linked to lasting differences in the offspring's stress responses and behavior, a landmark example of early experience shaping later biology through the mother's behavior.

Pups develop quickly. They gain fur and open their eyes over the first two weeks, begin to explore and sample solid food, and learn foraging cues and food preferences partly from the mother and other colony members. Young rats engage in vigorous social play — chasing, pouncing, and rough-and-tumble "play fighting" — that appears important for developing social and motor skills and that is accompanied by 50 kHz calls. Weaning occurs at roughly three to four weeks, after which juveniles integrate into colony life, and females can breed within a few months. Early life is high-risk, with predation, cold, and social disruption all taking a toll, which fits the species' strategy of producing many young.

11. Learning and Intelligence

The brown rat is one of the most intensively studied animals in the history of learning research, and the evidence for its cognitive sophistication is deep. Rats excel at operant and maze learning, forming associations between actions and outcomes with speed and flexibility, and much of what is taught about conditioning derives from work with them. They possess excellent spatial memory: research on the rat hippocampus, including the discovery of place cells by John O'Keefe, revealed neurons that fire when an animal is in a particular location, work central to the "cognitive map" theory of navigation and later recognized with a Nobel Prize. Rats readily learn the layout of complex environments and can take novel shortcuts, evidence of flexible spatial representation rather than mere habit.

Two lines of research are especially notable. Studies of metacognition — associated with work by Allison Foote and Jonathon Crystal — suggest that rats may adjust their behavior according to how much they "know," for example declining a difficult memory test in favor of a guaranteed smaller reward, a pattern interpreted by some as sensitivity to their own uncertainty, though the interpretation remains debated. And the affective neuroscience of Jaak Panksepp linked the 50 kHz "laughter-like" calls emitted during play and tickling to a positive-emotion system, arguing that rats have basic emotional processes with deep evolutionary roots. Most striking is the work on prosocial behavior by Inbal Ben-Ami Bartal, Jean Decety, Peggy Mason, and colleagues, in which a free rat learned to open a restrainer to release a trapped cage-mate, and would even share food with it — behavior the authors discussed in terms of empathy. These findings are genuinely important but their interpretation is contested (see Section 14): critics argue the "helping" may be driven by a drive to reach a social partner or to end distressing signals rather than by empathy as such. The honest summary is that rats show flexible learning, memory, apparent metacognition, positive and negative emotions, and reliable prosocial responses, while the richest mentalistic labels for these behaviors remain scientifically open.

12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

The brown rat's defensive behavior combines caution, flight, and, when cornered, effective aggression. Neophobia is a first line of defense: wariness toward novel objects and foods reduces the risk of predation and poisoning. When a rat detects a threat it typically flees to the burrow along a known route, or freezes, and rats readily learn and remember the locations of danger. Colony members respond to alarm — including 22 kHz ultrasonic distress calls and alarm pheromones in the scent of frightened animals — with heightened vigilance and avoidance.

Cornered rats defend themselves vigorously, and a threatened rat may adopt an upright defensive posture, lunge, and bite; this is the origin of the phrase "cornered like a rat." Because the brown rat is a foundational model in the neuroscience of fear, a great deal is known about its stress physiology: fear conditioning in rats has mapped brain circuits, particularly involving the amygdala, that underlie learned fear across mammals, including humans. Chronic stress in laboratory and captive rats — from confinement, isolation, or unpredictability — produces measurable physiological and behavioral changes, which is both scientifically informative and ethically significant given the animal's capacity to suffer.

13. Human Interaction

Few wild mammals are as entangled with humans as the brown rat, and the relationship is deeply contradictory. As an urban commensal, the brown rat lives almost everywhere people do, exploiting our waste, buildings, and transport, and it is treated as a serious pest: it damages stored food and structures, and can carry pathogens. This has driven centuries of control efforts, complicated by the rat's neophobia and taste-aversion learning, which blunt acute poisons and have contributed to the development of anticoagulant rodenticides and, in turn, to the evolution of resistance in some rat populations.

At the same time, the brown rat is one of humanity's most important partners in science. Domesticated for research from the nineteenth century, the laboratory rat became a premier model organism in physiology, pharmacology, psychology, and neuroscience, and much of what is known about learning, memory, emotion, stress, and the brain comes from rats. This role carries heavy ethical weight: because rats are sentient, social, and capable of suffering and of positive states, their use in research is governed by welfare regulations, ethical review, and the principles of replacement, reduction, and refinement. Brown rats are also kept as intelligent, sociable pets ("fancy rats"), and are trained for practical tasks such as detecting landmines and tuberculosis by scent. Culturally the rat is ambivalent — a symbol of filth and betrayal in some traditions, of prosperity, cleverness, and adaptability in others. Untangling the real animal from this symbolism is one of the tasks of ethology.

14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

Myth 1: Rats caused the Black Death simply by being dirty, filthy animals. Scientific reality: The historical picture is more complex than "rats spread plague through filth." Plague is caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis and, in the classic account, was transmitted chiefly by fleas — especially rat fleas — with the rat acting as a host rather than infecting people directly through dirt. Moreover, recent historical and modelling work has questioned how large a role rats played at all in the rapid spread of the medieval Black Death, with some researchers arguing that human ectoparasites or person-to-person routes better fit the pace of the epidemic. Rats deserve careful, evidence-based blame-apportioning, not a simple story of contamination by filth.

Myth 2: Rats are dumb, mindless pests. Scientific reality: The brown rat is one of the most capable learners studied. It masters operant and maze tasks, navigates using hippocampal "place cells" and cognitive maps (O'Keefe's Nobel-recognized work), forms powerful one-trial taste aversions, learns socially from colony-mates, and shows apparent metacognition. Its "pest" success is a direct expression of intelligence and flexibility, not stupidity.

Myth 3: Rats are solitary vermin. Scientific reality: Brown rats are highly social, colonial animals. They live in overlapping family groups sharing burrow systems, recognize individuals by scent, groom and huddle together, cooperate, and negotiate dominance relationships. Isolation is stressful for them, and much of their behavior only makes sense in a social context.

Myth 4: Rats are incapable of anything resembling empathy. Scientific reality: In experiments by Bartal, Decety, Mason and colleagues, free rats reliably freed trapped cage-mates from a restrainer and even shared food with them, and rats emit distinct positive and negative emotional calls. This is strong evidence of prosocial behavior and basic emotions. Whether it should be called "empathy" is genuinely debated — critics suggest the helping may reflect a drive for social contact or reduction of distressing signals rather than empathy proper — so the behavior is real and reliable, but its deepest interpretation remains open.

Myth 5: If a poison did not kill the rats, you simply need more of the same bait. Scientific reality: A sub-lethal dose of an acute poison teaches rats to avoid it. Their neophobia leads them to sample new baits in small amounts, and their strong learned taste aversion means a single bad experience produces lasting "bait shyness" across the colony. Effective control has to work with this psychology, not against it, which is one reason slow-acting anticoagulants replaced fast acute poisons.

15. Comparison With Another Species

The most instructive comparison is between the brown rat and the house mouse (Mus musculus), the other great rodent commensal of human settlements. Both are small, nocturnal, omnivorous murids that exploit human food and shelter, both are prolific breeders with fast life histories, both rely heavily on smell and whisker-based touch, both communicate using ultrasonic vocalizations, and both have become foundational laboratory model organisms. Their success rests on similar ingredients: adaptability, fast learning, and a close association with people.

Yet they differ in revealing ways. The brown rat is much larger, more powerfully built, more neophobic, and a committed burrower that digs extensive tunnel systems, whereas the smaller house mouse is more neophilic (often quick to investigate novelty), nests in cavities and structures rather than deep burrows, and can exploit tiny spaces and drier conditions. This difference in temperament has practical consequences: the mouse's curiosity makes it comparatively easier to trap with novel devices, while the rat's caution makes it warier of new objects and baits. The two also tend to compete, with the larger, more aggressive brown rat often displacing mice or the smaller black rat where they overlap. Comparing them shows how two closely related commensals can pursue the same broad strategy — living off humans — through contrasting behavioral styles, one bold and space-exploiting, the other cautious and burrow-based.

16. Ethology Lessons

The brown rat teaches several broad lessons in animal behavior. First, it is a powerful case study in how sociality and cognition reinforce one another: living in dense colonies of familiar, related individuals created the conditions in which recognition, communication, cooperation, and prosocial helping could evolve, and studying the rat has helped reveal the biology of learning, memory, emotion, and social behavior across mammals. Second, the rat illustrates how caution can be a form of intelligence. Neophobia and one-trial taste-aversion learning look like timidity but are sophisticated risk-management adaptations, and they explain the practical difficulty of controlling the species.

Third, the rat sharpens how ethologists talk about minds. Its prosocial "helping" and 50 kHz "laughter-like" calls sit exactly on the boundary between well-documented behavior and contested interpretation, making the rat a model for how to report findings honestly: the behaviors are real and replicable, while labels like "empathy" and "laughter" should be flagged as interpretations under debate rather than settled facts. Finally, the brown rat links pure ethology to ethics. Because it is demonstrably a learning, social, feeling animal — and because it is science's most-used mammalian model and a global pest — what we know about its mind bears directly on how it is treated, in laboratories, in cities, and in the stories we tell about it.
