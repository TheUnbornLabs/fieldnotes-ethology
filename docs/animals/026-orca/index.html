1. Opening Summary

The orca, or killer whale (Orcinus orca), is the largest member of the dolphin family and one of the most compelling subjects in all of ethology. It is a global apex predator that hunts everything from herring to great whales, yet its most remarkable features are social and cultural rather than merely physical. Across the world's oceans, orcas are divided into distinct populations, or ecotypes, that differ in diet, body form, and behaviour — some eat only fish, others only marine mammals — and that maintain these differences even where their ranges overlap. Many of these differences are learned and passed down, not encoded solely in genes. Orcas live in among the most stable societies known in any non-human animal: matrilines built around long-lived females, in which offspring of both sexes may stay with their mother for their entire lives. Within these groups, family members share vocal dialects specific to their matriline, transmitted like a spoken tradition. Females undergo menopause and live for decades afterward, apparently serving as knowledge-keepers who improve their family's survival — one of only a handful of species in which post-reproductive life is well documented. This book examines how orcas perceive, hunt, bond, and learn, and how careful science can separate the genuine sophistication of these animals from the "killer" mythology that surrounds them.

2. Scientific Identity

The orca, Orcinus orca, is a toothed whale (suborder Odontoceti) and, despite its size and the name "killer whale," is the largest member of the oceanic dolphin family, Delphinidae. It belongs to the order Cetartiodactyla, which unites cetaceans with even-toed ungulates. Orcas are unmistakable: robust black-and-white bodies, a conspicuous white eye patch, a grey "saddle" behind the dorsal fin, and — in adult males — a tall, erect dorsal fin that can exceed a metre and a half. Males reach roughly 8 to 9 metres and females somewhat less, making orcas by far the largest of the dolphins. They are the most widely distributed of all marine mammals, found from the poles to the tropics in every ocean, though they are most abundant in cooler, productive waters. Diet varies enormously between populations, from fish and squid to seals, sea lions, dolphins, and even large whales. Lifespan is long: females may live 50 to 80 years or more, males typically less. On the IUCN Red List, Orcinus orca is currently assessed as Data Deficient at the species level because the animals may in fact comprise several species or subspecies, but particular populations — notably the Southern Resident population of the northeastern Pacific — are recognised as endangered.

3. Evolutionary Background

Orcas descend from land mammals whose lineage returned to the sea over 50 million years ago, and their behaviour reflects the demands of an air-breathing, warm-blooded apex predator in a sound-dominated ocean. As with other toothed whales, echolocation evolved to hunt and navigate where light fails. But the orca's defining evolutionary story is the diversification of its populations into distinct ecotypes. In several ocean regions, sympatric orca populations — living in the same waters — specialise on entirely different prey and rarely, if ever, interbreed. In the North Pacific, "resident" orcas eat fish, especially salmon; "Bigg's" (transient) orcas eat marine mammals; and "offshore" orcas appear to specialise partly on sharks. These ecotypes differ in diet, in acoustic behaviour, in social structure, and even in subtle features of body shape and pigmentation. Because they remain genetically and behaviourally distinct despite overlapping ranges, biologists increasingly view them as cases of incipient speciation — populations in the early stages of splitting into separate species, driven substantially by learned foraging culture rather than by geographic isolation alone. The orca is therefore a leading natural example of how culture may shape evolution.

4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Orcas are active around the clock, structuring their movements around prey rather than around a fixed day-night rhythm. Fish-eating residents may travel widely along coastlines following salmon runs; mammal-hunting Bigg's orcas move quietly and unpredictably, often in smaller groups, to ambush wary prey. Like other cetaceans, orcas cannot afford to lose consciousness fully, because they must surface to breathe; they rest with one brain hemisphere at a time in unihemispheric slow-wave sleep, keeping enough awareness to swim and breathe. Socially, orcas are neither territorial in the classic sense nor loosely aggregated. Their defining pattern is the stable matriline: a mother and her descendants who travel together, often for life. Several related matrilines form a pod; pods that share dialect and ancestry form larger clans. Daily life mixes travel, foraging, resting, socialising, and play, and groups often coordinate these activities closely, moving and surfacing in near-synchrony. This combination of wide-ranging movement and deep social stability is central to how orcas exploit rich but patchy marine resources across enormous ranges.

5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

The orca's perceptual world is built above all on sound. Its central sensory capacity is echolocation, or biosonar: the animal produces rapid trains of high-frequency clicks, focused through a fatty structure in the forehead called the melon, and interprets the echoes that return from prey and surroundings. Returning sound reaches the inner ear largely through fat-filled channels in the lower jaw. From the timing and quality of echoes, an orca can locate and characterise objects in water where vision is useless. This is a physical, sound-based sense, not anything mysterious. Interestingly, the fish-eating residents and mammal-eating Bigg's orcas differ in how they use sound: mammal prey such as seals and dolphins can hear orca clicks and calls, so Bigg's orcas tend to hunt in relative acoustic silence, echolocating sparingly and vocalising little until a kill is made, whereas fish-eating residents, whose prey hear poorly in the relevant range, are far more vocal. Orca hearing extends well above the human range. Vision is useful at close quarters and above the surface, touch matters greatly in social life, and smell is essentially absent in these fully aquatic mammals. The orca brain is large and elaborately convoluted, consistent with — though not by itself proof of — the species' complex social and cultural lives.

6. Feeding Behavior

Orcas are the ocean's top predator, and their hunting is among the most specialised and behaviourally diverse of any animal — much of it learned rather than innate. Different populations have distinct, culturally transmitted techniques suited to their prey. In the North Pacific, fish-eating residents locate and pursue salmon, often sharing catches within the family. Mammal-hunting Bigg's orcas stalk seals, sea lions, porpoises, and dolphins using stealth and coordination. In the Antarctic, some orcas perform "wave-washing": several animals swim in formation to create a wave that sweeps a seal off an ice floe into the water. Off Norway and Iceland, orcas practise "carousel feeding," cooperatively herding herring into a tight ball near the surface and stunning the fish with slaps of their tail flukes before eating them. At Península Valdés in Argentina, orcas deliberately beach themselves on shore — an intentional stranding — to seize sea lion pups from the surf, then wriggle back into deeper water. Elsewhere, coordinated groups attack much larger baleen whales, targeting calves and using teamwork to exhaust and overwhelm them. These techniques are typically specific to particular populations and are learned by the young over years of practice, making orca feeding a premier example of cooperative hunting and cultural transmission in the wild.

7. Social Behavior

The social life of the orca is its most distinctive feature and one of the most stable known in any mammal. The foundational unit is the matriline: a mother and her sons and daughters, and her daughters' offspring, travelling together. In some populations — notably the fish-eating residents of the North Pacific — this bond is extraordinarily durable: offspring of both sexes remain with their mother for life, a pattern of bisexual philopatry that is rare among mammals. Related matrilines associate as pods, and pods that share a common dialect and ancestry form clans. Mammal-hunting Bigg's orcas live in smaller, somewhat more flexible groups, better suited to stealthy ambush hunting, though matrilineal bonds remain central. Within these societies, cooperation is pervasive: family members hunt together, share food, defend one another, and coordinate movement. Post-reproductive females occupy an important position, appearing to lead the group during difficult times. Because males typically stay with their mothers rather than dispersing, mating occurs between members of different pods or clans that meet temporarily, avoiding inbreeding while preserving family stability. Set against the fluid societies of many other dolphins, orca society is remarkable for the sheer permanence of its family bonds.

8. Communication

Orcas communicate through a rich acoustic repertoire alongside touch and body movement, but their most studied signal is the matriline-specific vocal dialect. Orcas produce three broad classes of sound: echolocation clicks used mainly for sensing, whistles used in close social contexts, and pulsed calls — the stereotyped, repeated calls that form the core of their vocal identity. Each matriline and pod uses a particular set of these pulsed calls, and the repertoire is learned, not innate: calves acquire their family's dialect from their mothers and relatives. Related groups share more calls; distantly related groups share fewer; and the pattern of shared and distinct calls maps onto the animals' social and genealogical structure, functioning almost like a spoken tradition or accent that marks family membership. Dialects help relatives recognise one another and coordinate, and they may help maintain the boundaries between clans and ecotypes. Physical communication matters too: orcas touch, rub, breach, tail-slap, and spy-hop, using these actions in social and possibly signalling contexts. As with other cetaceans, it is important to be precise: orca dialects are a genuine, learned, group-specific vocal culture, but they are not a human-style language with grammar and open-ended meaning.

9. Reproduction and Courtship

Orcas have a polygynandrous mating system in which both sexes may mate with several partners, and there is no lasting pair bond. Because sons and daughters typically remain in their mother's matriline for life in many populations, mating must occur outside the immediate family: individuals mate with members of other pods or clans encountered when groups gather, which prevents inbreeding while keeping families intact. Males compete for reproductive access, though the details of orca courtship in the wild are less thoroughly documented than in some other cetaceans. Breeding can occur across a broad season, with peaks that vary by population. Gestation is long — around 15 to 18 months, among the longest of any dolphin — and females give birth to a single calf. The interval between calves is several years, reflecting heavy maternal investment. Females mature at around age 10 to 15 and stop reproducing in their 30s or 40s, yet they may live for decades more. This late maturity, long gestation, and slow calving rate mean orca populations grow slowly and recover slowly from losses, a fact with serious conservation implications for small, threatened populations.

10. Parenting and Early Development

An orca calf is born tail-first underwater and must reach the surface for its first breath within moments. From then on, maternal care is prolonged and central to orca life. Calves nurse for a year or more and stay in close association with their mothers for many years — indeed, in matrilineal populations, often permanently. This long dependency is a learning period as much as a nursing one: calves must acquire the family's hunting techniques, ranging patterns, social conventions, and vocal dialect, none of which is fully innate. A young orca learns to hunt by watching and practising alongside experienced relatives; mastering skills such as wave-washing or intentional beaching can take years of trial, guidance, and repetition, and mothers and other kin have been observed apparently teaching or facilitating these skills. Calves benefit from the care and knowledge of the whole matriline, not the mother alone, and older females in particular contribute experience. Early mortality can be significant, and the drawn-out investment each calf requires helps explain the slow reproductive pace of the species. The bond between mother and offspring is among the most enduring known in the animal kingdom.

11. Learning and Intelligence

Orcas are among the most cognitively capable animals in the sea, and the evidence is best presented proportionately. Their intelligence is most clearly expressed in social learning and culture. Foraging techniques — carousel feeding, wave-washing, intentional beaching, coordinated attacks on large whales — are learned traditions transmitted within populations, meeting reasonable definitions of animal culture. Vocal dialects are similarly learned and passed down, making orcas vocal learners, a capacity rare among mammals. The ecotypes themselves, distinguished largely by learned diet and behaviour, are perhaps the strongest case in nature for culture shaping the divergence of populations. Orcas coordinate complex, role-differentiated group hunts that require anticipating both prey behaviour and the actions of companions. In captivity and in the wild they show rapid problem-solving and behavioural innovation, and novel behaviours have been observed to spread through populations. The post-reproductive "grandmother effect" — in which old females improve their family's survival, especially when salmon are scarce — implies that ecological knowledge accumulated over a long life is a real cognitive resource for the group. As always, honesty sets limits: orcas do not have a human-style language, and claims of near-human reasoning outrun the evidence. What the data support is a flexible, deeply social, culturally transmitting intelligence.

12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

As the ocean's apex predator, an adult orca has essentially no natural predators, so its "defensive" behaviour is directed less at escaping enemies than at coordinating the group and protecting the young and vulnerable. Calves and sick individuals are shielded within the matriline, and family members closely coordinate movement and surfacing. The orca's formidable capabilities are used offensively in hunting rather than defensively against threats. The real dangers to modern orcas are human-caused. Underwater noise from shipping and industry can interfere with the echolocation and communication on which the animals depend, and for prey-limited populations this can reduce hunting success. Chemical pollution is a particularly insidious threat: because orcas are long-lived apex predators, they accumulate high concentrations of persistent contaminants such as PCBs in their blubber, which can impair immune function and reproduction and are passed from mother to calf through milk. Prey depletion — especially the decline of Chinook salmon for fish-eating residents — is a direct stressor, and nutritional stress, pollution, and noise can act together. The orca's "defensive" story in the modern ocean is therefore chiefly a conservation story about human pressures on an animal that fears little in nature.

13. Human Interaction

Orcas have a long and increasingly fraught relationship with humans. For centuries they were feared as ravenous "killers" and were sometimes shot by fishers who saw them as competitors. In the modern era, attitudes shifted dramatically after live orcas were captured and displayed in marine parks beginning in the 1960s, transforming public perception from monster to charismatic icon. That very captivity is now central to the ethical debate around the species. Orcas are large, wide-ranging, deeply social, and culturally complex, and a growing body of welfare science holds that these characteristics make captivity especially damaging: captive orcas show collapsed dorsal fins, shortened lifespans in many cases, stereotypic behaviours, aggression, and the disruption of natural family bonds, and the confinement of such animals is now widely regarded as ethically untenable. In the wild, threats are indirect but serious: prey depletion, chemical pollution, underwater noise, ship strikes, and disturbance. Some populations are of particular conservation concern — the Southern Resident orcas of the northeastern Pacific are endangered, their recovery blocked by declining Chinook salmon, high contaminant loads, and vessel noise. Notably, despite their fearsome reputation and their obvious capacity to kill large prey, there are no records of orcas killing humans in the wild. The overall picture is of a predator we once feared, then confined, and must now protect.

14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

Myth "Killer whales" are man-eating monsters that attack people.

Scientific reality There are no confirmed records of a wild orca killing a human. The name "killer whale" is thought to derive from "whale killer," a label whalers used because orcas hunt other whales — not because they hunt people. Wild orcas sometimes investigate boats or swimmers, but predation on humans is not part of their behaviour. The "monster" image is a cultural inheritance, not a description of the animal.

Myth Orcas are whales, not dolphins.

Scientific reality The orca is a toothed whale in the broad sense, but taxonomically it is the largest member of the oceanic dolphin family, Delphinidae. In other words, it is a dolphin — the biggest one there is. The everyday name "killer whale" obscures this relationship, but by descent and anatomy the orca is a giant dolphin.

Myth All orcas are alike and eat the same prey.

Scientific reality Orcas are divided into distinct ecotypes that differ in diet, body form, dialect, and behaviour. Some, such as North Pacific residents, eat only fish; others, such as Bigg's (transient) orcas, eat only marine mammals; still others specialise on sharks or on other prey. These populations can share the same waters yet rarely interbreed, and their differences are largely learned. Treating all orcas as a single, uniform predator misses one of the most interesting facts about the species.

Myth Captivity is harmless — orcas thrive in marine parks.

Scientific reality Orcas are large, far-ranging, socially and culturally complex animals whose needs cannot be met in a tank. Captive orcas commonly show collapsed dorsal fins, stereotypic repetitive behaviours, elevated aggression, disrupted family bonds, and, in many cases, shortened lives. The welfare problems are well documented, and the ethics of confining such animals are now widely questioned. The apparent contentment of a performing orca is not evidence of well-being.

Myth Echolocation is a mysterious "sixth sense."

Scientific reality Echolocation is biosonar — an ordinary physical process. The orca emits high-frequency clicks, focuses them through the fatty melon, and interprets the echoes that return from objects and prey, receiving them mainly through fat channels in the lower jaw. It is sensing by sound, governed by physics, not telepathy or extrasensory perception.

Myth Menopause in orcas is just old age with no purpose.

Scientific reality Female orcas stop reproducing decades before they die, a genuine post-reproductive stage documented in only a few mammals. Research indicates that these older females act as ecological knowledge-keepers, leading their families to salmon during lean years and improving the survival of their descendants — the "grandmother effect." Menopause in orcas appears to be an adaptation that lets experienced females help their relatives, not merely the wearing-out of the body.

15. Comparison With Another Species

A revealing comparison is with the bottlenose dolphin (Tursiops truncatus), the orca's smaller relative in the same family, Delphinidae. Both are large-brained, long-lived, culturally transmitting cetaceans, so setting them side by side highlights how differently social life and culture can be organised even within one family. The central contrast is one of stability and scale. Bottlenose dolphins live in fluid "fission-fusion" societies in which group membership changes by the hour; a dolphin's companions in the morning may differ by afternoon, and males build shifting, multi-level alliances to compete for mates. Orca society is almost the opposite in structure: it is built on extraordinarily stable matrilines in which, in many populations, offspring of both sexes stay with their mother for life, and where family bonds can persist for decades. Culture expresses itself differently too. In bottlenose dolphins, foraging traditions such as sponge-carrying and mud-ring feeding spread through networks and mother-to-daughter lines within a fluid population. In orcas, culture is anchored to the matriline and clan: vocal dialects and specialised hunting techniques are family property, and distinct ecotypes maintain separate diets and dialects even in shared waters, to the point of behaving like incipient species. In scale, the orca is the ocean's apex predator and dwarfs the bottlenose dolphin, which is itself sometimes prey to orcas. Together the two show that dolphin culture is not one thing: it ranges from the fluid, alliance-based world of the bottlenose dolphin to the rigid, matriline-anchored, dialect-bearing societies of the orca.

16. Ethology Lessons

The orca teaches several durable lessons for the study of behaviour. First, it is perhaps the clearest natural demonstration that culture — behaviour transmitted by social learning rather than genes — can shape the divergence of populations: the ecotypes, distinguished largely by learned diet and dialect and drifting toward separate species, suggest that culture can be a force in evolution itself. Second, orca matrilines show that extreme social stability, with lifelong bonds between mother and offspring, can evolve in a large predator, offering a counterpoint to the fluid societies of many other social mammals. Third, the "grandmother effect" reveals that post-reproductive life can be adaptive, with accumulated ecological knowledge in old females materially improving their families' survival — a rare and important parallel to human menopause. Fourth, cooperative hunts such as wave-washing and carousel feeding demonstrate role-differentiated teamwork and long apprenticeships in a non-primate. Finally, the orca is a case study in proportionate science: an animal can be genuinely extraordinary — the ocean's top predator, a bearer of culture and dialect — without being either mythologised as a monster or sentimentalised, and the honest task of ethology is to describe its real, bounded capacities accurately.
