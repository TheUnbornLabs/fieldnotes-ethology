1. Opening Summary

The Great Cormorant (Phalacrocorax carbo) is a large, dark seabird that dives to depths of 10+ meters to hunt fish underwater, propelling itself with powerful feet. Unlike most aquatic birds, cormorants have partly wettable plumage: the outer portion of the body feathers soaks up water, which reduces buoyancy and helps the bird dive and stay submerged, while an inner layer of plumage retains some insulating air. The trade-off is that waterlogged feathers must be dried, which is why cormorants are so often seen perched with wings spread. (The older shorthand that cormorants simply "lack waterproofing oil" is a simplification of this feather-structure adaptation.) Great cormorants breed in colonies on cliffs and in trees, raising chicks through cooperative feeding. In East Asia, particularly Japan and China, cormorants have been trained by humans for centuries to hunt fish for people, a cultural practice still maintained in some regions. For ethologists, cormorants exemplify underwater hunting specialization, the trade-offs of feather structure, colonial breeding, and deep human-animal cultural partnerships.

2. Scientific Identity

The Great Cormorant, Phalacrocorax carbo, is a large waterbird weighing 2.5–3.5 kg with a 1.3–1.6 meter wingspan. Found across Europe, Asia, Africa, and Australia in coastal and inland water bodies. Diet consists of fish captured by underwater pursuit diving. Lifespan is 20–30 years. IUCN status: Least Concern.

3. Evolutionary Background

Cormorants evolved underwater pursuit diving in freshwater and coastal environments. Their partly wettable plumage reduces buoyancy and aids diving; the associated cost is reduced insulation and the need to dry the feathers afterward, expressed in the familiar wing-spreading (spread-wing) posture. This trade-off between diving performance and waterproofing is a classic example of an adaptive compromise.

4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Cormorants are diurnal, highly gregarious, diving during the day for fish and roosting on cliffs, trees, or posts at night. Breeding occurs in colonies. Daily routines involve hunting, socializing, drying feathers, and interacting with colony members.

5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

Cormorants have vision adapted for underwater hunting, with a highly flexible lens that helps them focus clearly both in air and beneath the surface. Underwater they rely chiefly on vision to detect and pursue prey. (Note: as birds, cormorants do not possess the lateral-line sense found in fish and amphibians; their prey detection is primarily visual.)

6. Feeding Behavior

Cormorants dive from the surface, propelling themselves mainly with their large webbed feet (rather than the wings) to pursue fish underwater; dives commonly reach around 10 meters and can be deeper. They consume fish whole. They are efficient predators and can take a substantial mass of fish per day.

7. Social Behavior

Cormorants are highly social, breeding in colonies and often roosting communally. Pair bonds form during breeding; both parents feed chicks through regurgitation.

8. Communication

Cormorants produce grunts, hisses, and ritualized displays used in territorial and pair-bonding contexts.

9. Reproduction and Courtship

Pairs breed in colonies. Females lay 3–4 eggs; both parents incubate and feed chicks.

10. Parenting and Early Development

Chicks are altricial. Parents feed them by regurgitation for extended periods. Fledging occurs after 8–10 weeks.

11. Learning and Intelligence

Chicks learn diving through practice and observation. Trained cormorants in human fisheries demonstrate significant learning capacity and habituation to humans.

12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

Cormorants face predation from eagles on breeding islands. Humans have persecuted cormorants extensively due to perceived competition with fish industries, leading to culling and netting to prevent breeding.

13. Human Interaction

Cormorants have been used in traditional fishing in East Asia for over 1,300 years. Fishermen train cormorants to dive and catch fish, which are then recovered from the bird. In Europe and North America, cormorants are often persecuted as pests affecting sport and commercial fishing. The species has benefited from legal protections and population recovery in some regions.

14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

Myth Cormorants are completely waterproof like other seabirds.

Scientific reality Cormorants have partly wettable plumage that reduces buoyancy and aids diving, so their feathers become waterlogged and must be dried. This is why cormorants perch with wings held open in the characteristic spread-wing posture, which promotes drying (and may also help thermoregulation). Drying can take a substantial amount of time after foraging.

Myth All aquatic birds have the same diving abilities.

Scientific reality Diving depth, duration, and hunting style vary greatly among aquatic birds. Cormorants are deep-diving specialists.

Myth Trained cormorants in fisheries are forcibly enslaved.

Scientific reality Trained cormorants in traditional Asian fisheries show learned behaviors and habituation; they return to trainers and receive food rewards. The relationship is complex but not necessarily cruel.

Myth Cormorants cannot survive in freshwater.

Scientific reality Great cormorants inhabit both coastal and freshwater environments, including large lakes and rivers.

Myth Cormorants are universal fish-eaters and consume everything.

Scientific reality Cormorants specialize on fish but show prey preference. Diet varies by region and available prey.

15. Comparison With Another Species

Great cormorants can be compared to Doublecrested Cormorants (Phalacrocorax auritus) of North America, which are similar in size and diving behavior but differ in range and some behavioral details. The comparison shows diving specialization across the cormorant family.

16. Ethology Lessons

Cormorants exemplify underwater pursuit diving specialization and the trade-offs of feather structure (lost waterproofing for enhanced diving). They show human-wildlife conflict in fishing contexts and the possibility of mutualistic human-animal partnerships (traditional fisheries). Cormorants illustrate how species can persist and thrive despite extensive human persecution, reflecting their adaptability and ecological success.

17. Glossary

Altricial — of chicks, helpless at hatching and requiring extended parental care. Foot-propelled diving — swimming underwater using the webbed feet for propulsion, as in cormorants. Monogamy — a mating system where individuals form pair bonds. Pursuit diving — chasing prey underwater using body propulsion. Spread-wing posture — the characteristic wing-holding-open behavior cormorants use to dry their wettable plumage after diving. Wettable plumage — feathers that absorb water, reducing buoyancy and aiding diving but requiring subsequent drying.
