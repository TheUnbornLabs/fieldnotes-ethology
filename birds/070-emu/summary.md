# Book 070 — The Emu: Summary

The Emu (*Dromaius novaehollandiae*) is Australia's largest bird, a flightless ratite weighing 40–60 kilograms and standing 1.5–2 meters tall. Unlike the polygamous ostrich, emus form monogamous pairs with a remarkable parental division of labor: males do all brooding and chick-rearing while females may depart before or shortly after hatching. Emus are highly nomadic, traveling vast distances across Australian habitats — semi-arid regions, open woodlands, grasslands, and deserts — in search of food and water. They are highly social, forming loose, dynamic flocks whose membership shifts with resource availability.

Emus are omnivorous ground foragers, consuming seeds, fruits, leaves, insects, and small animals. They obtain much water from food and must travel to waterholes during droughts. Their dietary flexibility and willingness to consume diverse foods allow survival in the unpredictable Australian environment. Emus are monogamous, and males invest heavily: they do all incubation (50–60 days without feeding), brood chicks for warmth, lead them to foraging areas, and defend them against predators. Females may stay to help initially but often leave the male to manage the brood alone.

Chicks are precocial, mobile within hours of hatching, and learn foraging and predator-avoidance by following the father. Early mortality is high from predation (especially dingoes and eagles) and starvation. Surviving chicks stay with the father for months, becoming fully independent at around 5–7 months (emus are flightless, so there is no true fledging). Young emus show individual recognition and remember food sources and water locations across large geographic ranges. Emus are wary birds alert to predators, defend chicks aggressively, and adjust behavior in response to environmental changes.

Emus have been used by Aboriginal Australians for millennia and were encountered by European colonizers who treated them as agricultural pests. The famous "Great Emu War" of 1932, when a military unit attempted to cull emus invading agricultural areas in Western Australia, largely failed and has become a symbolic example of human-wildlife conflict. Modern emus are protected in many areas and coexist with humans in modified Australian landscapes. The species is Least Concern, though populations fluctuate with drought cycles.

The emu exemplifies male parental care, nomadism in unpredictable environments, flexible social organization, human-wildlife conflict, and behavioral plasticity in modified landscapes.
