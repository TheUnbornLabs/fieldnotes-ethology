# Summary — The Village Weaver (*Ploceus cucullatus*)

The village weaver is a small, abundant, and intensely social African bird whose males are among the most skilful nest-builders in the animal world. Using strips of grass and leaf torn and carried in the bill, a breeding male threads, loops, and knots an enclosed, hanging nest with a woven entrance, and he builds many such nests within a colony, often crowded together in a single tree. He then displays at each completed nest to attract females. The ethological heart of the species lies in what happens next: the female inspects the nest and accepts or rejects it, and her judgement is central to the male's mating success. Males whose nests are repeatedly rejected may tear them down and rebuild. This makes the village weaver a vivid case of female choice acting on a constructed object.

Nest-building in weavers is a classic study system for the interplay of innate tendency and learning. The drive and general form of building have a strong innate basis, but building is not a rigid, flawless program: individuals vary, make errors, and improve with experience. The village weaver therefore cautions against the label "instinct-only." The enclosed, often pendent nest, sometimes placed over water, is primarily protective, making it harder for snakes and climbing predators to reach eggs and young. Some weavers also recognise and reject foreign eggs, a defence against brood parasites.

The species is colonial and polygynous. A successful male attracts several females to nests he has built, and the female takes the leading role in lining the nest, incubating a small clutch, and feeding the altricial young, which are provisioned largely with insects during the breeding season. Colonial life brings collective vigilance and mobbing against predators, alongside competition and conspicuousness.

Village weavers are dietary generalists eating seeds, grain, and insects, and they thrive around human settlement — hence the name. That same adaptability makes them agricultural pests in places, as flocks can damage cereal crops. The species is abundant and assessed as Least Concern. Compared with the sociable weaver, which builds a single giant communal structure, the village weaver's individual, male-built nests make the link between building skill, female inspection, and mating success unusually clear. Its lessons concern the innate-learned interplay, sexual selection on built objects, coloniality, and the ecology of human-adapted success.
