# Summary — The Ruby-throated Hummingbird (*Archilochus colubris*)

The ruby-throated hummingbird, *Archilochus colubris*, is a tiny member of the family Trochilidae (order Apodiformes) that weighs only about 3 to 4 grams yet sustains one of the highest metabolic rates of any vertebrate. It is the only hummingbird that regularly breeds in eastern North America, and it migrates to Mexico and Central America for winter. Its diet is nectar plus small insects and spiders, and it is assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List.

The species is an ethological case study in how energy shapes behavior. A body this small burns fuel extremely fast and can store almost none of it, so nearly everything the bird does follows from that energy budget. It hovers at flowers using an unusual figure-eight wingbeat that produces lift on both the downstroke and the upstroke, letting it hold still in air and fly backward. It feeds continuously through the day, drawing nectar with a fringed, extensible tongue and catching insects for the protein that nectar lacks.

Foraging is flexibly economic. A bird may defend a rich, concentrated nectar source aggressively when guarding pays, or trap-line along a circuit of scattered flowers when it does not, returning to each as it refills. This decision-making depends on the value of the resource, not on temperament. Efficient foraging rests on well-documented spatial and temporal memory: hummingbirds remember where flowers are, which they have emptied, and roughly when each refills.

To survive cold nights and food shortfalls, the bird can enter torpor, a controlled deep drop in body temperature and metabolic rate. Before autumn migration it lays down fat, and many individuals then cross the Gulf of Mexico in a single long over-water flight under their own power.

The mating system is promiscuous. Males perform arcing pendulum dive displays, flashing an iridescent throat patch called the gorget, but form no pair bond and give no parental care. The female alone builds an elastic, spider-silk cup nest, lays two tiny eggs, incubates them, and raises altricial young on regurgitated nectar and insects.

Ruby-throated hummingbirds are solitary and territorial, their social life mostly competition over flowers and mates. Compared with their swift relatives — fast, social, forward-flying aerial insectivores — they show how a shared aerial ancestry can diverge sharply. The bird's lasting lessons are about energetics, economic decision-making, and the specialised cognition a very small brain can support.
