# Summary — The Common Swift (*Apus apus*)

The common swift (*Apus apus*) is a small, sooty-brown, scythe-winged bird of the family Apodidae, more closely related to hummingbirds than to the swallows it superficially resembles. It breeds across Europe, North Africa, and temperate Asia and winters in sub-Saharan Africa, and it is currently assessed by the IUCN as Least Concern, though several regional populations have declined. The swift is the most thoroughly aerial of birds: it feeds, drinks, gathers nest material, sleeps, and often mates in flight, and tracking studies of wild birds provide strong evidence that non-breeding swifts can remain airborne almost continuously for months, landing mainly to breed.

Everything about the swift's body and behavior points toward a life in the moving air. Its long, narrow wings suit efficient, manoeuvrable flight, while its legs and feet are so reduced that the family name means "without feet"; the bird clings to vertical surfaces rather than perching or walking. It feeds on "aerial plankton" — small flying insects and ballooning spiders — flying with its mouth open and binding prey with saliva into a compact food ball that a parent carries back to its young. Because this food supply depends on warm weather, feeding, breeding, and migration are all tightly tied to conditions that concentrate insects in the air.

Swifts are colonial, gregarious birds that nest in cavities in buildings and cliffs, defending only the immediate nest hole. They are best known for the fast, calling "screaming parties" that circle rooftops on summer evenings. They are seasonally monogamous and show strong nest-site fidelity, returning across intercontinental migrations to the same small cavity year after year. Both parents incubate and feed the young, whose most striking adaptation is a torpor-like ability to survive days of food shortage when bad weather grounds the insects. When a young swift fledges it receives no further care, flying directly into an independent aerial life.

The swift's cognition is that of a navigational and aerial specialist rather than a tool-using problem-solver: it must find a specific nest cavity after months away and hold a course across continents, feats of spatial memory and orientation that make "instinct-only" an inadequate description. Its principal defence is speed and a concealed nest site rather than active resistance. Its closest tie to humans is its dependence on buildings for nesting, which makes the loss of cavities during renovation the main modern conservation concern, and has prompted the use of "swift bricks" and nest boxes. The swift teaches lessons about extreme specialisation, convergent evolution, and the way behavior and physiology together buffer an animal against an unreliable world.
