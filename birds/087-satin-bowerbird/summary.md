# Summary — The Satin Bowerbird (*Ptilonorhynchus violaceus*)

The satin bowerbird is the classic living demonstration of sexual selection through female choice. A mature male, glossy blue-black with a violet eye, clears a patch of forest floor in eastern Australia and builds a bower: two parallel walls of woven sticks forming an avenue, with a court decorated by carefully arranged objects that are overwhelmingly blue — feathers, flowers, berries, and, near people, blue plastic and litter. He paints the inner walls with chewed, pigmented plant matter, sometimes using a wad of material as an applicator, and he performs vigorous courtship displays accompanied by buzzing, mechanical calls and skilled vocal mimicry of other birds and sounds. The single most important fact about the bower is that it is not a nest. No eggs are laid and no young are raised there; it is purely a stage for courtship.

Females visit several bowers, inspect the structure, decorations, and display, and choose comparatively. Mating success is strongly skewed toward particular males, making the effective mating system polygynous. After mating the male's contribution ends entirely: the female leaves, builds her own nest elsewhere, and incubates and raises the altricial young alone, feeding them fruit and protein-rich insects. It is precisely this absence of male parental care that frees males to invest in bowers and display, and that lets sexual selection elaborate their behaviour.

A key part of the species' biology is delayed plumage maturation: young males wear the same green, scalloped plumage as females for several years before acquiring adult blue. During this long apprenticeship they build practice bowers, display, and appear to learn from adults, and the quality of bowers and displays improves with experience — evidence that these elaborate behaviours are learned and developmental, not fixed instinct. Males also compete directly, raiding rivals' bowers to steal decorations and wreck the structures, so a male must defend as well as build.

Ethologically, the satin bowerbird makes female choice visible; cautions against confusing a courtship arena with a nest; shows that even elaborate behaviour can be substantially learned; and warns against over-reading its decorating as human-like "art." Compared with the peacock, it shows that female choice can drive extravagance onto a built structure rather than onto the male's own body. It is widespread, common, and assessed as Least Concern.
