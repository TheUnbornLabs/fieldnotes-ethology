# Summary

The Wandering Albatross (*Diomedea exulans*) is among the largest flying birds and holds the greatest wingspan of any living species, with the largest individuals approaching or slightly exceeding 3.5 meters. It is a bird of the open Southern Ocean, spending most of its life at sea and returning to remote sub-Antarctic islands only to breed and molt. Its biology is defined by two linked themes: mastery of long-distance flight and an unusually slow, high-investment life history. Together these make the species a central example in the study of seabird ecology and behavior.

The albatross's signature behavior is dynamic soaring, a flight technique that extracts energy from the vertical gradient of wind speed above ocean waves. By repeatedly climbing into faster-moving air and descending across the wind, the bird can travel enormous distances with very little flapping, and satellite tracking has recorded individuals covering thousands of kilometers on a single foraging trip. This economy of flight underpins a foraging strategy in which birds range widely to find patchily distributed squid, fish, and crustaceans, taken mostly at the surface rather than by deep diving.

Reproductively, wandering albatrosses are among the slowest-breeding of all birds. They form long-term pair bonds, reinforced by elaborate, ritualized courtship displays, and successful pairs breed only once every two years because raising a single chick takes so long. The chick hatches helpless and depends on both parents, which alternate lengthy foraging trips to provision it; fledging takes roughly ten months or more. Combined with delayed maturity and great longevity, with many birds living beyond 40 to 50 years, this produces a life history that yields very few offspring per lifetime and recovers only slowly from any increase in adult mortality.

That slow tempo is precisely what makes the species vulnerable. Wandering albatrosses are readily hooked and drowned by longline fishing gear when they scavenge baited hooks, and bycatch has driven population declines that place the species in the IUCN Vulnerable category. Introduced predators such as rats and cats on breeding islands, along with marine plastic and climate-driven shifts in ocean systems, add further pressure. For ethologists, the wandering albatross illustrates how a highly specialized flight ecology and an extreme, low-reproduction life history are adaptive at sea yet leave the species acutely sensitive to human-caused mortality. It has become a flagship for seabird conservation and for mitigation measures that reduce bycatch.
