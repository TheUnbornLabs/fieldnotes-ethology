# Summary — The Mourning Dove (*Zenaida macroura*)

The Mourning Dove is one of North America's most abundant and widespread birds, a slender grayish-brown columbid of open country, farmland, and suburbs recognized across the continent by its soft, mournful cooing. That plaintive *coo-OO-oo-oo-oo* is the male's perch-coo, an advertising song used to hold a small breeding territory and attract a mate — and it is so owl-like in tone that it is the single most misidentified daytime bird sound in North American backyards.

The dove's success rests on a distinctive combination of columbid biology and an intense reproductive strategy. As a member of the family Columbidae — the pigeons and doves — it feeds its young, called squabs, on crop milk, a nutritious secretion sloughed from the lining of the crop. Crucially, both the male and the female produce this crop milk, so parental care is genuinely shared, and because the diet does not depend on catching insects, doves can breed whenever seeds are available. Squabs hatch altricial (naked and helpless) but grow fast on this rich food, leaving a famously flimsy twig-platform nest in under two weeks. Freed of the nest, a pair immediately starts again, so a single pair can raise several broods in one season. This multi-brooding on a cheap nest is the engine of the species' abundance.

The Mourning Dove is a ground-foraging granivore that gleans seeds whole and swallows grit — small stones, or gastroliths, that grind the hard seeds in its muscular gizzard. Like other columbids it drinks by suction, keeping its bill immersed and drawing water up continuously without tilting its head back, an unusual ability among birds that lets it rehydrate in seconds at an exposed waterhole. Its escape from predators depends on fast, strong, direct flight launched in an explosive takeoff. That takeoff produces the wing-whistle — a sharp twitter made mechanically by air rushing over modified flight feathers, not by the voice. Research indicates the whistle works as an automatic, honest alarm signal: a fleeing dove cannot help producing it, and nearby doves treat it as a cue to flush.

Socially, the dove alternates between seasonally monogamous pairs that defend nesting territory (with males chasing and even grappling with rivals) and gregarious flocks that forage, roost, and migrate together in the non-breeding season. It lives easily alongside people and is North America's most heavily hunted game bird, yet its prodigious breeding output keeps it abundant — a textbook example of a sustainably harvested, high-reproduction species. For ethology the Mourning Dove is a compact lesson in life-history strategy, in signals that are honest because they are unavoidable, in shared biparental care through crop milk, and in flexible seasonal sociality.
