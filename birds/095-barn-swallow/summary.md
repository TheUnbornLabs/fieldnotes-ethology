# Book 095 — The Barn Swallow: Summary

The barn swallow (*Hirundo rustica*) is one of the most widespread birds on Earth, a graceful, fork-tailed aerial insectivore in the family Hirundinidae. It breeds across much of North America, Europe, Asia, and North Africa and winters in the Southern Hemisphere and tropics, giving it one of the largest ranges of any passerine. Assessed as Least Concern, it is nonetheless in regional decline in some areas. Two things define its biology above all: a life lived on the wing, catching flying insects in continuous agile flight, and one of the animal kingdom's great long-distance migrations.

The barn swallow's body — streamlined form, long pointed wings, forked tail with elongated "streamers," and wide gape — is thoroughly specialised for aerial hunting. It catches flies, midges, and other airborne insects on the wing, often around livestock, and even drinks and bathes in flight. This specialisation makes it vulnerable: because its food is flying insects, cold or wet weather can cause severe food shortages that threaten adults and broods. Migration adds further risk, with high mortality from weather, exhaustion, and predation.

Socially, the barn swallow often breeds in loose colonies on human structures, and toward and after breeding it forms large communal roosts, especially during migration. It is socially monogamous but engages in extra-pair matings, so social pairing does not always match genetic parentage. It builds a distinctive mud-pellet nest attached to barns, bridges, and buildings, and shows strong fidelity to breeding sites and even specific nests across years — implying capable spatial memory and navigation, though the sensory basis of avian navigation remains an active research area. Both parents intensively provision the altricial young, whose survival hinges on insect abundance and weather; fledglings must then master aerial hunting.

The tail streamers, longer in males, became a celebrated case study in sexual selection, associated especially with Anders Pape Møller, who investigated whether tail length signals male quality and influences mate choice. The book presents this as an influential, much-studied hypothesis that has also been debated and re-examined, not a settled fact.

The book corrects myths: that swallows hibernate in pond mud (they migrate), that the tail is purely for flight, that swallows can feed on the ground when insects are scarce, that they nest only in natural sites, that they are strictly genetically monogamous, and that migration is safe for a strong flier. Compared with the unrelated common swift — an even more aerial insectivore — the barn swallow illustrates convergent evolution. Its ethology lessons concern specialisation, sexual selection and scientific caution, migration and navigation, commensalism with humans, and convergent evolution.
