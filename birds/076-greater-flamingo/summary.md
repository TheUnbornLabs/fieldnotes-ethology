# Summary — Book 076: The Greater Flamingo

The Greater Flamingo (*Phoenicopterus roseus*) is the largest flamingo species and one of the most specialised birds in the world. It belongs to its own order, Phoenicopteriformes, and occupies saline and alkaline lakes, lagoons, and salt pans across parts of Africa, southern Europe, the Middle East, and Asia. The IUCN lists it as Least Concern, but this global status conceals the vulnerability of individual colonies, since much of the species' reproduction is concentrated in a few fragile wetland sites.

The flamingo's biology is a tightly integrated package of behavior, morphology, and ecology. Its most famous feature, the pink plumage, is not fixed by genes but built from the diet: carotenoid pigments in the algae and small crustaceans it eats are deposited in feathers, skin, and even the "crop milk" fed to chicks. Deprived of such food, flamingos fade to white. Because colour depends on diet and on cosmetic preening, it functions as an honest signal of a bird's condition and influences mating success.

Feeding is equally distinctive. The bird feeds with its head down and bill inverted, pumping water and mud through comb-like lamellae with a piston-like tongue and straining out small food organisms. The Greater Flamingo's relatively coarse filter takes crustaceans, molluscs, insect larvae, and algae, which separates it ecologically from the smaller, finer-filtered Lesser Flamingo that specialises on microscopic cyanobacteria — a clear case of resource partitioning on shared lakes.

Flamingos are intensely social, living in flocks of hundreds to tens of thousands. Breeding is opportunistic, triggered by suitable water levels and food after rainfall, yet becomes tightly synchronised once underway, with mass courtship displays — head-flagging, wing-saluting, marching — sweeping the colony and bringing pairs into breeding condition together. Nests are cones of mud holding a single egg, incubated by both parents. Chicks hatch downy and mobile but depend on parental crop milk; they gather in large crèches where parents still locate and feed their own chick by its individual call. Independence comes after two to three months, and full pink colour after several years.

Learning matters more than the "instinct-only" caricature allows: young birds refine filter-feeding with practice, long-lived adults accumulate knowledge of scattered wetlands, and individual vocal recognition operates even in colonies of thousands. The flamingo's defences — vigilance, flight, safety in numbers, and predator-swamping synchrony — work against natural predators but leave it exposed to drought, water-regime change, and disturbance. The species teaches that behavior, form, and environment form one system, and that even mass societies can rest on individual recognition.
