# References / Further Reading

## Well-established sources

- Williams, T. D. (1995). *The Penguins: Spheniscidae*. Oxford University Press. (Standard reference on penguin biology and behavior.)
- Stonehouse, B. (1953). The Emperor Penguin *Aptenodytes forsteri* (Gray): I. Breeding behaviour and development. *Falkland Islands Dependencies Survey Scientific Report* No. 6. (Classic early field study of emperor breeding.)
- Ancel, A., Gilbert, C., Poulin, N., Beaulieu, M., & Thierry, B. (2015). New insights into the huddling dynamics of emperor penguins. *Animal Behaviour*, 110, 91–98. (Cooperative thermoregulation.)
- Jenouvrier, S., et al. (2014). Projected continent-wide declines of the emperor penguin under climate change. *Nature Climate Change*, 4, 715–718. (Climate vulnerability and population projections.)
- BirdLife International / IUCN Red List assessment for *Aptenodytes forsteri* (Endangered as of 2026, previously Near Threatened). (Conservation status.)

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

- IUCN Red List status: **Endangered** — uplisted from Near Threatened in the IUCN Red List update of April 2026, based on projections of a roughly 50% population decline by the 2080s driven by sea-ice loss. Sources: IUCN press release (iucn.org, "Emperor penguin and Antarctic fur seal now Endangered due to climate change," April 2026); British Antarctic Survey news release (bas.ac.uk); Mongabay (news.mongabay.com). Book, summary, and references updated accordingly.
- Male fast and incubation: males incubate the single egg alone for ~115 days (up to ~120), within a total breeding fast of about four months, losing roughly 40% of body mass. Consistent with published fasting-physiology literature (e.g., Groscolas and colleagues; CoolAntarctica factsheet). Book figures ("roughly four months," "up to 40%") verified.
- Dive depth/duration: record dives of ~535–564 m and durations up to ~27–32 min are documented (Kooyman and colleagues; "Extreme dives by free-ranging emperor penguins"). Book claim (">500 m; ~20–30 min") verified as within the documented range.
- Scientific name *Aptenodytes forsteri* and largest living penguin: verified.
