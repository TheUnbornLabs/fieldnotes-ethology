# Summary — The Barn Owl (*Tyto alba*)

The barn owl is one of the most important animals in the history of sensory biology, because it turned a night hunter's basic problem — catching a mouse it cannot see — into a solved neuroscience question. A barn owl can strike a moving rodent in complete darkness using hearing alone, displaying arguably the most acute directional hearing measured in any animal. Its pale, heart-shaped face is a precision instrument: a ruff of stiff feathers that gathers and funnels sound like a pair of parabolic dishes toward ear openings set at different heights on the two sides of the skull. That vertical asymmetry lets the owl locate a sound not only left-to-right (azimuth, computed mainly from the difference in arrival time at the two ears) but up-and-down (elevation, computed from the difference in loudness). Classic neuroethology associated with Masakazu Konishi and Eric Knudsen traced how the owl's midbrain builds an auditory space map, an ordered neural chart of directions in space, and showed that this map is calibrated by experience during development.

The barn owl hunts on the wing in a slow, low, quartering search flight over open country, sometimes hovering before dropping talons-first onto prey. Its flight is nearly silent, thanks to comb-like serrations on the leading edge of its wing feathers and a soft fringe on the trailing edge, so it neither alerts prey nor masks the faint sounds it is listening for. Excellent low-light vision complements the hearing, but in true darkness the eyes contribute nothing and hearing takes over. The diet is dominated overwhelmingly by small mammals, especially voles; indigestible fur and bone are ejected as pellets, whose contents make the barn owl an unusually easy predator to study.

Barn owls are largely solitary, nocturnal, and nearly global in distribution. They nest in cavities and human structures such as barns, towers, and nest boxes, laying large clutches that track rodent population booms. Incubation begins with the first egg, so hatching is asynchronous and the brood is graded in age and size; when food runs short, the youngest starve in a process of brood reduction that concentrates food into chicks that can be raised. When parents are away, nestlings engage in a vocal "negotiation" — research associated with Alexandre Roulin — in which hungrier chicks call more and better-fed siblings defer, settling who receives the next prey item.

The barn owl does not hoot; it screeches. It cannot rotate its head a full circle, but only about 270 degrees. It is not unusually "wise," and it does not see in absolute darkness. Its genuine distinction is a superbly integrated package of asymmetric ears, a parabolic facial ruff, a neural map of auditory space, and silent wings. Its main modern threats are secondary poisoning by rodenticides, road mortality, and the loss of rough grassland and nest sites, and it is assessed globally as Least Concern despite many regional declines.
