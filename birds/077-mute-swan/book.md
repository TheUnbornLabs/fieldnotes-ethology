# Book 077 — The Mute Swan: Pair Bonds, Territory, and the Politics of the Water

## 1. Opening Summary

The Mute Swan (*Cygnus olor*) occupies a peculiar place in the human imagination: it is at once a symbol of grace and devotion and, to anyone who has met a nesting pair at close range, a formidably aggressive bird. For ethologists, this tension is exactly what makes the species instructive. The Mute Swan is a large, long-lived, territorial waterbird whose behavior is organised around two linked commitments — a strong pair bond and the vigorous defence of a breeding territory. Studying it clarifies how monogamy actually works in birds (with more nuance than the romantic slogans allow), how territorial aggression is calibrated to threat, and how a conspicuous, semi-tame bird becomes entangled with people, culture, and conservation politics. The Mute Swan also carries a heavy freight of myth — that swans mate for life without exception, that a swan can break a person's arm with its wing, that it sings only as it dies. Sorting the well-supported behavior from the folklore is a useful exercise in the disciplined, cautious thinking that ethology demands. This book examines how the Mute Swan perceives its world, how it feeds and breeds, how it communicates despite its name, and what its famous devotion and famous temper really amount to.

## 2. Scientific Identity

The Mute Swan, *Cygnus olor*, is one of the largest and heaviest flying birds, a member of the waterfowl family Anatidae within the order Anseriformes, which also contains ducks and geese. Adults are among the most massive of waterfowl, commonly weighing roughly 9 to 12 kilograms or more, with males (cobs) larger than females (pens), and a wingspan on the order of 2 to 2.4 metres. The species is distinguished by its orange-red bill with a prominent black basal knob, larger in males, and by the graceful S-curve of the neck, which differs from the straighter necks of some other swans. It is native across temperate Europe and much of temperate Asia, and has been widely introduced elsewhere, notably in North America, southern Africa, Australia, and New Zealand. Mute Swans inhabit slow rivers, lakes, ponds, marshes, coastal lagoons, and other calm freshwater and brackish waters, often near people. They are chiefly herbivorous, feeding on aquatic vegetation supplemented by some invertebrates. Lifespan in the wild is often around a decade but can reach the twenties. On the IUCN Red List the Mute Swan is assessed as Least Concern, with a large and in places increasing range, though its introduced populations raise separate management concerns.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

The Mute Swan's behavior reflects the demands of being a very large, primarily herbivorous waterbird that raises slow-developing young. Great size brings advantages — few predators can threaten a healthy adult, and the bird can defend a rich patch of water — but it also imposes costs: swans need abundant aquatic vegetation, take years to reach breeding age, and produce comparatively few young that require prolonged care. In this context, several linked traits make evolutionary sense. Long-term pair bonding reduces the time and energy spent on courtship each season and allows an experienced pair to cooperate in the demanding tasks of building a large nest, defending a territory, and guarding a brood over months. Strong territoriality secures the food and safe water that a family of large birds needs. Pronounced sexual size difference and the male's larger bill knob are consistent with a mating system in which males compete over territory and mates and in which body size aids in defence. The swan's herbivory ties it to productive shallow waters and helps explain both its choice of habitat and the seasonal, resource-linked timing of its breeding.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

The rhythm of a Mute Swan's life divides sharply between the breeding season and the rest of the year. During breeding, a pair holds and patrols a territory, and much of the day is spent feeding, maintaining the nest, incubating, and repelling intruders. Outside the breeding season the picture changes: non-breeding and immature swans, and adults that have finished raising young, often gather in flocks, sometimes large ones, on lakes, estuaries, and sheltered coasts. This alternation between fierce territoriality in summer and tolerant flocking at other times is a striking feature of the species. Swans are active mainly by day, feeding in the shallows, though they may also feed on moonlit nights. They spend considerable time on maintenance behavior — preening keeps the plumage waterproof and insulating, which is vital for a bird that lives on cold water. Flight is powerful but laborious; the heavy birds need a long running take-off across the water, and their wingbeats produce a distinctive throbbing or singing sound that carries far. Moult brings a flightless period in late summer, during which swans are especially cautious and stay on open water.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

The Mute Swan perceives its world chiefly through vision, supported by hearing and touch. Vision is the dominant sense for a large diurnal waterbird: swans watch continuously for rivals, predators, and mates, and their territorial and social behavior is driven largely by what they see. The eyes give a wide field of view suited to detecting the approach of an intruder across open water, and much aggressive signalling is visual, relying on posture and the conspicuous white plumage. Touch is important in feeding: the bill is sensitive and is used to grope for and grasp submerged vegetation, sometimes in water too murky or deep for the food to be seen. Swans up-end, plunging head and neck below the surface and using the long neck to reach plants on the bottom that shorter-necked waterfowl cannot. Hearing supports communication within the pair and family and the detection of danger. As with waterfowl generally, the Mute Swan's cognition is best described as that of a capable, behaviorally flexible bird rather than an exceptional problem-solver; its perceptual world is organised around vigilance, feeding by touch and sight in shallow water, and the reading of social signals from mates and rivals.

## 6. Feeding Behavior

Mute Swans are predominantly herbivores of shallow water, and their feeding behavior is shaped by their size and their long necks. The core method is grazing on submerged aquatic plants, which the bird reaches by dipping its head under the surface or by up-ending — tipping forward so the body is vertical, tail in the air, and the long neck extended to the bottom. This gives the swan access to vegetation up to about a metre down, a depth beyond the reach of dabbling ducks, so that swans exploit a stratum of the water column that many other waterfowl cannot. They also graze on land, walking out to feed on grasses and crops near the water, and take some small aquatic animals, snails, and insects incidentally with the plant matter. Because a swan is large and eats a great deal, feeding occupies much of the day, and a pair's territory is in part a defended larder of aquatic plants. In places where swans are numerous or where people feed them bread — a poor and even harmful food for them — their grazing and their congregations can have local effects on aquatic vegetation and on relations with other water users, an issue that recurs in the management of introduced populations.

## 7. Social Behavior

The social life of the Mute Swan swings between two poles, and understanding the species means holding both in view. In the breeding season the fundamental social unit is the territorial pair, and the dominant social behavior is exclusion: the pair drives other swans, and often other waterbirds, out of its stretch of water. A breeding cob can be strikingly aggressive, adopting the "busking" display — wings raised in twin arches over the back, neck curved back, and surging across the water toward an intruder — and will attack rivals that do not retreat. Outside breeding, the same species is markedly gregarious. Non-breeders, immatures, and off-duty adults form flocks that can number in the hundreds, feeding and roosting together with little of the summer's hostility. Within these flocks there is some structure and some squabbling, but nothing like the intense territoriality of the breeding pair. Young swans remain with their parents through their first months and are then driven off before the next breeding attempt, after which they join the non-breeding flocks until they mature and secure a territory of their own. This alternation — solitary-pair aggression in summer, sociable flocking in winter — is a clean example of behavior being switched by ecological context rather than fixed.

## 8. Communication

Despite the name, the Mute Swan is not silent, and its communication combines sound, striking visual display, and physical action. The "mute" label is comparative: the species lacks the loud bugling calls of some other swans, but it produces a repertoire of hisses, snorts, grunts, and soft trumpeting or hoarse notes, used within the pair and family and, most memorably, as a threat. The loud hiss delivered by a defending swan, bill open and neck outstretched, is an unambiguous warning. The most distinctive vocal-mechanical signal is not from the voice at all but from the wings: in flight the wingbeats produce a rhythmic, singing or throbbing sound, audible over long distances, which helps keep flying birds in contact. Visual signalling carries much of the communicative load, particularly in aggression: the busking posture with arched wings, the head-back threat, and the ritualised approaches of a territorial cob convey intent before any contact. Within a pair, mutual displays reinforce the bond — synchronised head movements and the well-known facing posture in which two swans bring their heads together and their curved necks form a heart-shaped outline, an image that has done much to build the species' romantic reputation.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

The Mute Swan is socially monogamous and forms strong, often long-lasting pair bonds, and this is the feature most heavily wrapped in myth. Pairs frequently do remain together across many breeding seasons, and an established, experienced pair breeds more successfully, which is a real advantage of durable bonds. But "mates for life, always" overstates it: partners are sometimes replaced, "divorce" occurs — particularly after failed breeding — and a bird whose mate dies will typically pair again. Courtship involves mutual displays, synchronised movements, and the heart-shaped facing posture, and once bonded the pair cooperates closely. They build a large mound-shaped nest of vegetation, usually near or on the water, into which the pen lays a clutch commonly of around five to eight eggs. Incubation, chiefly by the pen with the cob guarding and sometimes relieving her, lasts roughly five to six weeks. Territoriality peaks during this period; the pair defends the nest and surrounding water vigorously. Mute Swans do not usually breed until they are several years old, reflecting the time needed to reach full size and secure a territory — a slow reproductive tempo consistent with a large, long-lived bird.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

Mute Swan young, called cygnets, hatch downy and mobile, with open eyes, and can leave the nest, swim, and feed themselves within a day or two — a precocial pattern typical of waterfowl. This does not mean they are independent: both parents guard and lead the brood for months, and parental care is prolonged and attentive. The cygnets, greyish-brown rather than white, follow their parents across the territory, learning where and how to feed, and are protected by the pair's continued aggression toward intruders. A well-known feature of Mute Swan family life is that small cygnets sometimes ride on a parent's back, sheltering among the feathers, which offers warmth and protection from cold water and underwater predators. The family stays together through the summer and often into autumn or winter. As the next breeding season approaches, the parents drive the now well-grown young away, and the juveniles join non-breeding flocks. Cygnet mortality can be high — from predators, chilling, collisions, entanglement, and other hazards — so the extended, cooperative guarding by both parents is central to getting even a few young to independence. Full white adult plumage and breeding maturity come only after a few years.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

The Mute Swan should not be dismissed as a creature of pure instinct; like other waterfowl it combines strong inherited behavioral tendencies with genuine learning. Cygnets learn feeding sites and techniques by following their experienced parents, acquiring knowledge of where good vegetation lies and how to reach it. Adults learn and remember the geography of their territory and the wider area, the locations of feeding and roosting sites, and the identities and dispositions of neighbours — a territorial system works partly because established residents recognise one another and settle disputes with less costly display over time. Mute Swans living among people become notably habituated, learning to tolerate close human presence and, at feeding spots, to associate people with food. Claims about swan intelligence should nonetheless stay proportionate: there is no good evidence of tool use, insight, or the elaborate cognition documented in corvids and parrots. What the evidence supports is a bird with reliable, adaptable behavior, a good spatial memory, individual recognition of mates and rivals, and a capacity to adjust to local conditions — including the human-dominated environments many Mute Swans now occupy.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

An adult Mute Swan has few natural enemies, and its defensive strategy reflects that confidence: rather than fleeing, a threatened breeding swan often confronts the threat. The busking display — wings arched, neck arched back, surging forward — the loud hiss, and, if pressed, physical attack with buffeting wings and biting bill are aimed at driving off rivals, predators, dogs, and sometimes people who come too near a nest or brood. This is where one of the species' most persistent myths arises, and it must be stated carefully: a large swan striking with its wing can deliver a genuinely painful blow and could conceivably injure a person, but the popular claim that a swan can readily "break a man's arm" is not supported and is best treated as exaggeration. Cygnets and eggs are the true vulnerability, exposed to foxes, large gulls, pike, snapping turtles (in some introduced ranges), and other predators, which is why the parents' aggression concentrates on the breeding season. The main sources of stress for Mute Swans in the modern landscape are human-linked: collisions with power lines, lead poisoning from ingested fishing weights and shot (a documented and serious problem in some regions), entanglement in tackle and litter, disturbance, and, for introduced populations, control programmes. The swan's defence is formidable against natural threats but offers little protection against these human hazards.

## 13. Human Interaction

Few wild birds are as culturally loaded, or as physically close to people, as the Mute Swan. In much of Europe it has been semi-domesticated for centuries, kept on ornamental waters and, famously in England, subject to elaborate ownership customs — the "swan-upping" tradition and historic royal claims over swans reflect a long history of the bird as property and status symbol. It features heavily in art, myth, and literature as an emblem of beauty, love, and transformation. This closeness cuts both ways. Familiarity has made the Mute Swan tolerant of people and a fixture of urban parks, but it has also exposed it to human hazards — lead poisoning, entanglement, collisions, and the harm done by feeding bread. The most contentious dimension is its status as an introduced species outside its native range. In parts of North America and elsewhere, Mute Swans established from escaped or released birds are treated as invasive: their aggression toward native waterfowl, their heavy grazing of aquatic vegetation, and their rapid increase have prompted management and culling programmes that are ethically and politically fraught. The Mute Swan thus sits at the intersection of affection and conflict — beloved where native and long-established, controversial where introduced.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

```text
Myth: Mute Swans always mate for life and never take a new partner.
Scientific reality: Mute Swans are socially monogamous and form strong, often long-lasting pair bonds, and lifelong fidelity is common. But it is not absolute. "Divorce" occurs, particularly after failed breeding attempts, partners are occasionally replaced, and a swan whose mate dies will usually pair again. The accurate statement is that swans form durable bonds that frequently but not invariably last for life.
```

```text
Myth: A Mute Swan can break a person's arm with a blow of its wing.
Scientific reality: A large swan defending its nest can strike hard with its wings and inflict a painful blow, and its aggression should be respected. But the claim that a swan can readily break an adult's arm is exaggerated and not supported by evidence. The wing blow is a real deterrent; the broken-bone story is folklore.
```

```text
Myth: The Mute Swan is silent.
Scientific reality: The name is comparative, not literal. The Mute Swan lacks the loud bugling of some other swans but is not voiceless: it hisses, snorts, grunts, and gives soft trumpeting notes, and its wingbeats produce a distinctive throbbing, singing sound in flight. It communicates by sound as well as by display.
```

```text
Myth: A swan sings a beautiful song just before it dies — the "swan song."
Scientific reality: The swan song is a myth of ancient origin with no basis in the bird's biology. Mute Swans do not produce a special death-song. The phrase survives as a metaphor, not as a description of anything the animal does.
```

```text
Myth: Feeding bread to swans is a kindness.
Scientific reality: Bread is a poor food for swans, offering little proper nutrition and potentially contributing to health problems and to over-crowding and fouling where birds gather to be fed. Swans are herbivores adapted to aquatic vegetation. Feeding bread is at best unhelpful and can be harmful.
```

```text
Myth: The white cygnet or "ugly duckling" grows into a swan by transformation.
Scientific reality: The fairy tale aside, Mute Swan cygnets are simply young swans. They hatch greyish-brown and moult gradually to white adult plumage over their first year or more; there is no transformation between species. The grey juvenile is a normal developmental stage, not a different kind of bird.
```

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

A revealing comparison is between the Mute Swan and the Whooper Swan (*Cygnus cygnus*), a close relative that shares the swan body plan but differs in ways that illuminate the Mute Swan's biology. The most obvious contrast is vocal: where the Mute Swan is comparatively quiet and communicates heavily through visual display and wing sound, the Whooper Swan is loudly vocal, with far-carrying bugling calls that play a central role in contact and in pair and family cohesion, aided by an elongated windpipe. The two also differ in bill colour — the Mute's orange bill with a black knob versus the Whooper's yellow-and-black bill lacking a knob — and in neck carriage, the Mute holding its neck in a graceful curve and the Whooper more upright. Ecologically, the Whooper Swan is a long-distance migrant of the far north, breeding in subarctic wetlands and migrating to milder wintering grounds, whereas the Mute Swan is more sedentary across much of its range. The comparison highlights how two large, herbivorous, pair-bonding swans can diverge in the balance of their communication — one leaning on sound, the other on display — and in their migratory strategy, while sharing the same fundamental family life.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

The Mute Swan teaches several lessons that reward careful, unsentimental attention. First, it is a case study in the reality of avian monogamy: pair bonds that are genuinely strong and often lifelong, yet not the flawless, exceptionless devotion of legend — a reminder to describe behavior as it is, with its variation and its "divorces," rather than as we wish it to be. Second, the species shows how the same animal can switch between opposite social modes — fierce territorial exclusion in the breeding season, tolerant flocking outside it — demonstrating that social behavior is often set by ecological context rather than fixed in the individual. Third, the swan's aggression illustrates the function of ritualised display: the busking posture and hiss usually settle disputes before any fight, so that conspicuous threat is a means of avoiding costly combat. Fourth, the Mute Swan is an object lesson in how myth accretes around a charismatic animal — the swan song, the arm-breaking wing, the perfect lifelong marriage — and in why the ethologist's task is to separate the well-supported from the folkloric. Finally, its role as a beloved native in one place and a controversial introduction in another shows how behavior interacts with human values to create conservation dilemmas. These are lessons about evidence and context, not moral fables about fidelity.

## 17. Glossary

**Anatidae** — the waterfowl family, containing ducks, geese, and swans, to which the Mute Swan belongs.
**Busking** — the Mute Swan's threat display, wings raised in arches over the back and neck curved back, used in territorial aggression.
**Cob** — an adult male swan.
**Pen** — an adult female swan.
**Cygnet** — a young swan.
**Bill knob** — the fleshy black knob at the base of the Mute Swan's bill, larger in males.
**Up-ending** — feeding by tipping forward so the body is vertical and the neck reaches submerged plants.
**Territoriality** — the defence of an area, here the breeding pair's stretch of water, against rivals.
**Social monogamy** — a mating system in which a male and female pair and cooperate to raise young, without implying lifelong or exclusive fidelity.
**Divorce (in birds)** — the breaking of a pair bond between two living birds that then pair with others.
**Precocial** — describing young that hatch developed, with open eyes and the ability to move and feed soon after hatching.
**Moult** — the periodic replacement of feathers, during which swans undergo a flightless period.
**Sexual size dimorphism** — a consistent difference in size between the sexes; in Mute Swans, males are larger.
**Habituation** — a decline in response to a repeated, harmless stimulus, seen in swans that tolerate close human presence.
**Introduced (invasive) species** — a species living outside its native range through human action, sometimes causing ecological harm.
