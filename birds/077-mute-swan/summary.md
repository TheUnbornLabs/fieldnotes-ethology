# Summary — Book 077: The Mute Swan

The Mute Swan (*Cygnus olor*) is one of the largest and heaviest flying birds, a member of the waterfowl family Anatidae. Native across temperate Europe and Asia and widely introduced elsewhere, it inhabits calm fresh and brackish waters, often close to people. It is chiefly herbivorous, reaching submerged plants by up-ending on its long neck. The IUCN lists it as Least Concern, though its introduced populations raise separate management concerns.

The species' behavior is organised around two linked commitments: a strong pair bond and vigorous territorial defence. Mute Swans are socially monogamous and often stay together for many seasons, but the popular "mate for life, always" slogan overstates the case — "divorce" occurs, especially after failed breeding, and a widowed bird usually re-pairs. Pairs build a large mound nest, the pen lays roughly five to eight eggs, and incubation runs about five to six weeks, with the cob guarding fiercely.

Territoriality is the summer keynote. A breeding cob will drive off rivals with the "busking" display — wings arched over the back, neck curved, surging across the water — a hiss, and, if needed, a physical blow. This gives rise to the myth that a swan can break a person's arm: a wing blow can hurt, but the broken-bone claim is exaggeration. Outside breeding, the same species becomes sociable, gathering in large, largely peaceful flocks — a clean example of social behavior switched by ecological context.

Despite its name, the Mute Swan is not silent: it hisses, snorts, and grunts, and its wingbeats produce a distinctive singing throb in flight. It communicates heavily through visual display, from aggressive postures to the heart-shaped facing posture between mates that underpins its romantic reputation. Cygnets hatch precocial — downy, mobile, able to swim within a day or two — yet still need months of guarding by both parents, and small cygnets may ride on a parent's back.

Learning matters: cygnets learn feeding sites from parents, adults remember territory geography and neighbours, and urban swans habituate to people. But claims of exceptional intelligence are unwarranted. The main modern threats are human-linked — lead poisoning, entanglement, collisions, and the harm of bread-feeding. Culturally cherished where native and long-established, the Mute Swan is controversial where introduced and treated as invasive. It teaches lessons about the real nuance of avian monogamy, context-dependent sociality, the function of ritualised threat, and the value of separating well-supported behavior from folklore.
