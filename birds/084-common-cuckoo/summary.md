# Summary — The Common Cuckoo (*Cuculus canorus*)

The common cuckoo, *Cuculus canorus*, is a medium-sized migratory bird of the family Cuculidae, breeding across Europe and temperate Asia and wintering in sub-Saharan Africa. Adults are insectivores with a notable specialisation on hairy, toxic caterpillars that most birds avoid. The species is assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, though it has declined in parts of its range.

The cuckoo is one of ethology's most important species because it is an obligate brood parasite: it never builds a nest or raises its own young. The female lays her eggs in the nests of other, smaller birds, which then incubate the cuckoo egg and rear the cuckoo chick, usually at the cost of their own brood. This strategy frees the female from the costs of parental care and lets her spread many eggs across many host nests, but it makes her success depend entirely on deceiving the hosts.

That dependence sets up a coevolutionary arms race, the reason the cuckoo is a model system in biology. Female cuckoos form host-specific lineages (gentes) whose eggs mimic the eggs of their chosen host, and they lay with extraordinary speed, often removing a host egg. Hosts fight back by mobbing adult cuckoos and by evolving the ability to recognise and reject foreign eggs, which in turn selects for ever-closer mimicry. Parasite and host escalate against each other over evolutionary time.

The cuckoo chick's behavior must be described mechanistically, not morally. Soon after hatching it ejects the host's eggs and nestlings from the nest, using a hollow in its back, so that it monopolises all the food. This is an evolved, largely innate survival behavior, not cruelty, performed correctly by a chick that has never seen another cuckoo. The chick then exploits its foster parents with a supernormal begging call that mimics the sound of a whole brood, drawing enough food to raise a nestling that soon dwarfs them.

Cuckoos have almost no social life: no pair bond, no family, no parental care. The male gives the famous two-note "cuck-oo" call to advertise and attract mates; the female has a distinct bubbling call. Compared with a typical host such as the reed warbler — a devoted biparental carer — the cuckoo shows both extreme divergence in life history and how coevolution binds the fates of parasite and host. Its lessons are about coevolution, mechanistic explanation, innate behavior, and ecological interdependence.
