# Summary — The African Sacred Ibis (*Threskiornis aethiopicus*)

The African sacred ibis (*Threskiornis aethiopicus*) is a medium-sized white wading bird of the family Threskiornithidae (the ibises and spoonbills), recognisable by its bare black head and neck and its long, down-curved black bill. It lives in wetlands and their margins across sub-Saharan Africa, feeding on a broad, opportunistic diet — insects, worms, crustaceans, molluscs, amphibians, fish, reptiles, the eggs and chicks of other birds, and carrion and refuse. In its native range it is assessed by the IUCN as Least Concern. It is famous above all for its place in ancient Egyptian religion, where it was sacred to the god Thoth and mummified as a votive offering in enormous numbers.

The ibis's defining behavior is tactile foraging: rather than hunting by sight like a heron, it probes its long, sensitive bill into mud, shallow water, and soft ground, detecting and seizing hidden prey by touch. This method, shared across its family, opens up a rich supply of concealed food. Combined with a highly flexible, omnivorous diet and a readiness to exploit human-created habitats such as rubbish tips and farmland, tactile foraging makes the sacred ibis an adaptable generalist.

The species is gregarious, feeding and roosting in flocks and nesting colonially, often alongside herons, egrets, and other waterbirds, in trees, reedbeds, or on islands. Pairs share incubation and the care of their young, and breeding is tied to favourable wetland conditions. The ibis is comparatively quiet, communicating through a limited range of croaking calls, visual displays, and the powerful signal of collective behavior at roosts and colonies.

The sacred ibis embodies a striking set of paradoxes. Revered and mummified in ancient Egypt, it is now essentially absent there as a wild bird. Secure across its native African range, it has become an invasive species in parts of western Europe, where birds escaped from collections established breeding populations that raise concern for native wildlife — particularly through predation on other birds' eggs and chicks — and are subject to control. The same flexibility that makes the ibis a resilient native bird makes it a problem where it is introduced. Its cognitive hallmark is this opportunistic adaptability rather than any spectacular problem-solving, and it is neither a tool-using genius nor an instinct-only automaton. The sacred ibis teaches that cultural symbolism must be kept apart from biology, that tactile foraging is a powerful alternative to sight-based hunting, and that a species' impact depends on the context of the ecosystem it inhabits.
