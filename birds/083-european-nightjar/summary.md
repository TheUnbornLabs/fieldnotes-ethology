# Summary — The European Nightjar (*Caprimulgus europaeus*)

The European nightjar, *Caprimulgus europaeus*, is a nocturnal and crepuscular aerial insectivore of the family Caprimulgidae, breeding across Europe and into Asia on heathland, forest clearings, and young plantations, and wintering in sub-Saharan Africa. It feeds on flying insects, especially moths and beetles, and is assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, though it has declined locally with habitat loss.

The nightjar is superbly adapted to the night. Large eyes gather scarce light for visually guided hunting; the bill is tiny but opens into a very wide, bristle-fringed gape that acts as an aerial net for insects; and the long wings give buoyant, manoeuvrable, nearly silent flight. By day the bird relies on extraordinary camouflage, resting motionless on a branch or the ground where its bark-and-leaf-litter plumage renders it almost invisible. Concealment, not active defence, is its master survival strategy, extended to unbuilt ground nests where eggs, incubating adults, and chicks all depend on crypsis.

The bird's most famous feature is folklore. Its ancient name "goatsucker" records a false belief that it drank milk from goats; in reality it gathers around grazing animals to catch the insects they attract, and its wide mouth is for aerial hunting alone. Nightjars do not echolocate — they hunt by sight — and their eerie, insect-like song is genuinely a bird's voice: the male's sustained, mechanical churring, delivered at dusk and through the night to advertise territory and attract a mate. Communication also includes wing-clapping display flights, flight calls, and white wing and tail spots flashed in the dark.

Nightjars are seasonally monogamous and largely solitary, spaced across suitable habitat. Breeding is notably timed to the lunar cycle, so that the peak demand of feeding well-grown chicks tends to coincide with brighter moonlit nights, when hunting is more productive. Both parents incubate the small clutch, laid directly on the ground, and feed the semi-precocial young, sometimes raising two broods. When threatened at the nest, adults may perform distraction displays.

Compared with the common swift — a diurnal, high-speed, colonial aerial insectivore — the nightjar shows how the shared problem of catching insects in flight can be solved in opposite ways, by darkness and camouflage rather than speed and daylight. The nightjar's lasting lessons are about camouflage, the correction of myth by evidence, and the tuning of behavior to environmental cues such as moonlight.
