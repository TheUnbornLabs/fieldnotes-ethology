# Summary — The Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*)

The bald eagle is a large North American sea-eagle famous as a national symbol but far more interesting as an animal than as an emblem. Behind its proud image is an efficient opportunist that feeds by whichever route costs the least effort. It catches fish, but it also scavenges carrion heavily and pirates catches from ospreys, gulls, and other birds — the food-stealing habit, called kleptoparasitism, that led Benjamin Franklin to remark on the bird's "bad moral character." In ecological terms this is not vice but optimal foraging: stealing a fish an osprey has already caught can return more calories per unit of effort than catching one directly.

Its behavior is shaped by an aquatic niche where food arrives in unpredictable pulses. Powerful feet with rough toe projections called spicules grip slippery fish; keen, high-resolution vision detects prey and, just as importantly, tracks the movements of competitors. The species is diurnal and seasonally flexible in its social life: strongly territorial and paired during breeding, then gregarious in winter, when many eagles gather in communal roosts and in large congregations at salmon runs and open water where food is abundant.

Bald eagles form strong, often long-lasting pair bonds and perform a spectacular courtship, including the cartwheel display in which two birds lock talons in the air and tumble downward together. They build some of the largest tree nests of any bird, returning to and enlarging the same nest year after year — a pattern of nest fidelity. Both parents incubate and feed the young, which fledge after about ten to twelve weeks but depend on their parents for weeks more. The famous white head and tail take about five years to develop; juveniles are mottled dark brown and are often mistaken for other raptors, including the golden eagle.

Despite its fierce look, the bald eagle's real voice is a weak, high, chirpy whistle — nothing like the powerful scream heard in films, which is almost always dubbed from a red-tailed hawk. This and other misconceptions (that the bird is featherless-"bald," that it eats only self-caught fish, that it is still endangered) are corrected in the book.

The bald eagle is also a landmark conservation story. In the twentieth century the pesticide DDT concentrated up the food chain through biomagnification and thinned the eggshells of fish-eating birds, and eagle numbers collapsed in the contiguous United States. After the 1972 DDT ban and strong legal protection, the population recovered and the species was delisted in 2007. It is now assessed as Least Concern. For ethology, the bald eagle is a compact lesson in foraging economics, behavioral flexibility, signal function, and the recovery of a top predator.
