# Book 093 — The Domestic Canary: Summary

The domestic canary is the domesticated form of the wild canary (*Serinus canaria*), a small finch of the family Fringillidae native to the Atlantic islands of Macaronesia — the Canary Islands, Azores, and Madeira. The wild bird is a modest greenish-yellow and grey finch; centuries of selective breeding have produced the familiar bright-yellow pet canary along with many colour, type, and song breeds. The wild species is common and assessed as Least Concern, while the domestic canary exists in huge numbers worldwide as a companion and exhibition bird.

The canary's outsized scientific importance rests on its song. The male's long, complex song functions naturally in mate attraction and territorial defence, and it is learned. Crucially, the canary's song is not fixed for life: it can be modified across seasons and years. This adult song plasticity is linked to seasonal changes in the song-control brain regions, and research on the canary — associated especially with Fernando Nottebohm and colleagues — provided influential evidence that the adult songbird brain adds new neurons (adult neurogenesis). This helped overturn the older assumption that adult vertebrate brains do not generate new neurons, a finding whose importance reached far beyond ornithology. The book attributes these claims to that research programme and frames them carefully.

Ecologically, the canary is a diurnal, seasonal, seed-eating finch. Wild canaries flock outside the breeding season and shift to pairs and territories during breeding, when males sing to attract mates and the female leads nest-building. Both parents provision the altricial young, which are fed a protein-richer diet than the adult seed diet. The species is socially monogamous within a breeding attempt and can raise multiple broods.

Its history with humans is unusually complete. Brought into captivity centuries ago, the canary became a beloved cage bird, was reshaped by artificial selection into many breeds (including song breeds selected for vocal style), served grimly as the "canary in the coal mine" (a role owed to small size and high metabolism, not special behavior), and became a keystone of neuroscience.

The book corrects several myths: that pet-canary yellow is the wild colour, that adult brains cannot add neurons, that all birdsong is fixed for life, that females sing elaborately, that mine canaries were trained or brave, and that the canary is broadly "super-intelligent." Compared with the zebra finch — the model of once-for-life developmental song learning — the canary is the model of renewable adult song plasticity, and together they show that song learning is not one uniform process. Its ethology lessons concern adult brain plasticity, artificial selection on behavior, the diversity of learning, and scientific precision.
