# Summary — The Eurasian Eagle-Owl (*Bubo bubo*)

The Eurasian Eagle-Owl is one of the largest owls in the world and, across much of Eurasia, the apex predator of the night. A big bird — up to about 70 centimetres tall with a wingspan approaching 1.8 metres — it hunts in darkness with feet and talons powerful enough to crush and carry prey as large as a hare, and it does something unusual among birds: it regularly kills and eats other predators, including hawks, buzzards, and smaller owls. This intraguild predation places it firmly at the top of the nocturnal food web. It ranges widely from Europe through the Middle East and Central Asia into East Asia, favouring rocky, broken country — cliffs, gorges, and boulder slopes — but also forests, steppe, farmland, and, increasingly, man-made cliffs such as quarries and even some urban and industrial sites. The IUCN Red List assesses it as Least Concern.

The eagle-owl hunts by perch-and-ambush: it waits on an elevated vantage, then launches a short, silent glide to strike, relying on a strong combination of acute low-light vision and good directional hearing. Its enormous, light-gathering eyes are nearly fixed in their sockets, which is why the bird rotates its head through such a wide arc to scan. The concave facial disc funnels faint sounds toward ear openings hidden at the sides of the head. The prominent ear tufts, despite their name, are not ears at all — they are feathers used for display and camouflage, a point that corrects a persistent myth.

Its diet is broad, dominated in many regions by rabbits, hares, rats, and voles but extending to birds, reptiles, amphibians, and other raptors. This dietary flexibility is a major reason the species thrives across so many habitats. Eagle-owls are largely solitary and territorial, advertising ownership mainly by sound: the deep, far-carrying "ooh-hu" hoot is above all a territorial and mate-communication signal, not a scare tactic. Pairs form long-term, often lifelong bonds and hold large territories. They build no real nest, laying on a bare cliff or rock ledge; incubation begins with the first egg, so chicks hatch at staggered ages, an adaptation to unpredictable food. The young leave the nest before they can fly, are fed for months while learning to hunt, and defend the nest — as do their parents — with a formidable threat display and striking talons.

The eagle-owl's history with people has turned from persecution to recovery. Once shot, trapped, and poisoned to local extinction in parts of Europe, it has rebounded under legal protection and reintroduction, recolonising former range and adopting quarries and man-made structures. It is a study in what it means to be a large, slow-breeding apex predator, and a corrective to the folklore of owls as gentle, wise, harmless creatures.
