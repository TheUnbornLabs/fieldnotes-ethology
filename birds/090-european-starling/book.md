# Book 090 — The European Starling: Murmurations, Mimicry, and the Spread of a Survivor

## 1. Opening Summary

The European starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*) is one of the most successful and most controversial birds on Earth. To some it is the author of the breathtaking murmuration — the shape-shifting, wheeling cloud of thousands of birds that darkens autumn skies — and a gifted vocal mimic; to others it is an aggressive invader that displaced native birds after being introduced to North America in the late nineteenth century. For ethologists the starling is exceptionally instructive on several fronts. Its murmurations are a textbook example of self-organised collective behaviour, in which coordinated, seemingly choreographed motion emerges from simple rules followed by each individual responding to its near neighbours, with no leader directing the flock. Its vocal mimicry places it among the accomplished imitators of the bird world. Its explosive spread across North America from a small nineteenth-century introduction is a classic case study of invasion biology and of a commensal species that thrives alongside humans. And its abundance, adaptability, and cavity-nesting habits make it a model for studying competition, sociality, and behaviour in a bird that is at once familiar and problematic. This book examines how the starling lives, feeds, communicates, and breeds; how murmurations really work; and how a bird can be both a wonder of collective behaviour and one of the world's most notorious invaders.

## 2. Scientific Identity

The European starling, *Sturnus vulgaris*, sometimes called the common starling, belongs to the starling family, Sturnidae, in the order Passeriformes (the perching birds). It is a medium-sized, stocky, short-tailed songbird, roughly 20 to 23 centimetres long, with a pointed bill and strong legs. In breeding plumage adults are glossy black with iridescent green and purple sheens, and the bill turns yellow; in fresh non-breeding plumage the birds are heavily spangled with pale spots, and the bill darkens. The species is native to much of Europe and western Asia and parts of North Africa, and it has been introduced, deliberately and accidentally, to North America, southern Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and elsewhere, becoming one of the most widespread birds in the world. It is a habitat generalist, thriving in farmland, grassland, urban and suburban areas, and other open and modified habitats, and it is strongly commensal with humans. The diet is omnivorous, including invertebrates and plant material such as fruit and seeds. Starlings are relatively short-lived small passerines, though some individuals live several years. On the IUCN Red List, *Sturnus vulgaris* is assessed as Least Concern globally, reflecting its enormous total range and population — even though, notably, it has declined in parts of its native range in recent decades, a decline that coexists with its superabundance as an introduced bird elsewhere.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

The European starling's success is best understood as the triumph of a highly flexible, social, cavity-nesting omnivore. Several features of its biology, shaped over evolutionary time, combine to make it an outstanding generalist and, in the right circumstances, an invader. Its omnivorous diet frees it from dependence on any single food; its use of cavities for nesting lets it exploit a range of sites, including human structures; its strong sociality supports large flocks that gain foraging and anti-predator advantages; and its capacity for vocal learning and mimicry reflects a flexible, learning-capable brain. These adaptations evolved in the varied landscapes of Eurasia, but they pre-adapted the species for life alongside humans and for colonising new lands. The starling's flocking behaviour, including the murmuration, is an evolved response to the pressures of group living, most plausibly linked to predator avoidance: coordinated, dense, rapidly manoeuvring flocks make it hard for a hawk or falcon to single out and target an individual, and the group also benefits from many eyes for detecting threats and from information about food. From an evolutionary standpoint, the starling is a case of a generalist body and behaviour meeting a world increasingly shaped by human activity — a combination that turned a common Eurasian bird into a global one.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

European starlings are diurnal, gregarious birds whose daily and seasonal lives are dominated by sociality. Outside the breeding season they are among the most social of birds, foraging in flocks by day and gathering into enormous communal roosts at night — sometimes tens of thousands or more birds converging on a reed bed, woodland, or urban structure to roost together. It is around these roosts, especially in autumn and winter, that the famous murmurations occur: as flocks assemble before settling to roost, they perform prolonged, coordinated aerial displays, wheeling and pulsing as dense clouds across the sky. By day, starlings spread out to feed in fields, lawns, and open ground, probing the soil for invertebrates, and they move readily between foraging areas. In the breeding season the intense communal roosting gives way to a more dispersed, nest-centred life, with pairs occupying cavities, though starlings often breed in loose colonies and remain social. Many starling populations are partially migratory, with northern and eastern birds moving to milder areas for winter, so seasonal movement is part of the annual cycle in much of the range. The rhythm of the starling year is thus one of dense winter sociality — communal roosts and murmurations — alternating with a more nest-focused breeding season.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

The European starling is a capable, flexible bird, and its perception and cognition are matched to a social, generalist, vocally accomplished life. Vision is important for foraging, for social interaction, and for the extraordinary coordination of flocks in flight, where each bird must track and respond to the movements of its near neighbours quickly enough to maintain cohesion at speed. Starlings, like other birds, have good colour vision and can perceive light in ranges beyond human vision. A notable sensory-behavioural specialisation is the starling's mode of ground foraging: it uses "open-bill probing," inserting the closed bill into the soil or turf and then opening it to force a hole and expose hidden invertebrates, a technique aided by the musculature and eye position that let the bird see into the opened gap. Hearing underpins the starling's rich vocal life, including its mimicry, which requires acute auditory perception and vocal learning. Cognitively, starlings are recognised as flexible learners; they have been used in laboratory studies of learning, of song, and of auditory perception, and they readily learn to exploit new food sources and situations. As always, interpretation should be proportionate: the starling is a smart, adaptable songbird with strong vocal-learning ability and quick behavioural flexibility, but sweeping claims beyond what testing supports should be avoided. Its perceptual and cognitive strengths are those that serve a social, opportunistic, learning bird.

## 6. Feeding Behavior

The European starling is an omnivore, and its dietary flexibility is one of the keys to its success. Its diet includes invertebrates — insects and their larvae, earthworms, and other soil animals — as well as plant material such as fruit, berries, seeds, and grain, with the balance shifting through the year: animal prey is especially important in the breeding season and for feeding nestlings, while fruit, seeds, and grain feature more at other times. A characteristic and efficient foraging technique is open-bill probing, in which the starling drives its closed bill into the ground and then opens it to create a hole, exposing invertebrates hidden below the surface; flocks of starlings working across a field or lawn in this way are a familiar sight. Starlings forage socially, often in flocks, which brings the advantages of many searchers and of following successful foragers to food. Their willingness to eat almost anything and to exploit human-associated food — from farmland grain and fruit crops to livestock feed and urban scraps — lets them thrive in modified landscapes and is another root of their conflict with agriculture, since flocks can damage fruit and cereal crops and consume livestock feed. Feeding behaviour in the starling is therefore both a demonstration of adaptable, socially informed foraging and a major reason for its abundance and its pest status.

## 7. Social Behavior

The European starling is profoundly social, and its collective behaviour is its most celebrated feature. Outside the breeding season starlings live in flocks and gather in vast communal roosts, and it is the assembling flocks around these roosts that produce murmurations — the enormous, coordinated aerial displays for which the species is famous. The murmuration is best understood as self-organised collective behaviour: the coordinated, apparently choreographed motion of the whole emerges from each bird following simple rules based on the positions and movements of a limited number of near neighbours, adjusting speed and direction to stay aligned and spaced. There is no leader and no central plan; the striking global patterns arise from local interactions, a phenomenon known as emergence. This is a crucial ethological point and a common source of misunderstanding: the flock is not being directed. The likely benefits of such flocking include predator avoidance — a dense, fast-manoeuvring flock confuses and deters aerial predators and dilutes any individual's risk — and the social and informational advantages of the communal roost. In the breeding season starlings are less densely social but often nest in loose colonies and remain gregarious. Within flocks and at nest sites there is competition and aggression, particularly over nesting cavities, which is central to the starling's impact on other cavity-nesting birds. The starling's social life thus spans from the emergent grandeur of the murmuration to the ordinary squabbles of birds competing for holes.

## 8. Communication

The European starling has a varied and impressive communication repertoire, combining a complex song with notable vocal mimicry. The male's song is a long, rambling sequence of whistles, warbles, clicks, rattles, and buzzes, and interwoven into it are imitations: starlings are accomplished vocal mimics, copying the calls and songs of other bird species and other environmental sounds, and even, in some individuals, imitations of human-made sounds and speech in captivity. This mimicry is part of the male's song and contributes to a repertoire that is used in attracting mates and in social interaction. Song complexity in male starlings has been studied in relation to mate choice, with evidence that females may attend to aspects of song, and to the size of a male's repertoire, in choosing mates — an example of sexual selection on a vocal signal. Beyond song, starlings use a range of calls for contact, alarm, and aggression, and the constant chatter of a starling flock or roost is a feature of its sociality. Visual communication accompanies the vocal: plumage, posture, and display feature in interactions, and the coordinated movement of the flock is itself a form of collective visual behaviour. The combination of a complex, mimetic song and a busy repertoire of calls suits a bird whose life is lived largely in the company of others.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

European starlings are generally socially monogamous within a breeding attempt, though the mating system is flexible and includes polygyny in some males and extra-pair activity, and starlings often breed in loose colonies. Breeding centres on the nest cavity. Starlings are cavity nesters that do not excavate their own holes; they use existing cavities — tree holes, holes in buildings and other structures, and nest boxes — and they are aggressive competitors for these sites, a behaviour central to their ecological impact. The male typically establishes a nest site and begins building a nest of grass and other material within the cavity, and he sings from near the site to attract a female; song repertoire and the nest site feature in courtship, and males may decorate the nest with green plant material, which has been studied in relation to attracting females and possibly to nest hygiene. Once paired, the female lays a clutch of several eggs, commonly around four to six, pale bluish in colour, and incubation is shared to varying degrees between the sexes over roughly a couple of weeks. Starlings may raise more than one brood in a season where conditions allow. The reproductive strategy is that of a prolific, adaptable bird: relatively large clutches, a short breeding cycle, use of a wide range of cavity sites including human structures, and the potential for multiple broods, all contributing to the species' capacity for rapid population growth.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

Parental care in the European starling is biparental, with both the male and the female contributing to raising the young, though the division of labour can vary and is affected by the flexible mating system, since a polygynous male may divide effort among more than one nest. After the female lays her clutch of several eggs in the cavity nest, incubation, which is shared, lasts roughly two weeks, and the eggs hatch into altricial nestlings — naked, blind, and helpless. Both parents then feed the nestlings intensively, bringing large quantities of invertebrate prey, which supplies the protein that fast-growing chicks require; this is why the breeding season is timed to periods of abundant invertebrate food. The nestlings grow quickly and remain in the protective cavity for around three weeks before fledging. After fledging, young starlings become independent relatively soon and join the flocks, and it is often the young birds, in their plainer plumage, that make up much of the huge post-breeding flocks. The combination of a large clutch, biparental provisioning, rapid nestling growth, the safety of a cavity, and the possibility of multiple broods gives the starling a high reproductive output, which underlies both its abundance in its native range and its capacity to build up enormous populations where it has been introduced.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

The European starling is a genuinely capable learner, and it has earned a place in the scientific study of animal cognition and behaviour. Its vocal mimicry is a clear demonstration of vocal learning: starlings acquire and reproduce the sounds of other species and their environment, and captive individuals can learn remarkable imitations, including of human speech. This learning ability extends to foraging and to exploiting new situations, which is part of why the species adapts so readily to novel and human-modified environments. Starlings have been used as laboratory subjects in studies of auditory perception, learning, and cognition — including well-known research on how they perceive and categorise sounds — making them an established model in behavioural science. Song and repertoire size have been examined in relation to mate choice, connecting the starling's learning ability to sexual selection. All of this supports describing the starling as an intelligent, flexible, learning-capable bird. At the same time, proportion matters: while starlings are smart and adaptable, they are not usually placed among the very top avian cognitive specialists like some corvids and parrots, and claims should be kept to what evidence supports — strong vocal learning, behavioural flexibility, and success as an opportunistic generalist. The murmuration, it should be stressed, is not evidence of a collective "group mind" or of high individual intelligence; it emerges from simple local rules, and confusing emergent coordination with intelligence is a common error.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

The European starling's principal natural threats are birds of prey — hawks and falcons that hunt flocking birds — and predators of eggs and nestlings at the cavity. Its defences are strongly tied to its sociality. Flocking is itself a key anti-predator strategy: large, dense, coordinated flocks and the manoeuvres of a murmuration make it difficult for an aerial predator to isolate and catch an individual, both by confusing the predator and by diluting each bird's personal risk, and the flock provides many eyes to detect danger early. The communal roost offers safety in numbers as well. At the nest, the enclosed cavity protects eggs and young, and starlings are vigorous in competing for and defending nest sites. Alarm calls coordinate the flock's response to threats. For the individual bird, the immediate responses to danger are alarm-calling and flight into the protective flock or cover. The most significant modern stressors on starlings are human-related and vary by region: in the native range, agricultural change and other factors have driven declines in some areas, while in introduced ranges starlings are subject to deliberate control as pests. The starling's defensive strategy, in short, is overwhelmingly a social one — the safety of the flock and the roost, plus the protection of the cavity nest — rather than individual defence.

## 13. Human Interaction

No aspect of the European starling is more entangled with humans than its relationship to us, which spans admiration, exploitation, and conflict. On one hand, the starling is a beloved spectacle: murmurations draw crowds and are among the most celebrated displays in nature, and the bird's mimicry and adaptability have long fascinated people. On the other hand, the starling is one of the world's most notorious invasive species, and much of that story is a human story. The North American population descends from birds deliberately released in New York in the 1890s — an introduction often linked in popular accounts to an effort to establish in America the birds mentioned in the works of Shakespeare, a detail widely repeated though its precise history is debated — and from a small founding number the species spread across the continent to become one of North America's most abundant birds. There and in other introduced ranges, starlings compete aggressively with native cavity-nesting birds for nest holes, damage fruit and cereal crops, consume livestock feed, foul roosting sites, and pose hazards to aircraft, and they are subject to extensive control efforts. Meanwhile, in parts of its native European range, the starling has declined, prompting conservation concern at home even as it is fought as a pest abroad. This paradox — a species simultaneously declining where native and superabundant where introduced — makes the starling a central case study in invasion biology, in the unintended consequences of introductions, and in the complex, contradictory ways humans relate to a single successful bird.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

```text
Myth: A murmuration is led by a leader bird or follows a master plan that directs the flock.
Scientific reality: There is no leader and no central plan. A murmuration is self-organised collective behaviour: each bird follows simple rules based on the position and movement of a limited number of near neighbours, adjusting to stay aligned and spaced. The striking patterns of the whole flock emerge from these local interactions — a phenomenon called emergence. Treating the murmuration as choreographed or directed misunderstands how it works.
```

```text
Myth: The murmuration shows that starlings have a collective "group mind" or exceptional intelligence.
Scientific reality: The coordinated motion emerges from simple local rules followed by individuals, not from a shared mind or high individual intelligence. Emergent collective behaviour and intelligence are different things. Starlings are capable learners, but the murmuration is a demonstration of self-organisation, not of a group intellect.
```

Myth: Starlings are native to North America.

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

An apt comparison is with the house sparrow (*Passer domesticus*), another small, adaptable, human-associated Old World bird that has been introduced around the world and, like the starling, became a widespread and sometimes problematic resident of North America and other regions. Both species are commensal with humans, thrive in farmland and urban environments, exploit human-associated food, nest in cavities and structures, and were introduced to North America in the nineteenth century, spreading widely. Both are also cited as species that compete with native cavity-nesting birds. Yet they differ instructively. The starling is a larger, gregarious, roost-forming bird famous for murmurations and vocal mimicry, and it forages by open-bill probing of the ground; the house sparrow is a smaller seed-and-scrap eater that forms looser social groups and lacks the spectacular collective aerial displays. Both, notably, have declined in parts of their native ranges even as they flourish as introduced birds — a shared paradox that makes both species case studies in how human-adapted generalists can succeed abroad while faltering at home. The comparison highlights that "commensal, adaptable, introduced generalist" is a repeatable ecological formula, achieved by more than one lineage, and that such species can be simultaneously invasive pests in one place and declining natives in another.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

The European starling teaches several important lessons. First and foremost, the murmuration is the premier accessible example of self-organised collective behaviour and emergence: complex, coordinated group patterns arising from simple rules followed by individuals responding to their near neighbours, with no leader and no master plan. Understanding this corrects a deep and common misconception and connects ethology to the wider science of complex systems. Second, and relatedly, the starling warns against confusing emergent coordination with intelligence — the flock's grandeur is not a "group mind." Third, the starling is a central case study in invasion biology and the unintended consequences of human introductions: a small nineteenth-century release grew into a continental population that competes with native birds and damages crops, illustrating how a human-adapted generalist can transform ecosystems. Fourth, the paradox of a species declining where native yet superabundant where introduced is a lesson in the limits of global conservation categories and the importance of regional detail. Fifth, the starling's vocal mimicry and its use as a laboratory subject show a flexible, learning-capable bird, described in proportion to the evidence. These are lessons about emergence, invasion, learning, and the interpretation of collective behaviour — not moral fables.

## 17. Glossary

**Murmuration** — the large, coordinated aerial display of a flock of starlings, especially around roosts; a case of self-organised collective motion.
**Self-organisation** — the arising of ordered, coordinated patterns from local interactions among individuals, without central control.
**Emergence** — the appearance of global patterns or behaviour from simple local rules; the murmuration is an emergent phenomenon.
**Local rules** — the simple behavioural rules each bird follows based on its near neighbours' positions and movements, producing flock coordination.
**Commensal (with humans)** — living in close association with humans and benefiting from human environments; the starling is strongly commensal.
**Invasive species** — a species introduced outside its native range that spreads and causes ecological or economic harm; the starling in North America.
**Cavity nester** — a bird that nests in a hole; the starling uses existing cavities and does not excavate its own.
**Open-bill probing** — the starling's foraging technique of inserting the closed bill into the ground and opening it to expose hidden prey.
**Vocal mimicry** — the imitation of the sounds of other species or the environment; a well-developed ability in starlings.
**Communal roost** — a large gathering of birds roosting together at night, characteristic of non-breeding starlings.
**Altricial** — describing chicks that hatch naked, blind, and helpless, as starling nestlings do.
**Social monogamy** — forming a breeding pair while allowing extra-pair activity; the starling's typical, flexible system, with some polygyny.
**Sturnidae** — the starling family.
**Partial migration** — a pattern in which some individuals of a population migrate while others do not; seen in many starling populations.
**Dilution effect** — the reduction of any individual's predation risk by being one of many in a group; a benefit of flocking.
