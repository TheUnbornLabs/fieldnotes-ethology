# Summary — The European Starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*)

The European starling is one of the world's most successful and most controversial birds — at once the author of the breathtaking murmuration, a gifted vocal mimic, and one of the most notorious invasive species on Earth. A medium-sized, glossy, spotted songbird native to Eurasia and North Africa, it is a habitat generalist strongly associated with humans, thriving in farmland, grassland, and cities. Its omnivorous diet, cavity-nesting habit, strong sociality, and capacity for vocal learning combine to make it an outstanding generalist and, where introduced, an invader.

The starling's most celebrated behaviour is the murmuration: the enormous, shape-shifting aerial display performed by flocks gathering at winter roosts. Ethologically this is a textbook case of self-organised collective behaviour. The coordinated, seemingly choreographed motion of the whole flock emerges from each bird following simple rules based on the positions and movements of a few near neighbours — with no leader and no master plan. This is emergence, and it must not be confused with a collective "group mind" or with high individual intelligence. Flocking of this kind also serves as anti-predator defence, confusing aerial hunters and diluting each bird's risk.

Starlings forage by open-bill probing, driving the closed bill into the ground and opening it to expose hidden invertebrates, and they eat both animal and plant food. They are cavity nesters that do not excavate their own holes but compete aggressively for existing ones — a habit central to their impact on native cavity-nesting birds. Both parents raise the altricial young, often producing large clutches and sometimes multiple broods, giving the species high reproductive output. Males sing complex songs laced with mimicry of other birds and environmental sounds, and repertoire features in mate choice.

The starling is also a central case study in invasion biology. The North American population descends from birds deliberately released in New York in the 1890s and spread across the continent, where starlings damage crops, compete with native birds, and are controlled as pests. Paradoxically, the species has declined in parts of its native European range even as it is superabundant abroad — a reminder that global "Least Concern" status can conceal regional decline. Compared with the house sparrow, another introduced human-adapted generalist, the starling shows that "commensal invasive generalist" is a repeatable ecological formula.
