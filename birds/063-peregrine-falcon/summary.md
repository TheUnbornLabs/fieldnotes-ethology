# Summary — The Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*)

The peregrine falcon is the fastest animal on Earth, a distinction it owes to a single specialised behaviour: the hunting stoop. Climbing above its prey, the falcon folds its wings and drops in a steep, controlled dive that can exceed 300 kilometres per hour, converting height into overwhelming speed to overtake a flying bird before it can react. This makes the peregrine an unusually clear example of how a predator's anatomy, senses, and behaviour are integrated around one ecological task.

The body is built for the stoop. The wings are stiff and tapered, changing shape through the dive to reduce drag and maintain control. Small bony structures inside the nostrils — nostril baffles or tubercles — are widely thought to help manage airflow at high speed so the bird can keep breathing. A nictitating membrane, a translucent third eyelid, sweeps across the eye to protect and clean it during the dive and at impact. The peregrine's vision is among the sharpest known: each eye has two foveae, including a deep central fovea, and the retina is sensitive into the ultraviolet, letting the falcon detect a pigeon-sized bird more than a kilometre away.

Peregrines hunt birds in flight almost exclusively. At the end of the stoop the falcon strikes with a clenched foot, stunning or killing the prey with the force of the blow and knocking it from the air; only afterward does it use the notched tomial tooth on its beak to sever the neck. Falcons are not hawks — they belong to a separate family, Falconidae, more closely related to parrots and songbirds than to the hawks and eagles they superficially resemble.

The species is nearly cosmopolitan, breeding on every continent except Antarctica. Traditionally a cliff-nester, it makes no real nest, only a simple scrape on a ledge. Pairs form strong bonds, perform dramatic aerial courtship, and raise chicks called eyases, feeding them through aerial food-passes and later dropping prey for fledglings to chase as hunting practice. Remarkably, peregrines have colonised cities, nesting on skyscrapers and bridges and hunting feral pigeons and starlings above the streets.

The peregrine's modern history is defined by the pesticide DDT, whose residues thinned the birds' eggshells and collapsed populations across the Northern Hemisphere in the mid-20th century. After DDT was banned, a landmark recovery followed, led by Tom Cade and The Peregrine Fund through captive breeding and "hacking," the gradual release of young falcons from artificial ledges. The peregrine is now listed as Least Concern. Long the most prized bird in falconry, it stands today as both a marvel of specialised design and one of conservation's great success stories.
