# Book 063 — The Peregrine Falcon: The Fastest Hunter Alive

## 1. Opening Summary

The peregrine falcon (*Falco peregrinus*) is the fastest animal on Earth, and it earns that title in a single, specialised behaviour: the hunting stoop, a folded-wing power dive that can exceed 300 kilometres per hour, with the fastest recorded dives measured at roughly 390 km/h (about 240 mph). (The exact peak-speed figure comes from a small number of measured dives and should be treated as approximate — *needs verification before publication*.) No other predator combines such extreme speed with such precise control, and this makes the peregrine an unusually clean case study in how anatomy, sensory equipment, and behaviour are tuned together to a single ecological problem — striking a flying bird out of open air.

For ethology the peregrine matters on several fronts. It shows how a predator's body and senses are integrated with its hunting behaviour, from a stiff wing to the deep central foveae of the eye and the bony structures inside the nostrils thought to help manage airflow at speed. It offers one of the best-documented conservation recoveries in history: after DDT thinned its eggshells and collapsed populations across the Northern Hemisphere, captive breeding and release rebuilt the species. And it demonstrates behavioural flexibility, having moved from remote cliffs onto city skyscrapers and bridges, where it hunts feral pigeons and starlings above the streets. The peregrine is at once a specialist of extraordinary refinement and an adaptable generalist of place.

## 2. Scientific Identity

- **Common name:** Peregrine falcon (the word *peregrine* means "wanderer," referring to its long-distance movements).
- **Scientific name:** *Falco peregrinus*.
- **Taxonomic group:** Order Falconiformes, family **Falconidae** (the true falcons and caracaras). Despite superficial resemblance, falcons are not closely related to hawks and eagles (Accipitridae); molecular work places Falconidae nearer to parrots and passerines. Numerous subspecies are recognised across the species' range.
- **Habitat:** Extremely varied — sea cliffs, mountain crags, river gorges, open country, coasts, wetlands, and increasingly urban environments where tall buildings and bridges substitute for cliffs. The unifying requirement is a high nesting ledge overlooking open airspace with abundant bird prey.
- **Geographic range:** One of the most widely distributed birds in the world, breeding on every continent except Antarctica. Some northern populations are strongly migratory, moving thousands of kilometres between breeding and wintering grounds; others are resident.
- **Diet:** Primarily medium-sized birds caught in flight — pigeons, doves, shorebirds, waterfowl, starlings, and many others; occasionally bats and, rarely, small mammals.
- **Lifespan:** Often several years in the wild, with some banded individuals living well over a decade; longer in captivity. Mortality is highest in the first year.
- **Conservation status:** IUCN Red List — **Least Concern**, following a strong recovery from the DDT-era crash.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

The peregrine is the product of selection for high-speed aerial predation on birds. Where many raptors evolved toward soaring and searching over ground for slower or hidden prey, falcons of the genus *Falco* specialised in overtaking agile, fast-flying birds in the open. This favoured a compact, muscular body, long pointed wings that reduce drag and allow rapid wingbeats, and a nervous and sensory system capable of tracking and intercepting a moving target at closing speeds no other animal reaches.

The stoop itself is an evolutionary solution to a hard problem: a level tail-chase against a fast bird is often a losing proposition, but a dive from height converts stored altitude into overwhelming speed, letting the falcon close before the prey can react. The peregrine's form — the tapering wing that stiffens into a swept dart, the deep keel and flight muscles, the reinforced and protected eyes, and the structures inside the nostrils — reflects the demands of controlling and surviving that dive. The tomial "tooth" on the beak, shared with other falcons, points to a lineage that dispatches vertebrate prey by severing the neck, distinct from the crushing-and-gripping strategy of hawks.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Peregrines are **diurnal** hunters, most active in the hours after dawn and again before dusk when prey is moving. They are **territorial** around the nest site, defending a home range that centres on a favoured ledge and its surrounding airspace. A resident pair may hold the same cliff or building for years, and prime sites can be occupied by successive generations of falcons.

Outside the breeding season, behaviour varies with latitude. Northern and Arctic breeders are long-distance **migrants**, some travelling between the high Arctic and the Southern Hemisphere; temperate and urban birds are often year-round residents. A hunting peregrine spends much of its day perched on a high vantage — a crag, a pylon, a skyscraper ledge — scanning the sky, punctuated by patrol flights and hunting sorties. Much of the day is spent watching rather than flying; the spectacular stoop is a brief, decisive event within an otherwise patient routine.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

Vision dominates the peregrine's perceptual world, and it is among the most acute in the animal kingdom. Each eye contains **two foveae** (regions of maximum sharpness) rather than the single fovea typical of mammals — a shallow lateral fovea and a **deep central fovea** whose pit magnifies the image and gives exceptional resolving power directed forward and to the side. Peregrines can detect a pigeon-sized bird at ranges of well over a kilometre. Their retinas have a very high density of cones, and, like many birds, they are sensitive into the **ultraviolet**, which can make certain plumage and trails more conspicuous. Eye movements are limited, so the bird turns its head to fix targets, and its visual processing supports the split-second corrections needed during a high-speed intercept.

The eye is protected by a **nictitating membrane**, a translucent third eyelid that sweeps across the surface to clean and moisten it and to shield it during the stoop and at the moment of impact, without fully blinding the bird. Hearing is functional but secondary. Smell plays little role. The brain regions governing vision and rapid motor control are correspondingly well developed, matching a lifestyle in which everything depends on seeing a target early and intercepting it precisely.

## 6. Feeding Behavior

The peregrine is a specialist **aerial hunter of birds**. Its signature tactic is the **stoop**: the falcon climbs above its prey, then folds its wings and drops in a steep, controlled dive, accelerating toward the top speeds for which it is famous. At the point of contact it does not usually grab the prey outright. Instead it strikes with a **clenched foot** — a raking or punching blow delivered at speed that stuns or kills the target and knocks it from the air. The falcon then wheels back, catches the falling bird or follows it down, and uses the **tomial tooth**, a notch on the upper beak, to sever the neck and dispatch it.

Several anatomical features make sustained high-speed diving possible. The **wing** is stiff and tapered, changing shape through the dive to minimise drag and maintain control. Small bony projections — often called **nostril baffles** or **tubercles** — sit within the nostrils and are widely thought to help manage the airflow entering the nares at extreme speed, so the bird can continue to breathe under conditions that would otherwise disrupt airflow; this is a frequently cited functional interpretation that continues to be studied. Not every hunt is a full stoop: peregrines also take prey in level pursuit, ambush birds from a perch, and hunt cooperatively as a pair, one bird flushing prey while the other strikes.

## 7. Social Behavior

Peregrines are essentially **solitary or paired** rather than flocking birds. The core social unit is the breeding pair, which defends a territory and shares the work of raising young. Pair bonds can be strong and long-lasting, with the same two birds returning to a site across seasons, though a lost mate is usually replaced.

Away from the nest, peregrines tolerate little company; they are not colonial and do not hunt in groups beyond the cooperating pair. Interactions with other peregrines and with rival raptors are frequently aggressive, involving spectacular aerial chases and stoops directed at intruders. Around the nest the pair mobs larger predators and competitors, including eagles and, in cities, gulls and corvids. This low sociality is characteristic of a predator whose hunting depends on individual skill and the control of airspace rather than on group living.

## 8. Communication

Peregrine communication is concentrated at the nest and territory. The most familiar sound is a loud, rasping **"kak-kak-kak" or "cack" alarm call**, given when a bird or human intruder approaches the eyrie; it is harsh, repeated, and carries well. A softer, wailing or chittering set of calls accompanies interactions between the pair, including greetings, the transfer of food, and nest-relief.

Visual signalling is important in **courtship and territory defence**. Pairs perform dramatic aerial displays — high circling, steep dives, rolls, and mock passes — that advertise fitness and reinforce the bond, and that also serve to warn off rivals. Body posture at the ledge, bowing between partners, and food presentation all carry information within the pair. Because peregrines live at low density and rely on conspicuous flight, much of their signalling is visual and long-range, backed by a small vocabulary of far-carrying calls at close quarters.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

Peregrines are typically **monogamous** within a breeding season and often across years, with both members of the pair investing heavily in the nesting attempt. Courtship features the species' most spectacular flying: the pair perform synchronised aerial displays, steep dives, and rolls, and the male may pass food to the female in mid-air as a courtship offering and a demonstration of his hunting ability.

They do not build a structured nest. Instead the female makes a simple **scrape** — a shallow depression scratched into gravel, soil, or debris on a high cliff ledge, and increasingly on the ledges and nest boxes of tall buildings and bridges. A typical clutch is a small number of eggs, incubated mainly by the female while the male provisions her. Breeding is seasonal, timed so that the demand of growing chicks coincides with abundant prey. Choice of a defensible, well-placed ledge is central to reproductive success, and competition for prime sites can be intense.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

The chicks, called **eyases** (singular **eyas**), hatch covered in white down and are wholly dependent on their parents. In the early stage the female broods and shelters them while the male hunts and delivers prey; the female tears food into pieces small enough for the young to swallow. As the eyases grow, both parents hunt to meet their rising demand.

The **aerial food-pass** becomes central to family life: an incoming parent carries prey to the nest area, and food is transferred to the female or, later, to fledged young, sometimes passed talon-to-talon in flight. Young peregrines develop quickly and fledge within several weeks, but they remain dependent for a period afterward, during which they practise flying and hunting. Parents help by dropping prey for the fledglings to chase and catch in the air — a form of practice that builds the coordination the stoop demands. Early mortality is high; learning to hunt effectively is the main hurdle to independence.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

Peregrine intelligence is best understood as finely tuned **sensorimotor and predatory skill** rather than the object-manipulating problem-solving associated with crows or parrots. Effective hunting is not fully innate: fledglings that can already fly are clumsy hunters, and their success improves markedly with practice as they learn to judge distance, closing speed, and the moment to strike. The parents' habit of passing and dropping prey for the young to pursue supports this learning.

Experienced peregrines show clear behavioural flexibility. They adjust their tactics to local prey and conditions, exploit specific hunting opportunities such as concentrations of migrating birds or, in cities, roosts of pigeons and starlings, and time their sorties to when prey is most exposed. Urban peregrines have adapted their whole hunting routine to a landscape of buildings, lights, and dense feral-pigeon populations, including hunting by artificial light. This capacity to read and exploit a novel environment, within the constraints of a highly specialised body, is the peregrine's cognitive signature.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

An adult peregrine has few natural predators and is itself a formidable aggressor, so its defensive behaviour centres on the **nest and young**. When a threat approaches the eyrie, the pair gives loud alarm calls and mounts vigorous aerial defence, diving at and striking intruders — including much larger eagles, and humans who venture near the ledge. This bold mobbing is effective enough to deter most nest predators.

Eggs and helpless eyases are the vulnerable stage, at risk from mammalian and avian predators able to reach the ledge, which is one reason peregrines favour inaccessible, overhung sites. Away from the nest, the main pressures are competition, weather, and the risk of injury during high-speed hunting itself. For a predator of the peregrine's abilities, the primary "defence" is the same equipment that makes it a hunter: speed, agility, and a willingness to attack a threat head-on.

## 13. Human Interaction

Few birds have a more entangled history with people than the peregrine. It has been the most prized bird in **falconry** for centuries, valued for its speed, trainability, and the drama of the stoop, and it retains that status among falconers today. Culturally it is a widespread symbol of speed, keen sight, and martial prowess.

The defining modern chapter is the **DDT crisis**. From the 1940s the pesticide DDT and its breakdown product DDE accumulated in food chains and caused peregrines to lay thin-shelled eggs that broke under the incubating bird. Populations collapsed across North America and much of Europe, and the peregrine disappeared as a breeding bird from large regions, including the eastern United States. After DDT was banned in many countries, a landmark recovery followed: **Tom Cade** founded **The Peregrine Fund**, which pioneered large-scale **captive breeding** and the release technique known as **hacking**, in which young falcons are reared and gradually released from artificial ledges until they can hunt for themselves. Reintroduced birds recolonised cliffs and, strikingly, adopted **cities**, nesting on skyscrapers, cathedrals, and bridges and hunting the abundant feral pigeons and starlings of the urban sky. Today many people first meet peregrines through nest-box webcams on tall buildings. The species remains protected, and threats such as illegal persecution, collisions, and residual contaminants persist, but the peregrine is now a conservation success story.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

```text
Myth 1: Peregrine falcons are dangerous to people.
Scientific reality: Peregrines pose no threat to humans. They hunt birds in
flight and are far too small to regard a person as prey. The only aggression
people encounter is defensive dive-bombing near a nest, which is meant to
drive an intruder away, not to injure; keeping a respectful distance from an
eyrie resolves it.

Myth 2: The peregrine falcon is still an endangered species.
Scientific reality: The peregrine crashed during the DDT era, but after the
pesticide was banned and captive-breeding and release programmes rebuilt
populations, it recovered strongly. It is now listed as Least Concern on the
IUCN Red List and nests widely, including in many cities.

Myth 3: A stooping peregrine kills its prey by biting it with the beak.
Scientific reality: At the end of the dive the peregrine strikes the prey with
a clenched foot, stunning or killing it with the force of the blow and knocking
it from the air. The notched tomial tooth on the beak is used afterward, to
sever the neck and dispatch the prey once it has been secured — not to make
the initial kill at speed.

Myth 4: Falcons are just a kind of hawk.
Scientific reality: Falcons belong to their own family, Falconidae, which is
not closely related to the hawks and eagles (Accipitridae). Genetic studies
place falcons nearer to parrots and songbirds. The resemblance between falcons
and hawks is a case of convergent evolution toward a predatory lifestyle, not
close kinship.

Myth 5: The peregrine flies at over 300 km/h in normal, level flight.
Scientific reality: Those record speeds are reached only in the stoop, a
gravity-assisted dive from height, not in ordinary flapping flight. In level
pursuit the peregrine is fast but flies at a small fraction of its stooping
speed. The famous figure describes a brief, specialised dive.

Myth 6: The bony bumps inside a peregrine's nostrils were the direct model for
jet-engine intakes.
Scientific reality: The nostril tubercles or baffles are real structures widely
interpreted as helping manage airflow into the nostrils at high speed. The
popular claim that engineers copied them to design supersonic jet intakes is a
persistent story that is not well supported; treat it as an anecdote, not
established engineering history.
```

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

A useful contrast is the peregrine falcon against the **golden eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)**, because they represent two very different solutions to being a large diurnal raptor.

The peregrine is a **high-speed aerial specialist**. Its long, pointed, stiff wings and compact body are built for acceleration and control at extreme speed; it hunts almost exclusively **birds taken in flight**, striking them with a clenched foot at the end of a stoop and killing with a tomial tooth. It is comparatively small, breeds on ledges via a simple scrape, and dominates open airspace.

The golden eagle is a **soaring generalist** of the family Accipitridae. Its broad, long, slotted wings are built for economical soaring and lift over terrain; it searches the ground and takes a wide range of **mammalian and bird prey** — hares, marmots, ground birds, and carrion — which it seizes and kills with immense **gripping talons** rather than a high-speed strike. It is far larger and heavier, and builds a bulky stick nest.

The comparison sharpens what the peregrine is: not simply a "smaller eagle," but a member of a separate lineage tuned to a different problem. Where the eagle's power lies in soaring endurance and crushing grip over open ground, the peregrine's lies in speed, acute vision, and the split-second interception of a flying bird. Two large raptors, two families, two entirely different hunting economies.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

The peregrine teaches how tightly **anatomy, sensory systems, and behaviour co-evolve around a single ecological task**. The stoop is not just a fast dive; it is made possible by a stiff tapered wing, a reinforced and protected eye with deep central foveae, nostril structures thought to manage airflow, and a tomial tooth for dispatch — a whole body organised around one hunting strategy. Studying the peregrine shows that a "behaviour" like high-speed predation is really an integrated package of form, perception, and action.

It also illustrates that even a highly specialised predator can be **behaviourally flexible**: peregrines moved from cliffs to skyscrapers and learned to exploit urban pigeons, showing that specialisation of the body does not preclude adaptability of habit. And the DDT episode offers a stark ecological lesson about **bioaccumulation and top predators** — a persistent chemical spread thinly through the environment concentrated up the food chain and struck the peregrine hardest precisely because it sits at the top. The recovery that followed, driven by captive breeding and hacking, is one of the clearest demonstrations that informed intervention can reverse a human-caused collapse. These are lessons about integration, adaptability, and responsibility, not manufactured moral tales.

## 17. Glossary

**Stoop** — the peregrine's high-speed hunting dive, in which it folds its wings and drops from height, converting altitude into speed to overtake and strike flying prey.

**Terminal velocity** — the maximum speed at which drag balances the accelerating force of gravity in a dive; the peregrine's streamlined, wing-folding posture allows it to reach very high speeds before this limit slows it.

**Tomial tooth** — a notch and matching projection on the cutting edge of the upper beak, characteristic of falcons, used to sever the neck of prey and dispatch it.

**Nostril baffle / tubercle** — a small bony structure inside the peregrine's nostril, widely interpreted as helping manage the airflow entering the nares during high-speed flight so the bird can continue to breathe.

**Nictitating membrane** — a translucent third eyelid that sweeps sideways across the eye to clean, moisten, and protect it, including during the stoop and at impact, without fully obscuring vision.

**Fovea** — a pit in the retina where visual receptors are densest and sight is sharpest; peregrines have two foveae per eye, including a deep central fovea giving exceptional acuity.

**Visual acuity** — the sharpness or resolving power of vision; the peregrine's is among the highest known, letting it detect small birds at great distances.

**Ultraviolet vision** — sensitivity to ultraviolet light, present in many birds including the peregrine, which can make some plumage patterns and trails more visible.

**Scrape nest** — a simple nest consisting of a shallow depression scratched into gravel, soil, or ledge debris, with no built structure; the peregrine's typical nest.

**Food-pass** — the transfer of prey between falcons in flight, often from an incoming parent to its mate or to fledged young, sometimes talon-to-talon.

**Hacking** — a falconry-derived release technique used in reintroductions, in which young falcons are reared and gradually released from an artificial ledge, being fed until they can hunt for themselves.

**Captive breeding** — breeding birds in controlled conditions to produce young for release, central to the peregrine's recovery after the DDT-era crash.

**DDT** — a synthetic pesticide whose residues (chiefly DDE) accumulate in food chains and caused eggshell thinning in peregrines and other raptors, driving major population declines before it was banned in many countries.

**Eyas** (plural **eyases**) — a nestling peregrine (or other falcon) still in the nest; a falconry term also used in ornithology.

**Eyrie (aerie)** — the nesting ledge or site of a bird of prey such as the peregrine.
