# Summary — The Great Spotted Woodpecker (*Dendrocopos major*)

The great spotted woodpecker, *Dendrocopos major*, is a medium-small pied woodpecker of the family Picidae, widespread across the Palearctic in temperate and boreal woodland, parks, and gardens from Britain to Japan. It is a broad omnivore — taking wood-boring insect larvae, other insects, conifer seeds and nuts, and seasonally the eggs and nestlings of other birds — and it is assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List.

The species is ethologically instructive because it forces a distinction that popular accounts blur. It uses its bill in two very different ways. Excavation is the slow, forceful chiselling used to reach insect food and to dig nest cavities. Drumming is a rapid, rhythmic burst of blows on a resonant surface chosen for its sound — a long-distance communication signal, not feeding, that advertises territory and attracts a mate. Both sexes drum, with males generally drumming more. Distinguishing these two behaviours is a first lesson in careful observation.

The woodpecker body is an integrated toolkit for working wood: zygodactyl feet with two toes forward and two back grip vertical bark, stiff tail feathers prop the bird into a tripod, a reinforced bill chisels, and an exceptionally long, barbed, sticky tongue — supported by the hyoid apparatus that curls around the skull — extracts larvae from tunnels the bird cannot see into. The bird also uses "anvils," crevices where it wedges cones or nuts to hammer them open, returning to favoured sites and improving with practice.

The much-repeated claim that the skull is a perfect natural shock absorber is only partly supported. Several features plausibly help the bird tolerate repeated impacts, but the precise biomechanics remain an active research question, and the honest account flags that uncertainty rather than repeating a tidy myth.

Great spotted woodpeckers are largely solitary and territorial, spaced across the woodland and communicating by sharp "kik" calls and drumming rather than by flocking. They are seasonally monogamous; both sexes excavate a fresh nest cavity, share short incubation, and feed altricial young that grow noisy before fledging. By making new cavities each year, woodpeckers act as ecological engineers, supplying nest holes for many other species. Compared with the ground-foraging, ant-eating green woodpecker, the great spotted woodpecker highlights how a shared body plan can diverge toward different foods and signals. Its lasting lessons are about separating similar behaviours, integrated adaptation, honest uncertainty, and ecological engineering.
