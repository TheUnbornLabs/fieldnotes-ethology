# Summary — The Carrion Crow (*Corvus corone*)

The carrion crow is an all-black corvid of Europe and eastern Asia and one of the most successful large birds living alongside humans. A member of the crow family, it combines a generalist diet, a flexible temperament, and a large brain relative to its body size. Its name reflects its scavenging, but it eats almost anything: invertebrates, small vertebrates, eggs and nestlings, grain, fruit, carrion, and human food waste. This breadth is a large part of its success and buffers it against the failure of any single food source. The IUCN Red List assesses the species as Least Concern.

Carrion crows are diurnal and lead a two-sided social life. In spring, mated pairs — often bonded for years, sometimes assisted by grown offspring in a form of cooperative breeding — defend a nesting territory. Outside the breeding season, non-breeders flock, and in autumn and winter many birds gather at large communal roosts. Across a band of central Europe and the British Isles the carrion crow meets the grey-and-black hooded crow, interbreeding along a narrow hybrid zone.

Intelligence is the species' defining theme. Carrion crows are strong problem-solvers, though they are not habitual tool-users like the New Caledonian crow; their cleverness shows instead through flexibility — learning new food sources, exploiting human routines, and caching surplus food while guarding it against pilferers. Corvids tested on water-displacement, "Aesop's fable" style tasks can select functional objects to change their physical environment and reach a floating reward, evidence of a grasp of certain cause-and-effect relationships. Widely reported behaviors include dropping hard nuts onto hard surfaces to crack them; the further claim that crows deliberately use car traffic to break nuts is intriguing but largely anecdotal and should be treated cautiously.

Crows also read the human world with striking precision. They recognise individual human faces and remember which people have threatened them, and this learned wariness can spread through a population to birds that never had the original encounter — though much of the strongest experimental work is on the closely related American crow (Marzluff), so it must be attributed carefully. Gatherings around dead crows, sometimes called "crow funerals," appear to be less about mourning than about learning where danger occurred; again the key studies (Swift & Marzluff) are on American crows, with carrion crows showing comparable abilities.

The carrion crow is deeply entangled with people: a classic synanthrope and urban adapter, an ecologically useful scavenger, a persecuted farm pest, and a heavily symbolic figure in folklore. It teaches that intelligence in the wild is best understood as flexibility, that a single crow's discovery can become shared knowledge through social learning, and that calibrated caution — neophobia balanced by the ability to overcome it — lets a wild bird thrive in the human world without being tamed by it.
