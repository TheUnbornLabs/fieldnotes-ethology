# References / Further Reading — The Carrion Crow (*Corvus corone*)

Sources are separated into those safe to name and those that should be checked against a database before final publication. Following the collection's citation policy, no authors, titles, journals, years, DOIs, or page numbers have been invented.

## Well-established sources (safe to name)

- John M. Marzluff and Tony Angell, *In the Company of Crows and Ravens* — a widely read popular-science account of corvid behavior and the crow–human relationship.
- John M. Marzluff and Tony Angell, *Gifts of the Crow* — an accessible synthesis of corvid cognition and behavior for a general readership.
- Corvid-cognition research associated with Nicola Clayton and colleagues (Cambridge) — a well-known body of experimental work on corvid memory, caching, and physical/social cognition. Named generally here; verify specific studies (including water-displacement / "Aesop's fable" experiments) before citing article-level details.
- IUCN Red List — species assessment for *Corvus corone* (carrion crow), global status Least Concern. Consult the current Red List entry for range, population, and threats.
- Cornell Lab of Ornithology (Birds of the World / All About Birds) — species accounts and general corvid biology.
- Animal Diversity Web (University of Michigan Museum of Zoology) — species profile for the genus *Corvus* and related crows.
- Standard animal-behavior textbooks (e.g. Alcock, *Animal Behavior*) for general ethological concepts such as social learning, communication, caching, and innovation.

## To verify before final publication

Note carefully that several of the strongest studies were conducted on the **American crow** and should be attributed to that species, with carrion crows noted as showing comparable abilities.

- Experimental studies on crows recognising individual human faces and remembering threatening people — much of this work is on the American crow (John Marzluff and colleagues). *Further reading to verify before final publication.*
- Studies on the social transmission of learned wariness toward specific humans through a crow population. *Further reading to verify before final publication.*

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

- Scientific name *Corvus corone*, family Corvidae, order Passeriformes — confirmed (Animal Diversity Web; BirdLife DataZone; Birds of the World).
- IUCN Red List status **Least Concern** — confirmed via BirdLife DataFactsheet / IUCN (assessed 2024; range ~85 million km², population 54–92 million mature individuals, stable trend).
- Split from hooded crow (*Corvus cornix*) with a narrow hybrid zone — treatment as separate species confirmed by BirdLife/IOC; both are recognised species. (Note: some legacy sources still treat cornix as a subspecies of corone; the book's framing as a modern split is accurate.)
