# Book 069 — The Ostrich: Summary

The Ostrich (*Struthio camelus*) is the world's largest and heaviest living bird, standing up to about 2.8 meters tall; large males can weigh well over 100 kilograms, with females somewhat smaller. Despite being flightless, ostriches are among the fastest two-legged runners on Earth, capable of sustained speeds of 50+ km/h and bursts to 70 km/h. Native to African savannas, grasslands, semi-deserts, and deserts, ostriches are nomadic within large home ranges, moving in search of water and forage. They are herbivorous and omnivorous ground foragers, eating seeds, grasses, leaves, insects, and occasionally small vertebrates.

Ostriches are polygamous and moderately social. Territorial males establish and defend territories, mating with multiple females within their area. Females do not form pair bonds; instead, multiple females lay eggs in communal nests — sometimes 40+ eggs in a single nest. Males incubate eggs at night; females may incubate during the day. Incubation lasts 42–46 days. Chicks are precocial, mobile within days of hatching, and form crèches (groups) with chicks from multiple nests, cared for cooperatively by adults.

Ostriches have large eyes, giving them excellent vision for detecting predators in open grasslands. Their large body size and powerful legs provide defense against predators; they deliver powerful kicks that can injure or kill large threats. Their primary predator-escape strategy is running at high speed and with maneuverability. Adult ostriches have been hunted and revered by humans for millennia, from ancient Egypt through the 19th-century feather trade to modern commercial farming in South Africa and elsewhere. Wild populations persist in African protected areas, though habitat loss and localized overhunting have reduced populations in some regions.

The ostrich exemplifies extreme flightlessness, bipedal running specialization, communal breeding, and adaptation to arid and semi-arid environments. Its long history with humans — sacred reverence to commercial farming — illustrates deep connections between wildlife and human culture and economy.
