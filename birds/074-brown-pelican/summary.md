# Summary

The Brown Pelican (*Pelecanus occidentalis*) is a large coastal waterbird of the Americas, instantly recognizable for its enormous bill and throat pouch and for its dramatic plunge-diving hunt. Spotting fish from the air, the bird folds its wings and drops into the sea, using the impact to help stun prey before scooping water and fish into its expandable pouch, then draining the water and swallowing the catch. It is one of only a few pelican species that feeds this way; most others fish cooperatively from the surface. For ethologists, the brown pelican is valuable both as an example of a highly specialized foraging adaptation and as one of the clearest conservation case studies of the twentieth century.

Brown pelicans are gregarious birds that forage in loose flocks and breed in colonies on islands and sheltered coasts. Pairs are monogamous during the breeding season, and both parents share incubation of a clutch of usually two to three eggs and feed the young by regurgitation. Chicks hatch altricial and helpless, requiring weeks of parental care before fledging. Young birds refine the demanding skill of plunge-diving gradually, through repeated practice rather than from a single fixed program, and the precise, high-speed dive is buffered by specialized skull, neck, and air-sac structures that absorb the impact and protect the bird.

The species is best known for its near-collapse and recovery. During the mid-twentieth century, the pesticide DDT and its breakdown product DDE accumulated up the marine food chain and interfered with the birds' calcium metabolism, causing severe eggshell thinning. Eggs broke under the weight of incubating parents, breeding failed across large areas, and the brown pelican disappeared from parts of its former range, becoming a listed endangered species in the United States. After DDT was banned for agricultural use in the United States in 1972 and breeding habitat was protected, populations rebounded strongly, and the species was eventually removed from the U.S. endangered species list.

For ethology and conservation biology, the brown pelican teaches two connected lessons. First, behavioral specialization such as plunge-diving is powerful but comes with vulnerabilities, especially when a contaminant strikes at reproduction. Second, when a specific stressor is identified and removed, a resilient species can recover dramatically. The brown pelican thus stands as a hopeful emblem of environmental regulation working as intended, while remaining exposed to newer pressures such as oil spills, fishing-gear entanglement, and human disturbance at nesting colonies. Its global IUCN status is now Least Concern.
