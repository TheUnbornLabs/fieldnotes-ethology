# Book 091 — The House Sparrow: Summary

The house sparrow (*Passer domesticus*) is a small Old World sparrow (family Passeridae) whose life is bound to human settlement. Native to Eurasia and North Africa, it has been carried by people to the Americas, Australia, southern Africa, and beyond, making it one of the most widely distributed wild birds on Earth. It is a commensal of humans — a wild animal that exploits the grain, food waste, and nesting nooks that farms, villages, and cities provide — and it is scarce in landscapes without people. Globally it is assessed as Least Concern, yet many long-established urban populations, especially in parts of western Europe, have declined sharply in recent decades for debated and probably multiple reasons.

The house sparrow is a diurnal, largely sedentary bird that moves between a gregarious, flock-based life outside breeding and a more territorial, pair-based life during the breeding season. It feeds mainly on seeds and grain, foraging on the ground in flocks, but takes a flexible diet that includes insects and other invertebrates — food that is especially important for nestlings, whose growth depends on animal protein. This dependence on insect food links breeding success to invertebrate abundance near the nest, one of the hypotheses proposed for city declines.

Socially, house sparrows show dominance relationships, and in males the black throat "badge" has been much studied as a possible signal of dominance status — the "badge of status" hypothesis. This idea is influential but debated, and the book presents it as a well-investigated hypothesis rather than a settled fact. Communication relies on the familiar chirp, a range of alarm and threat calls, loud collective calling at communal roosts, and visual displays. The species is socially monogamous but engages in extra-pair matings, so social pairing does not always match genetic parentage. House sparrows are cavity and crevice nesters, and competition for the limited supply of good nest sites is a central feature of their breeding system. Both parents provision the altricial young, which fledge after roughly two weeks; pairs often raise several broods in a long season.

The house sparrow is behaviourally flexible, quick to exploit new foods and situations, and used as a model for studying boldness, problem-solving, and urban stress in wild birds — though claims of cleverness should stay proportionate, with no evidence of tool use or human-like reasoning. Its defences rest on vigilance, quick use of cover, and the safety of the flock.

The bird's chief ethological lessons are its commensal relationship with humans, the difficulty of confirming status-signalling hypotheses across populations, and the sober point that a globally abundant species can decline steeply at local scales — common does not mean safe.
