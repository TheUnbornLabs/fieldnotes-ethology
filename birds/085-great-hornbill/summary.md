# Summary — The Great Hornbill (*Buceros bicornis*)

The great hornbill, *Buceros bicornis*, is a very large, long-lived canopy bird of the family Bucerotidae, inhabiting wet and moist forests of the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, and Sumatra. It is predominantly frugivorous — figs are a staple — with some animal prey taken opportunistically, and it is topped by a massive bill bearing a large, mostly hollow casque. On the IUCN Red List it is assessed as Vulnerable, reflecting decline from habitat loss and hunting.

Two features make the species ethologically exceptional. The first is its breeding behavior. After courtship, the female enters a large tree cavity and, with the male's help, seals the entrance behind a plaster of mud, droppings, and food remains, leaving only a narrow slit. She is effectively walled in for weeks or months, laying and incubating a small clutch and often moulting her flight feathers while confined, while the male alone brings food through the slit. This must be described as a cooperative, evolved strategy — it protects the female and brood from predators and from competition for scarce cavities — not as the female's imprisonment. It is highly protective but concentrates all risk on the male's survival and provisioning.

The second feature is its ecological role. By swallowing large fruits whole and voiding intact seeds far from the parent tree, the great hornbill is a major seed disperser, especially of large-seeded rainforest trees that depend on big frugivores. This earns it the name "gardener of the forest," and it means the bird's decline threatens forest regeneration.

The casque, the bird's most conspicuous feature, is large but mostly hollow and light. On current evidence it functions mainly in display and possibly in sound resonance rather than as a weapon, but its precise function remains uncertain and should be stated as such. The loud whooshing sound of a passing hornbill is mechanical, made by air through the heavy wing feathers, not by the voice.

Great hornbills form strong, apparently long-term monogamous pair bonds and can be gregarious at fruiting trees and communal roosts outside breeding. They are long-lived and slow-breeding, raising very few young per year, which makes populations slow to recover. Compared with the unrelated but similar-looking toucans of the Americas — a case of convergent evolution — the hornbill shows both convergence in large-billed frugivory and its own distinctive innovations. Its lessons are about reproductive trade-offs, honest scientific uncertainty, ecological interdependence, and convergence.
