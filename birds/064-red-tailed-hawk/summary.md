# Summary — The Red-Tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*)

The red-tailed hawk is the most widespread and familiar large hawk in North America, and one of the clearest examples in raptor ethology of success through generalism. It belongs to the genus *Buteo* — the broad-winged, soaring "buzzard-hawks" — and its whole body plan is tuned not for speed but for cheap, sustained flight on rising air. That design suits an opportunist that scans wide areas of open ground for scattered prey.

Its range runs from Alaska and Canada through the United States and Mexico into Central America and the Caribbean, across nearly every open and semi-open habitat: prairie, desert, farmland, woodland edge, and, increasingly, suburbs and cities. The one constant requirement is a mix of open hunting ground and elevated perches or nest sites. The IUCN lists it as Least Concern, and it is one of North America's raptor success stories — adaptable enough to have benefited from human-altered landscapes.

The red-tail's hunting is built on flexibility. It uses perch-and-pounce (watching from a pole or tree and dropping onto prey), soaring on thermals to scan large areas, and kiting — hovering into a steady wind to hold position over one spot. Its diet tracks whatever is locally abundant: mainly small mammals such as voles, ground squirrels, and rabbits, plus reptiles, birds, and occasional carrion. Vision dominates its senses, with acuity several times sharper than a human's and possible ultraviolet sensitivity that may help locate rodent runways.

Socially, the red-tail is solitary outside the strong, often long-lasting monogamous pair bond. Pairs defend a territory, perform a dramatic aerial "sky-dance" courtship, build bulky stick nests in trees, on cliffs, or on buildings, and share role-divided parental care. Juveniles lack the famous brick-red tail — theirs is brown and banded, turning red only with maturity around the second year.

The species is deeply entangled with people. Its piercing *keeeer* scream is the sound Hollywood routinely dubs over eagles; it is the classic beginner's bird in falconry; and famous urban individuals such as New York's "Pale Male" show its synanthropic ability to live among us. Several myths surround it: the "eagle" cry is really this hawk; "chicken hawks" rarely take poultry or pets; hawks are not all alike (light and dark morphs and regional forms, including Harlan's hawk, differ markedly); and the red tail is not present at hatching. Set against the peregrine falcon — a narrow speed specialist — the red-tail illustrates the alternative strategy of the broad, patient generalist, a route that has made it the continent's most familiar hawk.
