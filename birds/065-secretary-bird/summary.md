# Summary — Book 065: The Secretary Bird (*Sagittarius serpentarius*)

The secretary bird is one of ethology's most instructive cases of a predator whose lifestyle diverges sharply from its ancestry. It is a genuine bird of prey — a member of the order Accipitriformes, kin to hawks, eagles, and Old World vultures — but it has committed almost fully to a terrestrial, cursorial life on the open grasslands and savannas of sub-Saharan Africa. Standing over a metre tall on long, crane-like legs, it hunts on foot, walking many kilometres a day and scanning the grass from a height its tall stance provides. It is the sole living member of its own family, Sagittariidae.

Its most famous behaviour is the kick strike. Rather than seizing prey with talons from the air, the secretary bird stomps its prey with rapid, powerful blows. Biomechanics research associated with Steve Portugal and colleagues, using captive birds striking a target, measured strikes delivering roughly five times the bird's body weight, with the foot in contact for only about fifteen milliseconds, landed with high accuracy. This combination of force, speed, and precision lets the bird dispatch dangerous prey — including venomous snakes — while lifting its foot clear before it can be bitten. Its long legs, tough scaled leg skin, and accurate timing manage the risk; there is no evidence it is simply venom-immune.

Despite its snake-killing reputation and its scientific name, the secretary bird is a dietary generalist. Its prey spans insects (especially grasshoppers and locusts), small mammals such as rodents and hares, lizards, amphibians, and birds' eggs and nestlings, as well as snakes. Insects and small mammals often make up the bulk of the diet.

The bird leads a double life. By day it walks and hunts on the ground; at night it roosts, and it nests, in the flat crowns of trees such as acacias, flying up each day. It flies and soars well, and mated pairs — which are monogamous and often long-bonded — perform both aerial and ground courtship displays. Communication is largely visual, using the erectable crest of quill-like head feathers (the debated source of the "secretary" name), posture, and display flights, supplemented by deep croaking calls near the nest.

The origin of the name "secretary bird" is genuinely uncertain and disputed; the popular quill-pen story is only one unproven explanation. What is not in dispute is the bird's conservation plight: it is now classified as Endangered on the IUCN Red List, uplisted as the grasslands it depends on are lost and degraded through agriculture, overgrazing, bush encroachment, and development. As a wide-ranging grassland specialist, its decline is a barometer of savanna health. The secretary bird's central ethological lesson is that behaviour, more than lineage, defines an animal's ecological niche.
