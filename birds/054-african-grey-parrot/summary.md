# Summary — The African Grey Parrot (*Psittacus erithacus*)

The African grey parrot is the animal that forced science to rethink what a bird's mind can do. Native to the lowland and moist forests of West and Central Africa, it is a medium-large grey parrot with a red tail, feeding on fruit, seeds, nuts, and mineral clay, and living for several decades. It is diurnal and intensely social, moving in noisy flocks built around strong, long-term pair bonds, and coordinating through a rich vocal repertoire that includes individually distinctive contact calls functioning somewhat like names.

The grey's fame rests on vocal learning — the ability to acquire and modify sounds through experience — and on what Irene Pepperberg's decades of work with the parrot Alex revealed could be built on top of it. In controlled tests, Alex did not merely echo speech. He used spoken English labels to identify the colour, shape, material, and number of objects, judged whether two things were the same or different, and used a word for the absence of a difference — a concept approaching a bird's version of "none" or zero. This is best described as trained referential communication: the meaningful, flexible use of learned labels within tested contexts. It is genuinely impressive, and it is bounded. Alex showed no open-ended grammar and no unlimited sentence-building; his was a world of labels, categories, and small numbers, not human language.

Behind these abilities is an unusual brain. Relative to body size, parrots have large brains, and their forebrains pack neurons at very high densities, giving a small grey a neuron count rivalling far larger-brained primates. That cognitive capacity shows up beyond the language work: greys are strong problem-solvers, and captive experiments associated with Auguste von Bayern and colleagues found that greys will voluntarily help a partner obtain food by handing over exchange tokens, a striking demonstration of prosociality in a bird.

The same traits that make the grey remarkable also make it vulnerable. A flocking, pair-bonding, long-lived, intelligent animal is poorly suited to a solitary life in a barren cage. Boredom, loneliness, and stress in captivity are linked to feather-plucking and other welfare problems, and greys are demanding companions requiring decades of enrichment and social contact — not easy, low-effort pets. Most seriously, heavy trapping for the international pet trade, alongside habitat loss, has driven steep declines in the wild. The African grey is now Endangered on the IUCN Red List and listed on CITES Appendix I, the strictest level of trade protection. In this bird, scientific wonder and conservation crisis are tightly bound: the intelligence that made it a landmark of animal-cognition research is also what made it a target. Understanding its behaviour is part of the case for protecting it.
