# Summary

The Great Cormorant (*Phalacrocorax carbo*) is a large, dark, fish-eating waterbird found across Europe, Asia, Africa, and Australia, in both coastal seas and inland lakes and rivers. It is a specialist underwater pursuit predator, diving from the surface and propelling itself mainly with its large webbed feet to chase and seize fish, which it swallows whole. Its wide distribution and its close, often contentious relationship with human fisheries make it a valuable subject for studying feeding specialization, adaptive trade-offs, and human-wildlife conflict.

The cormorant's most distinctive feature is its plumage. Unlike the fully waterproof feathers of many seabirds, cormorant body feathers are partly wettable: the outer portion absorbs water, reducing buoyancy and helping the bird submerge and stay down, while an inner layer retains some insulating air. This structure improves diving performance but has a cost, because waterlogged feathers must be dried. That is why cormorants are so often seen perched with wings held open in the characteristic spread-wing posture, a behavior that promotes drying and may also assist thermoregulation. The old shorthand that cormorants simply "lack waterproofing oil" is a simplification of this feather-structure adaptation. Their eyes are adapted for clear vision both above and below the surface, and, as birds, they detect prey underwater primarily by sight rather than by any fish-like lateral-line sense.

Great cormorants are highly social, breeding in colonies on cliffs, in trees, or on the ground, and often roosting communally. Pairs form during the breeding season, and both parents incubate a clutch of usually three to four eggs and feed the altricial young by regurgitation, with fledging after roughly eight to ten weeks. Young birds develop diving and foraging skill through practice.

The species is also woven into human culture and conflict. In East Asia, particularly Japan and China, people have practiced cormorant fishing for many centuries, training birds to dive and retrieve fish; the relationship is complex, involving learned behavior, habituation, and food rewards, and is better described in those terms than as simple cruelty or servitude. In Europe and North America, by contrast, cormorants are frequently persecuted as competitors with sport and commercial fisheries, prompting culling and nest control. Legally protected in some regions, the species has recovered and expanded in others, and its global IUCN status is Least Concern. For ethologists, the great cormorant illustrates a clear adaptive compromise between diving ability and waterproofing, and the recurring tension between an ecologically successful predator and human economic interests.
