# Summary — The Grey Crowned Crane (*Balearica regulorum*)

The grey crowned crane (*Balearica regulorum*) is a tall, grey-bodied African crane of the family Gruidae, crowned with a distinctive spray of golden feathers and marked with white and red facial patches. It lives in the wetlands, marshes, and grasslands of eastern and southern Africa, feeds omnivorously on seeds, insects, and small animals, and is long-lived, capable of surviving for several decades. It is the national bird of Uganda. Crucially, it is assessed by the IUCN as Endangered, having declined steeply through wetland loss and the capture of wild birds for the pet and ornamental trade.

The crowned cranes are the most basal, or ancient, of living cranes and retain the ancestral ability to perch in trees, which most other cranes have lost. The grey crowned crane's life is anchored on wetlands, which provide safe roosting and nesting sites, while surrounding grasslands and farmland provide feeding grounds; the bird's daily rhythm shuttles between the two. Outside the breeding season it is gregarious, forming flocks that feed and roost together, but during breeding, pairs withdraw to defend wetland nesting territories.

The species is famous for its dancing — coordinated bows, leaps, wing-spreads, and tossing of vegetation. Contrary to popular belief, dancing is not confined to courtship: birds dance throughout the year and at all ages, and it is thought to maintain the pair bond, coordinate partners, and relieve tension. Grey crowned cranes are largely monogamous, forming durable pair bonds. They produce a booming call with the help of an inflatable red throat sac, unlike the typical cranes whose trumpeting depends on a coiled windpipe. Their chicks are precocial — downy and mobile soon after hatching — but still receive prolonged care from both parents, reflecting the value of each chick to a slow-reproducing bird.

The grey crowned crane is a socially attentive, adaptable bird, but its slow life history makes it vulnerable: it cannot quickly replace losses from habitat destruction, capture, poisoning, and powerline collisions. Its natural defences of vigilance, flocking, and concealed wetland nesting protect it against predators but not against these human pressures. Its conservation depends on protecting and restoring wetlands, curbing the illegal trade in wild birds, and reducing hazards. The species teaches that a ritualised behavior like dancing can serve many social functions, that behavior can be tightly bound to habitat, and that visibility and cultural fame do not guarantee a species is secure.
