# Summary — The Superb Lyrebird (*Menura novaehollandiae*)

The superb lyrebird is a large, ground-dwelling Australian songbird and probably the most accomplished vocal mimic known. In the moist forests and fern gullies of southeastern Australia, males clear display mounds on the forest floor and, through the austral winter breeding season, sing long, complex songs while throwing an ornate tail forward over the body in a shimmering visual display. Woven into each male's own song are strikingly accurate imitations of many other bird species and other natural sounds. Females visit, assess the performance, and choose; mating success is strongly skewed toward preferred males. The system is polygynous, and males provide no parental care.

The lyrebird's fame rests on its mimicry, but that fame needs careful framing. Wild superb lyrebirds mimic mainly the sounds of their natural environment, chiefly other birds. The widely shared clips of lyrebirds imitating chainsaws, camera shutters, and car alarms are real but exceptional, and are largely associated with captive or human-habituated individuals; they should not be presented as ordinary wild behaviour. Mimicry is also learned, not innate: young males acquire and refine their repertoire from the sounds around them over several years, so song develops with age and can carry local traditions — a genuine case of cultural transmission and a valuable system for studying vocal learning.

After mating, the female alone builds a substantial domed nest, usually on or near the ground, lays a single egg, and incubates and raises the one altricial chick without help. This very low fecundity is offset by a long adult lifespan and repeated breeding. Away from display, the lyrebird is an insectivore that rakes powerfully through leaf litter and soil with strong legs and large feet, and in doing so turns over enormous quantities of material, acting as something of an ecosystem engineer that shapes the forest floor.

Ethologically, the superb lyrebird illustrates sexual selection through female choice on vocal and visual display; cultural transmission of learned song; and the importance of source-critical description, since its most famous behaviour has been badly distorted in popular media. Its genius is specifically vocal, and broader claims about its intelligence are not well supported. Assessed as Least Concern, it depends on moist forest and is threatened locally by fire and introduced predators.
