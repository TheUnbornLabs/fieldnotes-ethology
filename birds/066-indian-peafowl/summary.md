# Book 066 — The Indian Peafowl: Summary

The Indian peafowl (*Pavo cristatus*) is a classic example of sexual selection in birds, where the male's elaborate train is thought to have evolved through female mating preferences rather than for survival. Exactly how strongly, and by which cues, present-day peahens use the train in mate choice remains an active scientific debate — some studies report clear preferences for elaborate trains, while others find little effect — so the peafowl is best treated as a debated rather than settled case. Native to the Indian subcontinent and Sri Lanka, this large Phasianidae (pheasant family) member has thrived in human settlements for millennia, becoming revered in Hindu and Buddhist tradition and the national bird of India.

Males weigh 4–6 kg and females 3–4 kg. The males' iridescent plumage and especially the famous "train" — elongated tail coverts bearing enormous eyespots — are used in visual display or "fanning," where the male raises the coverts upward to create a vertical fan. This display is accompanied by loud two-syllable calls carrying over long distances and posturing that emphasizes the shimmering head and neck. The honesty-of-handicap hypothesis offers one explanation for the train's evolution: only males in excellent condition can afford to build, maintain, and carry such an expensive ornament while surviving and foraging, so it may signal quality. Alternative and complementary accounts include Fisherian runaway selection, and the relative importance of visual versus acoustic signals in choice is still under study.

Indian peafowl are diurnal ground foragers, feeding on seeds, insects, small reptiles, amphibians, and vegetation. Outside breeding season they gather in loose flocks; during breeding season males assemble in "leks" — gathering areas where they display competitively. Females visit leks to observe displays and choose mates based on display quality and male condition. This lek-mating, polygynous system produces no pair bonds; females raise chicks alone after mating once with chosen males.

Breeding concentrates in monsoon and post-monsoon periods (June–October in Asia) when food abounds. Females select nest sites and lay 4–8 eggs, incubating them alone for roughly 28 days. Upon hatching, chicks are precocial: covered with down and able to forage independently within hours. Mothers brood chicks at night, lead them to foraging areas by day, and provide vigilant protection but not direct food. Early mortality is high; surviving chicks remain with mothers until several months old.

Young birds learn foraging, predator avoidance, and social cues by observing mothers and flock members. Adult males learn display behaviors through observation and practice. The species shows significant behavioral flexibility and has adapted rapidly to life in human parks and temple grounds, becoming semi-tamed while retaining essential wild behaviors.

In the wild, peafowl are wary birds defended by size, alertness, speed, and group threat detection. Primary predators include raptors, carnivorous mammals, and snakes. Feral populations descended from escaped domesticated birds now thrive in Europe, North America, Australia, and Pacific islands, demonstrating the species' adaptability. The peafowl exemplifies how extreme sexual selection and human coexistence are not mutually exclusive, and its cultural history shows how a single species occupies both scientific and cultural attention.
