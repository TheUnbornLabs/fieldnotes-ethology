# Summary — Book 079: The Mallard

The Mallard (*Anas platyrhynchos*) is the archetypal dabbling duck and one of the most widespread birds in the Northern Hemisphere, the ancestor of most domestic ducks and an adaptable generalist that thrives from wild marshes to city parks. It is a medium-large member of the family Anatidae, strongly sexually dimorphic — the green-headed drake and mottled brown female — and listed by the IUCN as Least Concern, with a very large population, though its readiness to hybridise threatens some rarer ducks.

Its success is the success of a generalist. An omnivorous, opportunistic diet, tolerance of almost any shallow wetland and climate, and a readiness to exploit human environments have given it an enormous range. It feeds by dabbling and up-ending, filtering water and mud through bill lamellae and taking seeds, plants, grain, and aquatic invertebrates as season and opportunity allow.

The Mallard is best known to ethology as a case study in sexual selection and sexual conflict, which must be described as biology rather than through human moral categories. The drake's bright plumage reflects mate choice and male competition; the female's cryptic plumage suits her exposed role as the sole incubator. Pair bonds are seasonal and non-exclusive: extra-pair copulations are common, and some drakes force copulation on other females. This coercion has driven a famous evolutionary arms race — male ducks have an elongated, complex phallus, and females have evolved correspondingly complex tracts that give them influence over fertilisation. These are outcomes of selection, not moral failings.

The female alone selects a concealed ground nest, lays about eight to thirteen eggs, and incubates roughly four weeks; the drake usually leaves at the onset of incubation. Ducklings are highly precocial, feeding themselves within a day of hatching, led and guarded but not fed by the female, and imprinting on her during a sensitive period. Drakes moult into drab "eclipse" plumage during the flightless late-summer moult. The loud "quack" is mainly a female sound; the drakes' most elaborate signalling is the ritualised communal courtship display studied by early ethologists.

Learning matters: imprinting, learned feeding and roosting sites, migration routes, and, in urban birds, association of people with food. Defences rest on wariness, explosive flight, camouflage, nest concealment, and female distraction displays. Domesticated, hunted, cherished in parks, and studied for over a century, the Mallard also poses a conservation paradox — a hugely successful generalist that can genetically swamp rarer relatives. It teaches lessons about sexual selection, sexual conflict, behavioral flexibility, and the discipline of describing behavior objectively.
