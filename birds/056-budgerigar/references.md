# References / Further Reading — The Budgerigar

Sources are separated into those that are safe to name as written and those whose specific details must be verified before publication. No citations here are invented; where a study is real but its exact bibliographic details are not confirmed, it is described generally and flagged.

## Well-established, safe-to-name sources

- IUCN Red List — *Melopsittacus undulatus* (budgerigar) species assessment (Least Concern). A checkable primary source for conservation status, range, and population.
- Cornell Lab of Ornithology — *Birds of the World* / *All About Birds*, budgerigar species account. Reputable reference for identification, distribution, and general biology.
- Peer-reviewed literature on budgerigar vocal learning and call convergence — an established body of work showing open-ended vocal learning and the convergence of contact calls among mates and flockmates. *Further reading to verify specific studies before final publication.*
- The contagious-yawning study associated with A. C. Gallup and colleagues, reporting contagious yawning in budgerigars as the first such demonstration in a non-mammal. *Cite generally; verify exact authorship, title, journal, and year before final publication.*
- The mate-choice-by-competence study reporting that female budgerigars preferred males demonstrating problem-solving (puzzle-box) ability. *Cite generally; verify exact authorship, title, journal, and year before final publication.*
- Peer-reviewed literature on ultraviolet/fluorescent plumage signalling and tetrachromatic colour vision in budgerigars. *Further reading to verify specific studies before final publication.*

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

- Scientific name *Melopsittacus undulatus*, order Psittaciformes, family **Psittaculidae** (sole species in genus *Melopsittacus*) — confirmed (BirdLife DataZone; Animal Diversity Web; IOC). Family placement in Psittaculidae is current.
- IUCN Red List status **Least Concern** (very large range, population increasing/stable) — confirmed (BirdLife DataZone; IUCN).
- Endemic to mainland Australia; wild plumage green/yellow — confirmed (Animal Diversity Web; Bush Heritage Australia).
