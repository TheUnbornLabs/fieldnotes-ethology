# Summary — The Budgerigar

The budgerigar (*Melopsittacus undulatus*) is a small parrot of arid inland Australia and, in captivity, one of the most popular pet birds in the world. Weighing only about thirty grams and endemic to the semi-arid interior, it is a grass-seed specialist whose behaviour is built around the unpredictability of its environment. Rainfall in central Australia is patchy and irregular, so budgerigars do not hold fixed territories or follow set migratory routes. Instead they are nomadic and irruptive, moving to wherever recent rain has produced a flush of grass seed and gathering into flocks that can number in the tens of thousands, famous for their tightly synchronised flight.

Sociality runs through every part of budgerigar life. The birds forage on the ground for seeds, drink in coordinated flights to water, and roost together, and their communication is overwhelmingly vocal. Budgerigars are open-ended vocal learners: they modify their contact calls and their long, quiet warble song throughout life. Strikingly, the calls of mates and flockmates converge, so that social partners come to share call types — a learned acoustic signature that binds the flock together and shows their mimicry is structured, not random.

Several findings have made this little bird important to ethology. It was the first non-mammal experimentally shown to exhibit contagious yawning, extending a phenomenon once thought to be mammalian. Female mate choice has been shown to shift toward males that can solve a novel foraging puzzle, linking demonstrated competence to reproduction. And budgerigars see the world through tetrachromatic eyes sensitive to ultraviolet light, responding to fluorescent signals on the crown and cheeks that are invisible to humans but function as genuine mate-choice cues.

Reproduction matches the boom-and-bust ecology. Budgerigars are socially monogamous cavity nesters that breed opportunistically when rain triggers a seed flush, the female incubating an altricial brood while the male provisions the nest, so reproduction tracks resource pulses rather than a fixed calendar.

A persistent misconception is worth correcting: wild budgerigars are green and yellow. The blues, whites, greys, and violets seen in pet shops are captivity-derived colour morphs produced by selective breeding, not natural coloration. The wild population remains enormous, and the IUCN Red List classes the species as Least Concern. Taken together, the budgerigar shows how an unpredictable, arid environment can favour mobility, sociality, and lifelong vocal learning, and how much behavioural science can be packed into a very small bird.
