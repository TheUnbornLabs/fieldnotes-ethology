# Summary — The Common Raven (*Corvus corax*)

The Common Raven is the largest of the world's songbirds and one of the most cognitively studied animals outside the great apes. Entirely glossy black, with a heavy bill, shaggy throat feathers, a deep croaking call, and a wedge-shaped tail in flight, it ranges across almost the whole Northern Hemisphere and occupies habitats from Arctic tundra to desert to city edge. The IUCN Red List classifies it as Least Concern, reflecting a large and often expanding population.

The raven is a broadly omnivorous, opportunistic scavenger, and much of its remarkable behavior grows out of that lifestyle. Carrion is patchy and short-lived, so ravens rely on sharp memory, flexible problem-solving, and the ability to read both rivals and allies. When food is plentiful they cache surplus in hidden spots and retrieve it later. Because other ravens watch and pilfer these stores, cachers deploy tactical countermeasures — concealing food behind obstacles, delaying, or re-caching once an observer has left. Thomas Bugnyar and colleagues have documented such behavior, which implies some sensitivity to what a competitor can see, a capacity often discussed as perspective-taking, though its interpretation is debated.

Ravens also show apparent future planning: a study attributed to Kabadayi and Osvath reported that they can select and keep a tool or token for a reward available only later, forgoing an immediate smaller treat. In Bernd Heinrich's classic experiments, described in *Mind of the Raven*, ravens solved the meat-on-a-string problem in a coordinated sequence that suggested insight rather than trial and error. High neuron counts packed into the corvid forebrain, reported by Olkowicz and colleagues, offer a plausible physical basis for these feats. Throughout, the responsible position is to admire the raven's flexibility while treating the deeper cognitive claims as genuinely unsettled science.

Raven social life runs in two phases. Young, non-breeding birds are gregarious and nomadic, gathering at carcasses with food-recruitment calls and roosting communally, while bonded adults hold year-round territories and form long, cooperative pairs, courting through spectacular aerial tumbling. Ravens have a large learned vocal repertoire including mimicry, engage in rich object and social play such as sliding down snowbanks, and forage commensally alongside wolves. They have never been domesticated but thrive near people and loom large in human myth as omens, tricksters, and messengers. Those symbolic roles, however, are facts about human culture rather than raven biology. Taken together, the raven teaches that intelligence is not tied to brain size, that ecology and sociality jointly shape cognition, and that scientific humility must accompany even the most exciting findings.
