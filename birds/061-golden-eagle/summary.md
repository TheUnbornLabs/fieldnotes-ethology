# Summary — The Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)

The golden eagle is the most widely distributed eagle in the world, a powerful predator of open and mountainous country across North America, Europe, North Africa, and Asia. It is a "booted" eagle of the genus *Aquila*, with feathers running down its legs to the toes — an adaptation for gripping and killing live prey on the ground, in contrast to the fish-catching and scavenging sea-eagles. Large, dark brown, with a golden nape, it shows reversed sexual size dimorphism, the female being noticeably larger than the male.

Its behaviour is shaped by the challenge of finding scarce food across enormous areas. The eagle is diurnal and hunts largely by **soaring on thermals and updrafts**, turning the landscape's moving air into an energy-efficient search engine, then **stooping** onto prey in a fast, controlled dive. Typical prey includes hares, rabbits, marmots, ground squirrels, and ground birds such as grouse; the diet shifts with local abundance and includes carrion, especially in winter. Golden eagles also use low surprise attacks and, for larger or elusive prey, reports describe **coordinated pair hunting**. They occasionally take prey larger than themselves, and there are credible but exceptional reports of attacks on young ungulates. They cannot, however, lift heavy prey away, and feed on it where it falls.

Vision dominates the eagle's world. High photoreceptor density and **two foveae per eye** — a deep forward-aimed fovea and a shallower lateral one — give visual acuity estimated at several times a human's, letting a soaring bird spot prey at great range. Golden eagles are strongly **territorial**, holding large, defended territories for years, and form **long-term monogamous pairs**. Courtship centres on spectacular aerial displays, including the undulating **sky-dance** and talon-grappling cartwheels — displays that signal fitness and territory, not the mid-air mating they are sometimes mistaken for. Pairs build large stick **eyries** on cliffs or in tall trees, reused and enlarged over many years.

Breeding is marked by asynchronous hatching and frequent **siblicide (cainism)**, in which the older chick often kills its younger sibling — an evolved brood-reduction strategy rather than mere cruelty. Young mature slowly, first breeding at four to five years.

The golden eagle carries deep cultural significance, from falconry to the Kazakh eagle hunters and its role as a national and spiritual symbol. Globally it is **Least Concern**, but it faces real threats from persecution, **lead poisoning** from ammunition in carrion, and collisions with wind turbines and power lines. For ethology, it demonstrates how flight physics becomes foraging strategy, how body size ties to a slow life history, and how "brutal" behaviours can be adaptive.
