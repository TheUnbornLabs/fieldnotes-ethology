# Summary — The Griffon Vulture (*Gyps fulvus*)

The griffon vulture (*Gyps fulvus*) is a large, pale sandy-brown Old World vulture of the family Accipitridae (the hawks, eagles, and Old World vultures), with a wingspan of two and a half metres or more, a downy head and neck, and a distinctive neck ruff. It ranges across southern Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, and parts of Asia, in mountainous and open country with cliffs for nesting. It is an obligate scavenger, feeding almost entirely on the carcasses of medium-to-large mammals, and it is long-lived, capable of surviving for several decades. Globally it is assessed by the IUCN as Least Concern, but this status masks severe regional threats, above all from poisoning.

The griffon vulture is a specialist for finding and consuming scattered, unpredictable carrion. Its broad wings are built for soaring on thermals, letting it search vast areas for little energy, and its exceptional eyesight is the tool for spotting carcasses from great height. Crucially, and in contrast to some New World vultures that use smell, the griffon vulture finds its food overwhelmingly by sight — including by watching the movements of other vultures. This social information use, in which a descending bird draws in many others across a wide area, is central to how the species finds food and is a genuine cognitive achievement rather than mere instinct.

Griffon vultures are highly social: they roost communally on cliffs, nest colonially, and gather in competitive aggregations at carcasses, which they strip with remarkable speed. They are monogamous, form long-term pair bonds, and raise a single, slow-growing chick per breeding attempt, with delayed maturity — a slow life history that makes populations extremely vulnerable to added adult mortality. Their communication relies on hisses and grunts at nests and carcasses and, above all, on the visual signal of one another's flight.

By rapidly disposing of dead animals, the griffon vulture performs an invaluable sanitation service, and it is agent of cleanliness rather than a spreader of disease. Yet human activity is the overwhelming cause of vulture decline: illegal poison baits, lead from ammunition, and — devastatingly in related Asian Gyps vultures — the veterinary drug diclofenac have caused catastrophic losses, compounded by collisions with energy infrastructure. Because vultures gather at carcasses and breed slowly, poisoning is catastrophic. The griffon vulture teaches lessons about specialisation, social information use, convergent evolution with New World vultures, and the acute vulnerability of a slow-breeding scavenger to human chemicals.
