# Book 094 — The European Robin: Summary

The European robin (*Erithacus rubecula*) is a small, russet-breasted chat of the family Muscicapidae, widespread across Europe and into western Asia and North Africa. Despite its shared English name, it is not closely related to the American robin (*Turdus migratorius*), a thrush. It is largely insectivorous, common, and assessed as Least Concern. Beloved as a tame garden bird — famous for following gardeners to take worms from turned soil — it is in reality a fiercely territorial animal and a subject of landmark behavioural and sensory science.

The robin's most celebrated ethological finding concerns its red breast. Classic experiments associated with David Lack showed that a territorial robin will attack a mere tuft of red feathers, while a red-less juvenile-plumaged model provokes little aggression. The red breast is thus a sign stimulus (releaser) that triggers territorial aggression — a textbook illustration of how behavior can be organised around key signals. Robins are unusually territorial for a small songbird, defending individual feeding territories even in winter, and both sexes may sing across much of the year in defence of space. They can also recognise the songs of individual neighbours, allowing subtler, learned social responses.

The robin's second great scientific claim concerns orientation. Behavioural evidence, associated especially with Wolfgang and Roswitha Wiltschko, indicates that migratory robins use a magnetic compass, and that this compass is an "inclination compass" (responding to the field's angle rather than its polarity) and appears to be light-dependent, tied to the visual system. A leading hypothesis proposes light-sensitive molecules such as cryptochromes in the eye. The book presents this as strong, important evidence while flagging clearly that the underlying mechanism remains an area of active debate rather than a closed case.

Ecologically, the robin is a diurnal, low-light-active insectivore that hunts by perch-and-pounce and exploits disturbed ground, broadening its diet toward fruit and seeds in autumn and winter. It is seasonally monogamous; courtship involves the male shifting from aggression to tolerance and courtship feeding. The female builds the nest and incubates, and both parents feed the altricial young. Juveniles are spotted brown and lack the red breast — a behaviourally significant delay, since it spares them the aggression the red signal releases in adults.

The book corrects myths: that the two robins are the same bird, that the red breast is mere decoration, that robins are gentle toward each other, that only males sing in spring, that the magnetic compass is fully understood, and that juveniles have red breasts. Compared with the gregarious, flocking American robin, the European robin is solitary and individually territorial. Its ethology lessons concern sign stimuli, individual recognition, animal orientation, honest handling of scientific uncertainty, and the danger of inferring relationship from shared names.
