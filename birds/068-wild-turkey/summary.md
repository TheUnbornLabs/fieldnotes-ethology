# Book 068 — The Wild Turkey: Summary

The Wild Turkey (*Meleagris gallopavo*), a large North American ground bird, nearly disappeared from existence through overhunting but has undergone one of the most dramatic wildlife recoveries, now numbering over 4 million across North America. The species is famous for the male's elaborate display — fanned tail, iridescent plumage, and loud gobble — but equally fascinating is its complex social life: females with chicks form loose flocks with cooperative care; males form casual associations called "struts" and display competitively; and the species demonstrates sophisticated learning and behavioral flexibility.

Wild turkeys weigh up to 11 kg (males) and inhabit mixed deciduous and pine forest with open understory. They are diurnal ground foragers eating seeds, nuts, insects, and vegetation. They roost in trees at night for predator protection. Both sexes are relatively social; hens form flocks, sometimes providing alloparenting (care of chicks other than their own); gobblers form strut partnerships that may increase mating success through cooperative display.

Breeding occurs seasonally in late winter and spring. Males gobble to attract females; gobbles carry over a kilometer and serve both to advertise presence to females and to establish dominance among rival males. Females nest alone, incubating 8–15 eggs for 28 days. Poults are precocial and learn foraging locations and predator avoidance from mothers through example and calling. Early survival is challenging; mothers defend chicks aggressively, but predation, cold, and disease cause high poult mortality.

Young birds demonstrate significant learning abilities. They learn home range, food sources, and predator-avoidance behaviors through observation and experience. Gobblers learn to recognize dominant and subordinate males. Turkeys show cultural variation in vocalizations across regions, suggesting social transmission of behaviors.

Turkeys' near-extinction in the early 20th century and their remarkable recovery following habitat restoration and hunting management represents one of the most successful conservation achievements. Turkeys now thrive in suburban areas, forests, and mixed landscapes. They are wary but adaptable, shifting behavior in response to hunting pressure and predation. The species exemplifies how large wildlife can recover from near-extinction with proper management and how behavioral flexibility allows adaptation to human-modified landscapes.
