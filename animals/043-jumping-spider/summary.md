# Summary — Book 043: The Jumping Spider

Jumping spiders (family Salticidae) are the largest of all spider families, with more than 6,000 species found on every continent except Antarctica. They break nearly every assumption people hold about spiders. Rather than sitting in a web and waiting, they are active, daylight, visually guided hunters that stalk mobile prey and take it with a calculated leap — behaving far more like a cat than an ambush trapper. Silk is used only as a safety dragline and to build shelters and egg sacs, never as a prey-catching web.

Their defining feature is exceptional vision for such a small animal. Eight eyes are split into two systems. The large forward-facing principal (anterior median) eyes work like tiny telescopes: a fixed front lens focuses light down a long tube onto a small, layered telephoto retina, giving spatial acuity among the finest in the animal kingdom for the size, plus colour vision — ultraviolet in some species. Because each principal retina sees only a narrow strip at a time, muscles move the retinas to scan a scene. The secondary eyes are fixed, wide-field, motion detectors that give near-panoramic coverage and trigger the spider to turn and look. Jumping spiders also sense substrate-borne vibration through their legs, but sight rules their world. Their leaps are powered not by big muscles but by hydraulic leg extension — a rapid rise in internal blood pressure that straightens the legs explosively.

Two genera stand out. *Portia*, a spider-eating jumping spider of the Old World tropics, is a celebrated case of invertebrate cognition: it plans detour routes to reach prey it can no longer see, invades other spiders' webs and drums deceptive vibrations to lure the owner within reach (aggressive mimicry), and improvises by trial and error — all with a brain smaller than a grain of rice, apparently trading speed for capability. The Australian peacock spiders (*Maratus*) show the other great engine of behavioural evolution: sexual selection. Courting males raise a brilliant, sometimes ultraviolet-reflecting abdominal fan, wave ornamented legs, and drum coordinated vibratory songs into the ground, all judged by sharp-eyed, highly selective females.

Jumping spiders are solitary, diurnal, and — importantly — harmless to humans; their venom suits small insect prey, and their curious, head-turning manner has made them popular subjects for photographers and hobbyists. Compared with a web-building orb-weaver, which economises on eyes and brain and spends on silk and patience, the salticid economises on silk and spends on vision and cognition. For ethology, jumping spiders are a frontier: proof that a miniature nervous system can support genuine perception, planning, and flexible problem-solving.
