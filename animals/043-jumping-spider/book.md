# Book 043 — The Jumping Spider: Sharp Eyes and Courtship Dances

## 1. Opening Summary

Jumping spiders overturn nearly everything casual observers assume about spiders. They do not sit motionless in a web waiting for a meal to blunder in. Instead they stalk, they watch, they calculate, and they pounce — small, fuzzy, wide-eyed hunters that behave far more like a cat than a trapdoor ambusher. The family Salticidae is the largest of all spider families, with well over 6,000 described species, and it is defined above all by one extraordinary organ: a pair of large, forward-facing principal eyes that give these animals the sharpest spatial vision known in any animal their size. For ethology, jumping spiders are a landmark case. They demonstrate that a brain smaller than a grain of rice can support genuine visual perception, colour discrimination, route planning, and flexible problem-solving. The genus *Portia*, a group of spider-eating salticids from the Old World tropics, plans detour routes to reach prey it can no longer see and improvises attacks by trial and error — behaviour once assumed to require a far larger brain. Meanwhile the Australian peacock spiders, *Maratus*, perform some of the most elaborate courtship displays in the animal kingdom, combining vivid coloured ornaments with rhythmic body movements and songs drummed into the ground. Studying jumping spiders forces biologists to rethink how much cognition miniature nervous systems can deliver.

## 2. Scientific Identity

Jumping spiders are members of the family **Salticidae**, within the order Araneae (spiders) and class Arachnida. The family name comes from the Latin *saltus*, "a leap." With more than 6,000 species in over 600 genera, Salticidae account for roughly one in eight of all named spider species, making them the single most diverse spider family.

Two genera anchor this book. *Portia* comprises roughly twenty species of web-invading, spider-hunting salticids native to Africa, Asia, and Australasia. The genus *Maratus*, the peacock spiders, contains roughly 100 described species endemic to Australia, famous for the males' iridescent abdominal flaps.

Jumping spiders are found on every continent except Antarctica, from tropical rainforest and desert to temperate gardens, walls, and window frames; some species live above the tree line in the Himalayas. They are almost entirely predatory, feeding on insects and other small arthropods, with some specialists (like *Portia*) preying on other spiders and at least one species (*Bagheera kiplingi*) eating largely plant matter. Most jumping spiders are small — 2 to 20 millimetres — and short-lived, typically completing their life cycle within a year, though some survive longer. The family as a whole is not assessed as threatened, but Salticidae are so numerous and under-studied that most individual species have no formal conservation status.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

The salticid body plan is a study in how vision reshapes an animal. Most spiders detect prey through vibration and touch, using silk or bristles as their primary interface with the world. Jumping spiders took a different evolutionary path: they abandoned prey-catching webs and invested heavily in eyes and the neural machinery to use them. The pressure driving this was almost certainly the payoff of active, daylight, visually guided hunting — a diurnal predator that can identify, range, and stalk mobile prey has access to food that a stationary web-sitter cannot reach.

Vision this good is metabolically and neurologically expensive, and the salticid solution — a small number of very high-resolution eyes paired with several wide-angle motion detectors — reflects a trade-off between acuity and the space available in a tiny head. In *Portia* and its relatives, the same visual sophistication was turned toward hunting other spiders, an unusually dangerous prey that can hunt back. This selective pressure appears to have favoured not just sharp eyes but flexible, deliberative behaviour. In *Maratus*, sexual selection has driven a parallel arms race, elaborating male colour and movement under the scrutiny of highly visual, choosy females. Salticids therefore illustrate two great engines of behavioural evolution — predation and mate choice — both operating through the same exceptional visual system.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Jumping spiders are overwhelmingly **diurnal**, active in daylight when their vision works best; most retreat at night into small silk shelters. They are **solitary** for nearly all of their lives, coming together only to mate and, briefly, when a mother guards her eggs. They do not defend large territories in the way a vertebrate might, but individuals occupy and revisit familiar patches of habitat — a stretch of bark, a wall, a cluster of leaves — and use silk retreats as fixed refuges for resting, moulting, and overwintering.

A typical day is a sequence of short bursts of activity: scanning the surroundings from a vantage point, moving to a new perch, stalking prey, and periodically grooming the eyes and legs. Movement is deliberate and often stop-start, because the animal frequently pauses to look. Unlike web-builders, salticids are constantly on the move across surfaces, tethered at all times by a **dragline** of silk that they pay out behind them as a safety line. At night, in cold weather, and during the vulnerable hours of moulting, the silk retreat becomes home; some species overwinter communally in loose aggregations of separate silk cells, but this is proximity, not sociality.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

Vision defines the jumping spider. Salticids have eight eyes that fall into two functional systems. The large forward-facing pair — the **principal eyes**, also called the **anterior median (AM) eyes** — are the high-resolution instruments. Each is built like a tiny telescope: a fixed front lens focuses light down a long, narrow tube onto a small, layered **telephoto retina** at the back. That long eye tube gives a long focal length in a small body, producing spatial acuity that, per unit size, is among the finest in the animal kingdom — fine enough to resolve the detail of prey and rivals from several body lengths away.

Because each principal retina sees only a narrow strip of the world at once, the salticid solves the field-of-view problem with a remarkable trick: the retinas move. Muscles swing and rotate the **scanning retinas** behind the fixed lenses, sweeping the narrow high-acuity zone across a scene, so the spider can inspect an object in detail without turning its whole body. The principal eyes also support **colour vision**; many salticids have multiple photoreceptor types, and some, including peacock spiders, are sensitive into the **ultraviolet** and use UV-reflecting ornaments in courtship.

The remaining eyes are the **secondary eyes** — most importantly the large anterior lateral pair flanking the principal eyes, plus smaller side and rear eyes. These are fixed, wide-field, motion-sensitive detectors that together give nearly panoramic coverage; they catch movement and trigger a turn so the principal eyes can be aimed at whatever moved. Beyond vision, salticids sense **substrate-borne vibration** through slit sense organs in their legs — essential for courtship songs and for *Portia*'s manipulations — and detect airborne chemicals and touch through sensory hairs. But it is the eyes that make them exceptional.

## 6. Feeding Behaviour

Jumping spiders are **active visual hunters**. A salticid detects potential prey with its motion-sensitive secondary eyes, orients to bring the principal eyes to bear, inspects and identifies the target, then approaches in a slow, cat-like stalk, often pausing and crouching. When within range it launches a precise **leap**, seizing the prey with its fangs and injecting venom, while a **dragline** of silk anchored before the jump acts as a safety tether that arrests a fall or a miss. The spider does not build a web to catch food; silk here is purely a safety line.

The leap itself is powered not by large muscles but by **hydraulic leg extension**. Spiders have no extensor muscles in some leg joints; instead they rapidly raise internal blood (haemolymph) pressure to straighten the legs explosively, catapulting the body forward several times its own length with startling accuracy. Because the jump is visually guided, the spider judges distance in advance and adjusts its take-off.

Diet varies with species. Most take flies, small bugs, moths, and other arthropods. *Portia* is a specialist **araneophage** — a spider-eater — that invades the webs of other spiders and preys on them, including species larger and more venomous than itself, as well as hunting other jumping spiders. This dangerous diet is the backdrop for *Portia*'s celebrated intelligence.

## 7. Social Behaviour

Salticids are fundamentally solitary predators, and their default response to another jumping spider is caution, competition, or predation rather than cooperation. There are no true colonies, packs, or cooperative groups in the family in the sense seen in social insects or a few communal web-spiders. Encounters between individuals are governed mainly by sex and size: two rival males may face off in visual contests, squaring up and displaying at each other, with the smaller or less persistent one usually retreating before contact escalates to combat.

Where salticids aggregate, it is for practical reasons rather than sociality — for example, favourable overwintering sites where many individuals build separate silk retreats close together. Mothers guard their egg sacs and newly hatched young for a period, the closest thing to sustained social bonding in most species, but the spiderlings soon disperse to independent lives. This near-solitary lifestyle makes the sophistication of salticid signalling all the more striking: because interactions are brief and high-stakes — a potential mate, a rival, or a meal — the visual and vibratory communication that governs them has been honed to an unusual degree.

## 8. Communication

For an animal that meets its own kind only occasionally and briefly, the jumping spider has a rich communication repertoire, built on the same two channels it uses to hunt: **vision** and **substrate-borne vibration**. Visually, males (and in some species females) perform stereotyped **courtship displays** — waving and semaphoring the legs and pedipalps, raising and fanning ornamented body parts, and adopting postures that show off colour patches. Because salticids see colour, including ultraviolet in some, these displays are genuinely visual signals aimed at an eye capable of appreciating them, not incidental movements.

The second channel is vibration. By drumming, scraping, and tapping specialised body parts against the leaf or ground, a courting male sends **substrate-borne vibrations** — effectively songs — that the female detects through the slit sensilla in her legs. In peacock spiders these vibratory songs are structured, multi-part sequences precisely coordinated with the visual dance. The two channels reinforce each other: the visual display works over open ground and at a distance, while the vibratory song carries through the substrate and continues to inform the female even as the male manoeuvres. Beyond courtship, visual displays also mediate male-male contests, and *Portia* exploits the vibratory channel offensively (see Section 11), transmitting deceptive signals onto other spiders' webs.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

Jumping-spider courtship is among the most elaborate in the invertebrate world, and the **peacock spiders** (*Maratus*) are its showpiece. A male peacock spider carries a folded, brilliantly coloured flap on his abdomen. When he detects a female with his principal eyes, he raises the flap like a fan, unfurls it into a vivid disc of iridescent blues, reds, and yellows — some of which reflect ultraviolet — lifts and waves his third pair of legs, and dances from side to side, all while drumming a coordinated vibratory song into the ground. The female watches with her high-acuity eyes and assesses the performance. Mate choice is exacting: an unimpressive or mistimed display can end the courtship, and in some species a rejected or careless male risks being attacked and eaten by the female.

This intense **female choice** is the selective force behind the ornaments and choreography. Across salticids more broadly, males typically approach females with caution, using ritualised displays partly to court and partly to signal that they are suitors rather than prey. Rival males may also display at one another. After successful courtship and mating, the female stores sperm and later produces one or more silk **egg sacs**. Reproductive strategy centres on producing and guarding these clutches rather than on prolonged pair bonds; the male's contribution usually ends at mating.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

After mating, the female jumping spider lays her eggs in a silk **egg sac**, usually tucked inside a silk retreat under bark, a leaf, or a stone. In most species she stays with the clutch, guarding it against predators and parasites until the eggs hatch. The emerging **spiderlings** are miniature versions of the adult, already equipped with functioning eyes, and they typically remain in or near the natal retreat through their first moult before dispersing.

Development proceeds through a series of moults, during each of which the spider is soft and vulnerable and depends on its silk retreat for shelter. Young salticids must hunt for themselves almost immediately once independent; there is no extended feeding of offspring by the mother in the way mammals or birds provision young. Instead, the crucial inheritance is a visual system and hunting behaviour that work from the start. Even very young jumping spiders orient to prey with their principal eyes and make guided leaps, though accuracy and the more sophisticated behaviours improve with size and experience. Survival risks are high: spiderlings are prey for larger arthropods, other spiders (including *Portia* and even cannibalistic salticids), and small vertebrates, so clutches are large and mortality heavy.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

This is where jumping spiders become famous. The genus *Portia* is one of the most celebrated cases of invertebrate cognition, and much of what we know comes from decades of experiments by researchers studying its hunting of other spiders. *Portia* faces a hard problem: its prey are dangerous spiders, and a single misstep can be fatal. Its solutions look strikingly deliberate.

First, **detour route planning**. In laboratory tests, a *Portia* placed where it can see prey across a gap will scan the surroundings, then set off along a circuitous path — sometimes moving *away* from and losing sight of the prey — that leads to it by a route the spider evidently worked out visually in advance. Choosing the correct detour among alternatives, and committing to it even when the goal disappears from view, implies that the spider forms a plan from what it sees and then executes it. Second, **aggressive mimicry** on webs. When hunting a web-building spider, *Portia* plucks and vibrates the resident's web, generating **substrate-borne vibrations** that mimic a trapped insect, a courting male, or ambient disturbance — luring the owner within striking range. Crucially, *Portia* does not have a fixed signal: it varies the vibrations and, by **trial and error**, converges on whatever pattern draws that particular victim, effectively improvising a solution and remembering what works during an encounter.

These abilities are all the more remarkable given the size of the salticid brain. The trade-off appears to be speed: *Portia* is famously slow and deliberate, as if compensating for limited neural hardware with time and iterative visual analysis. More broadly, salticids show good spatial memory and adjust hunting tactics to prey type, making jumping spiders a key model for how much genuine cognition a miniature nervous system can support.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

For a small, protein-rich animal that moves about in the open by day, predator avoidance is constant. The jumping spider's first line of defence is the same panoramic vision it uses to hunt: the wide-field secondary eyes give near-total coverage and detect an approaching threat early, triggering a rapid turn, freeze, or escape. A startled salticid may simply leap away, and here the **dragline** is again vital — the silk safety line lets the spider drop off a surface to escape and then climb back, turning a fall into a controlled getaway.

When escape is not possible, salticids rely on crypsis and, in some species, mimicry. Many are camouflaged against bark or foliage; a number of species are striking **ant mimics**, matching the shape, colour, and even the walking gait of ants — a defence that exploits predators' avoidance of aggressive, chemically defended ants. Jumping spiders are venomous in the sense that all their prey capture depends on venom, but their venom is adapted to subduing small arthropods and poses no meaningful threat to humans. Faced with a rival or a threat it cannot flee, a salticid may perform a threat display, raising and spreading its front legs to look larger. Retreating into the silk shelter, especially during the defenceless moult, is the ultimate refuge.

## 13. Human Interaction

Jumping spiders are among the most human-tolerant of spiders and among the most liked. Their large, forward-facing principal eyes give them an appealing, almost expressive "face," and their curious, head-turning behaviour — a salticid will often swivel to watch a person, or track a finger — has made them popular subjects for photographers, nature enthusiasts, and a growing community of hobbyists who keep species such as the regal jumping spider (*Phidippus regius*) as small pets. They are common in and around human dwellings, hunting flies on walls, fences, and window frames, where they are harmless and mildly beneficial as pest controllers.

They are not domesticated, but they are increasingly studied and increasingly cherished. The discovery of the peacock spiders' spectacular displays, spread widely through high-speed macro video, has turned *Maratus* into ambassadors for invertebrate conservation and has driven both scientific and public interest. *Portia*'s problem-solving has made jumping spiders a touchstone in debates about animal minds. From a conservation standpoint the main concerns are habitat loss and the general decline of the insects salticids eat; because most species are tiny and unassessed, protecting the habitats they share with other invertebrates matters more than species-by-species measures. Culturally, jumping spiders are steadily shifting the public image of spiders from something to fear toward something to marvel at.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

```text
Myth 1: All spiders build webs to catch prey, and spiders are basically alike.
Scientific reality: Jumping spiders are active visual hunters that stalk and leap on prey. They use silk only as safety draglines and to build shelters and egg sacs — not as prey-catching webs. Spiders are enormously diverse in how they live and hunt.
```

```text
Myth 2: Spiders are mindless reflex machines that only react automatically.
Scientific reality: Portia, a spider-eating jumping spider, plans detour routes to reach prey it cannot currently see, improvises deceptive web vibrations by trial and error, and adjusts its tactics — evidence of planning and flexible problem-solving, not fixed reflexes.
```

```text
Myth 3: Jumping spiders are dangerous or medically venomous to people.
Scientific reality: Jumping spiders are harmless to humans. Their venom is tuned to subduing small insects; bites are rare, and any effect is minor and local. They are among the most human-tolerant spiders.
```

```text
Myth 4: Spiders have poor eyesight and sense the world only by touch and vibration.
Scientific reality: Jumping spiders have some of the sharpest vision known for their size. Their large principal eyes have a long telephoto retina that gives high spatial acuity and colour vision (ultraviolet in some species), and the retinas move to scan a scene.
```

```text
Myth 5: A bigger brain is required for any real intelligence, so a tiny spider cannot truly solve problems.
Scientific reality: With a brain smaller than a grain of rice, Portia demonstrates route planning and trial-and-error learning. It appears to trade speed for capability, solving problems slowly and deliberately — showing that miniature nervous systems can support genuine cognition.
```

```text
Myth 6: The peacock spider's dance is just random wiggling.
Scientific reality: Maratus courtship is a precisely structured performance combining a raised, ultraviolet-reflecting coloured fan, choreographed leg-waving, and coordinated substrate-borne vibratory songs, all assessed by highly selective, sharp-eyed females.
```

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

Set a jumping spider beside a classic **web-building orb-weaver** (family Araneidae), and the two embody opposite solutions to the same problem — catching mobile prey.

The orb-weaver is a **sit-and-wait trapper**. It invests its energy in silk, spinning a large, geometric web of sticky capture threads, then waits, often motionless, for prey to fly or crawl into the snare. Its world is largely vibratory: it detects a struggling insect by the plucking of the web's threads and homes in by touch. Its eyes are small and, in many orb-weavers, of poor resolution; vision is not its primary sense.

The jumping spider is the mirror image: an **active visual hunter**. It builds no capture web, investing instead in eyes and neural processing. It finds prey by seeing it, stalks it across surfaces, and takes it with a calculated leap, using silk only as a safety dragline. Its world is dominated by high-acuity colour vision; vibration serves communication and, in *Portia*, deception rather than prey detection.

The contrast is instructive: the orb-weaver economises on brain and eyes and spends on silk and patience; the salticid economises on silk and spends on vision and cognition. *Portia* even bridges the two worlds — a salticid that invades orb-weavers' and other spiders' webs, turning the trapper's own vibratory channel against it. The same ecological problem is solved by radically different behavioural and sensory investments.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

Jumping spiders teach several durable lessons. First, **sensory systems shape behaviour**: the entire salticid lifestyle — diurnal, stalking, visually communicating — follows from the evolution of the principal eyes. Change the dominant sense, and you change the animal. Second, **intelligence is not a simple function of brain size.** *Portia* shows that route planning and trial-and-error problem-solving can emerge in a nervous system smaller than a grain of rice, apparently by trading speed for depth of analysis. This reframes questions about which animals "have minds" and warns against equating small brains with mere reflex.

Third, salticids illustrate the twin engines of behavioural evolution working through a single system: **predation** drove the eyes and *Portia*'s hunting cognition, while **sexual selection** drove the peacock spiders' extravagant, multi-channel courtship — both exploiting the same visual sophistication. Fourth, they show how **multimodal signalling** — combining vision and substrate vibration — lets a small animal communicate reliably across different conditions and distances. Finally, jumping spiders are a reminder that the familiar and the overlooked can be scientifically profound: an animal common on garden walls turns out to be a frontier for the study of perception and cognition. No moralising is needed; the lesson is simply how much behavioural complexity can be packed into a very small package.

## 17. Glossary

- **Salticidae** — the jumping-spider family; the largest spider family, defined by acute vision and active hunting.
- **Principal (anterior median) eyes** — the large, forward-facing pair of high-resolution eyes that provide the jumping spider's sharp, detailed vision.
- **Secondary eyes** — the fixed, wide-field, motion-sensitive eyes (lateral and rear) that give near-panoramic coverage and trigger orienting.
- **Telephoto retina** — the long, layered retina at the back of the principal eye's narrow tube, giving high spatial acuity in a small body.
- **Scanning retina** — a principal-eye retina moved by muscles to sweep its narrow high-acuity zone across a scene.
- **Dragline silk** — the safety line a jumping spider trails behind it, anchoring leaps and enabling controlled falls; not used to catch prey.
- **Active hunter** — a predator that finds, stalks, and attacks mobile prey rather than trapping it in a web.
- **Detour (route) planning** — working out a roundabout path to prey in advance from visual information, then following it even when the prey is out of sight.
- **Aggressive mimicry** — producing deceptive signals (here, web vibrations) that lure prey within reach, as *Portia* does on other spiders' webs.
- **Substrate-borne vibration** — signals sent through a leaf or the ground and sensed via leg organs; used in courtship songs and in *Portia*'s deception.
- **Hydraulic leg extension** — straightening the legs by raising internal blood (haemolymph) pressure, the mechanism that powers the jump.
- **Palps (pedipalps)** — the small paired appendages near the mouth; used in courtship display and, in males, in sperm transfer.
- **Courtship display** — stereotyped visual and vibratory performance used to attract and be assessed by a mate.
- **Araneophage** — a predator that eats other spiders, as *Portia* does.
- **Ant mimicry** — resembling an ant in shape, colour, and movement as a defence against predators.
- **Ultraviolet (UV) vision** — sensitivity to ultraviolet light, used by some salticids to see UV-reflecting courtship ornaments.
