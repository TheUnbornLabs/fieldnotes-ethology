# Summary — The African Wild Dog (*Lycaon pictus*)

The African wild dog is one of the most cooperative mammals known to science and one of the most endangered carnivores in Africa. It is not a feral domestic dog, a jackal, or a hyena, but a distinct species and the sole living member of its genus, *Lycaon*. The species name *pictus* — "painted" — refers to the mottled black, tan, white, and yellow coat that is unique to each individual. Wild dogs are lean, long-legged, large-eared canids of sub-Saharan savanna and open woodland, and they are cursorial hunters: they run prey down over distance rather than ambushing it.

Cooperation defines nearly everything about the species. A wild dog is an obligate cooperative hunter — it cannot succeed alone. Packs course after medium-sized antelope, exploiting endurance and teamwork to achieve hunting success rates that regularly exceed those of lions. Because they share their range with lions and spotted hyenas that steal kills, wild dogs kill and eat quickly, an adaptation once misread as cruelty. After a kill they share food generously, regurgitating meat to feed pups, den guards, nursing females, and injured or aged packmates, and often letting pups eat first.

Wild dog society is unusually cohesive and low in aggression. A pack centres on a dominant breeding pair supported by related non-breeding adults. Reproduction is strongly skewed: normally only the dominant pair breeds, and the whole pack helps raise their single, often very large litter through allo-parenting. This pooling of effort is a hallmark of the species. Field researchers (Walker and colleagues) have also documented a remarkable "sneezing" behaviour during pre-hunt rallies that appears to function like a quorum vote — the pack is more likely to set off once enough sneezes accumulate, though rank can lower the threshold needed.

The wild dog's extreme sociality is also its vulnerability. Packs need very large ranges and occur at naturally low densities, so habitat fragmentation hits them hard. Wire snares, road collisions, and diseases spread from domestic dogs — especially rabies and canine distemper — can destroy entire packs, and small, fragmented populations recover poorly. Below a threshold pack size, hunting, den-guarding, and pup-rearing all falter. The IUCN Red List assesses *Lycaon pictus* as Endangered.

For ethology, the wild dog shows how far cooperation can be taken — in hunting, food-sharing, reproductive skew, and even collective decision-making — while also illustrating the fragility that comes with depending so completely on the group. Its history is a lesson in the real cost of persecuting an animal that was long misunderstood.
