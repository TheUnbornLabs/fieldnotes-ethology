# Summary — The Wolf (*Canis lupus*)

The gray wolf, *Canis lupus*, is the largest wild member of the dog family and the ancestor of the domestic dog. It is a habitat generalist that once ranged across most of the Northern Hemisphere and still occupies parts of North America, Europe, and Asia, though its range is now fragmented. Wolves are obligate carnivores whose lives centre on hunting large hoofed prey such as deer, elk, and moose. Globally the species is assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, but this masks regional endangerment, local extirpations, and intense conflict with people.

The wolf is a cornerstone species in ethology because it makes cooperation visible. A wild wolf pack is usually a family: a breeding pair and their offspring of one or more years, who hunt together, defend a territory, and raise pups cooperatively. This modern understanding replaced the older "alpha wolf" model, which came largely from studies of captive, unrelated wolves forced together in enclosures. That rigid dominance picture was popularised and then revised — notably by biologist L. David Mech, who publicly reconsidered the "alpha" terminology and showed that wild packs are family units led by a breeding pair, not ladders of rivals fighting for rank.

Wolves perceive their world mainly through smell and hearing, with vision playing a supporting role in low light. They are crepuscular but flexible, most active at dawn and dusk. They communicate across three ranges: howling for long-distance contact and territorial advertisement, scent-marking for a persistent chemical map of territory and identity, and body language for close encounters. Hunting is genuinely cooperative — wolves benefit from working together and often target vulnerable prey — though there is no firm evidence that they consciously assign roles or plan like a human team.

Reproduction is organised around the pair bond. Wolves usually breed once a year, and in a typical pack only the breeding pair reproduces while offspring disperse to form new packs elsewhere. Pups are born helpless in a den and raised cooperatively through allo-parenting, with the breeding male and older offspring helping to feed and guard them; the pack later moves them to above-ground rendezvous sites.

Wolves also anchor discussions of predator ecology and trophic cascades, as seen in Yellowstone, where their return influenced prey and vegetation — a real effect whose magnitude scientists still debate, since climate and other species acted at the same time. Comparing wolves with domestic dogs shows how quickly behavior can evolve: dogs seek human contact and read human cues more readily than wolves do. The wolf's enduring lesson is about evidence, cooperation, and ecological connection — and about how good science corrects its own mistakes.
