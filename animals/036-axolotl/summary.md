# Summary — The Axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*)

The axolotl is a salamander — an amphibian, not a fish — that matters to biology less for its behavior than for its extraordinary developmental biology. Native only to the high-altitude lake system of the Valley of Mexico, it now survives in the wild solely in the remnant canals and wetlands of Xochimilco near Mexico City, where it is listed as Critically Endangered.

Its defining trait is neoteny, or paedomorphosis: the axolotl keeps its larval body plan — feathery external gills and a finned tail — and stays fully aquatic even as it reaches sexual maturity. Most salamanders metamorphose into land-living adults, but the axolotl does not, because the thyroid-hormone surge that normally triggers transformation does not occur under natural conditions. The tissues remain capable of responding, and metamorphosis can be forced artificially with thyroid hormone or iodine, but this is not part of the animal's natural life.

The axolotl is equally famous for regeneration. It can regrow lost limbs, tail, jaw, spinal cord, and parts of the heart and brain, rebuilding structures from a wound-site mass of cells called a blastema. This has made it one of the most important biomedical research models in the world, studied for clues about tissue repair. Regeneration is not immortality, however: axolotls still age, sicken, and die, typically within about 10 to 15 years.

Behaviorally the axolotl is comparatively simple and largely solitary. It is a bottom-dwelling, crepuscular-to-nocturnal sit-and-wait suction feeder that pulls in nearby prey by rapidly expanding its mouth and throat. With poor eyesight, it relies on its lateral line to sense water movement, on electroreception to detect the electric fields of living prey, and on chemical senses. Its social life is minimal. Courtship, its most structured behavior, involves the male releasing chemical cues, nudging and performing a swaying "hula," and leading the female in a walk over deposited spermatophores, which she takes up for internal fertilization. There is no parental care; females attach hundreds of eggs to plants and leave.

The axolotl embodies a stark conservation paradox: millions live in laboratories, aquariums, and homes, while the wild population — the source of that lineage — is tiny and still declining under pollution, urbanization, habitat loss, and introduced predatory fish. The pale pink and white animals familiar from pet shops are leucistic and albino captive morphs; wild axolotls are dark and mottled. Woven into Aztec belief through the god Xolotl, who was said to have become an axolotl to escape sacrifice, the species remains a symbol of Mexican identity and a reminder that abundance in captivity cannot substitute for a protected wild home.
