# Glossary — The Axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*)

- **Neoteny / Paedomorphosis** — retention of juvenile or larval features into sexual maturity; in the axolotl, keeping the aquatic, gilled larval form for life.
- **Metamorphosis** — the developmental transformation from larva to adult form; largely absent in the axolotl under natural conditions, though it can be induced artificially.
- **External gills** — the feathery, blood-rich gill structures on the sides of the head, used to extract oxygen directly from water.
- **Regeneration** — the regrowth of lost or damaged body parts, including limbs, tail, jaw, spinal cord, and portions of heart and brain tissue.
- **Blastema** — the mass of unspecialized, dividing cells that forms at a wound site and rebuilds the missing structure during regeneration.
- **Leucistic** — a pale coloration caused by reduced pigment (with dark eyes); the source of the common pink-white axolotl morph, which is a captive variant rather than the wild color.
- **Spermatophore** — a jelly packet capped with sperm that the male deposits on the substrate and the female takes up with her cloaca for internal fertilization.
- **Lateral line** — a system of mechanoreceptive organs along the head and body that detects water movement and pressure changes, functioning as "distant touch."
- **Electroreception** — the ability to detect weak electric fields produced by living prey, useful in murky water and darkness.
- **Suction feeding** — capturing prey by rapidly opening and expanding the mouth and throat to pull in water and prey.
- **Larval form** — the immature, aquatic, gilled body plan that the axolotl retains throughout its life.
- **Critically Endangered** — the IUCN Red List category indicating an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild; the axolotl's current status.
- **Paedomorphic (obligate)** — describing an animal that remains in the larval form as a fixed natural condition rather than as a facultative response to environment.
- **Endemic** — native to and found naturally only in one restricted area; the axolotl is endemic to the Valley of Mexico.
- **Thyroid hormone** — the hormone that drives salamander metamorphosis; its natural surge does not occur in the axolotl, but supplying it (or iodine) can force metamorphosis.
