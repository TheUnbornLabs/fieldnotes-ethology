# Summary — The American Lobster

The American lobster (*Homarus americanus*) is a large, clawed marine crustacean of the cold northwest Atlantic and one of ethology's clearest models for how animals build and, remarkably, remember a social order. It is a benthic, mostly nocturnal scavenger-predator that shelters in rocky crevices and burrows by day and forages the seafloor at night, eating crabs, mussels, clams, urchins, worms, small fish, carrion, and sometimes other lobsters. Its wild coloration is dark and mottled, not red; the familiar red appears only when cooking frees a pigment normally masked in the shell.

The lobster's defining ethological feature is its dominance system. When two similar lobsters meet, usually over shelter, they settle the dispute through an escalating, ritualized contest — assessment, threat postures such as the meral spread, pushing, and only sometimes a claw lock — until one yields. The winner becomes dominant and the loser subordinate. Crucially, the pair then does not need to re-fight: the subordinate recognizes the specific individual that beat it and defers. This chemical individual recognition, developed in work associated with Jelle Atema and Thomas Breithaupt, is mediated by urine. Lobsters release urine from nephropores below the eyes and, using gill-driven water currents, aim it at rivals and mates; the urine carries identity, sex, and status. Contests also produce lasting winner and loser effects, and an animal's tendency toward aggression or submission is linked to serotonin, octopamine, and posture.

The lobster perceives its dark world chiefly through chemistry. Its antennules bear dense aesthetasc hairs for distance "smell," letting it track odor plumes to food and read other lobsters, while its two unequal claws — a heavy crusher and a slender cutter — handle prey. Growth is indeterminate: the animal increases in size only by molting (ecdysis), staying briefly soft and vulnerable each time, and it can live for decades. Reproduction is bound to this cycle. A female molts to mate in the protected den of a dominant male, using pheromonal urine signals to lower his aggression; she later carries fertilized eggs under her tail for months before releasing planktonic larvae, which eventually settle to the bottom.

The lobster is also the center of a serious modern welfare debate. It has no vocal apparatus, so the "scream" heard during boiling is escaping gas, not a voice — but whether decapods feel pain remains a real scientific question, argued in work associated with Robert Elwood, and some jurisdictions now recognize these animals as sentient. Far from a mindless automaton, the lobster shows that individual recognition, ritualized conflict, and chemically managed social life can evolve in an armored invertebrate with a simple nervous system.
