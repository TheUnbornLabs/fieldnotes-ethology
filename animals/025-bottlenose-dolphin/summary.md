# Book 025 — The Bottlenose Dolphin: Summary

The common bottlenose dolphin (*Tursiops truncatus*) is a toothed whale — a cetacean, not a fish — found in temperate and tropical waters worldwide, from coastal bays to the open ocean. It is one of the most socially and cognitively complex mammals in the sea, and studying it has reshaped how ethologists think about animal society, communication, and culture.

Bottlenose dolphins live in fluid "fission-fusion" societies, in which the membership of a group changes from hour to hour. Within this flux, some individuals maintain relationships for years. In Shark Bay, Western Australia, decades of research by Richard Connor, Michael Krützen, and colleagues have documented nested, multi-level male alliances: pairs and trios cooperate to consort with females, these join into larger alliances that compete with rivals, and those may associate more broadly still. Coordinating relationships across such layers is cognitively demanding and has few parallels outside humans and some primates. Female social life centres on kin-influenced networks and the long, intensive raising of calves.

The species perceives its world chiefly through sound. Its signature capacity is echolocation, or biosonar: the dolphin emits rapid click trains, focuses them through the fatty melon in its forehead, and interprets the echoes to locate and characterise prey and objects in dark or murky water. This is a physical process, not a "sixth sense." Dolphins also carry signature whistles — individually distinctive, learned whistles that function much like names. Work by Vincent Janik, Stephanie King, and colleagues shows that dolphins can even copy a companion's signature whistle, apparently to address that specific individual — though this is individual vocal labelling, not a human-style grammar.

Foraging is diverse and often cooperative, and much of it is culturally transmitted rather than innate. Traditions include sponge tool-use in Shark Bay (passed mainly from mother to daughter), mud-ring feeding, and strand-feeding. Dolphins are strong vocal and social learners, and a study by Diana Reiss and Lori Marino reported mirror self-recognition, consistent with self-awareness. Such intelligence claims should stay proportionate: dolphins are extraordinary but do not possess telepathy or a full language.

The popular image of the uniformly friendly, ever-smiling dolphin is misleading. The "smile" is fixed jaw anatomy, not emotion, and dolphins also show aggression, infanticide, and coercive mating. Their closeness to humans brings both affection and harm: fisheries bycatch, pollution, noise, and the ethics of captivity — where cognition-based welfare arguments carry particular weight — are central conservation and ethical concerns. Slow reproduction, with a single calf every few years, means populations recover slowly from losses.
