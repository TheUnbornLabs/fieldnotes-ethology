# Book 025 — The Bottlenose Dolphin: Glossary

- **Cetacean** — a fully aquatic marine mammal of the group that includes whales, dolphins, and porpoises; bottlenose dolphins are cetaceans, not fish.
- **Odontocete** — a toothed whale; the suborder of cetaceans, including dolphins, that echolocate.
- **Fission-fusion society** — a social system in which group composition changes frequently as individuals split from and merge with others.
- **Nested (multi-level) alliance** — cooperative male partnerships that are themselves grouped into larger alliances, as documented in Shark Bay dolphins.
- **Signature whistle** — an individually distinctive, learned whistle that broadcasts a dolphin's identity and can be copied by others to address that individual.
- **Echolocation (biosonar)** — sensing by sound: emitting clicks and interpreting the returning echoes to locate and characterise objects and prey.
- **Melon** — the fatty structure in a dolphin's forehead that focuses and projects echolocation clicks.
- **Vocal learning** — the ability to acquire and modify sounds through learning rather than producing only innate calls.
- **Cultural transmission** — the passing of learned behaviours between individuals and across generations by social learning rather than by genes.
- **Sponging (tool use)** — the Shark Bay tradition in which dolphins carry marine sponges on their beaks to protect them while foraging on the seafloor.
- **Cooperative foraging** — hunting techniques in which dolphins coordinate to capture prey, such as herding fish, strand-feeding, or mud-ring feeding.
- **Mirror self-recognition** — the capacity to recognise oneself in a mirror, reported in bottlenose dolphins as evidence consistent with self-awareness.
- **Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep** — resting one brain hemisphere at a time, allowing the dolphin to keep breathing and stay partly alert.
- **Bycatch** — the incidental capture of non-target animals, such as dolphins, in fishing gear; a major cause of dolphin mortality.
- **Ecotype** — a population adapted to a particular habitat (e.g. inshore versus offshore) that differs behaviourally and genetically from others of its species.
