# Summary — Ants (family Formicidae)

Ants are a family of more than 14,000 species of eusocial insects that live, almost everywhere on land, in colonies organised around a reproductive division of labour. One or a few queens lay the eggs, while large numbers of sterile female workers forage, build, care for the brood, and defend the nest. Because reproduction and maintenance are split this way, biologists treat the colony as a **superorganism** — a single functioning unit in which the queen resembles a germline and the workers the body. This framing reshapes how we read worker "self-sacrifice": a sterile worker is less a failed individual than a specialised cell of a larger organism, and its evolution is illuminated by **kin selection** and the unusual **haplodiploid** genetics of ants and their relatives.

What makes ants a landmark case in ethology is that their impressive, intelligent-looking behaviour has no central controller. There is no manager and no planning queen. Instead, coordination **self-organises** from many individuals following simple local rules with feedback. Ants live in a **chemical world**, sensing it largely through their antennae. They lay **pheromone trails** to food; successful routes are reinforced and amplified while unused ones evaporate, which lets a colony converge on the shortest path to a food source without any ant ever comparing distances. This principle inspired **Ant Colony Optimization**, a real family of computer algorithms for routing problems. Ants also coordinate through **stigmergy** — communication by modifying the shared environment — which underlies nest building and trail networks, and they recognise nestmates by the waxy **cuticular hydrocarbons** that carry each colony's scent. Food moves through the colony by **trophallaxis**, mouth-to-mouth sharing.

The family's lifeways are extraordinarily varied. Leafcutter ants (*Atta*) farm fungus on cut leaves; army ants (*Eciton*) conduct swarm raids and bivouac in shelters made of their own bodies; weaver ants (*Oecophylla*) sew leaf nests using silk squeezed from their larvae; harvester ants store seeds; many ants herd and "milk" aphids for honeydew (**trophobiosis**); and slave-making ants raid other colonies. Many species show **worker polymorphism**, with small **minors** and large-headed **majors** or soldiers suited to different jobs.

Several popular beliefs are wrong. Not every ant toils constantly — colonies keep large reserves of inactive workers. The queen does not rule or direct anything. No individual ant is truly clever; the intelligence is a property of the colony as a system. And most ants are harmless to people, with painful stingers like fire ants being the exception. For ethology, ants are the clearest natural demonstration that complex, adaptive, seemingly purposeful behaviour can emerge from simple parts — a lesson that reaches from evolutionary biology into computer science and robotics.
