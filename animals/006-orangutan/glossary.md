# Glossary — Book 006: The Orangutan

**Arboreal** — Living mainly in trees; orangutans are the most arboreal of the great apes.

**Semi-solitary** — A social pattern in which adults live mostly alone within a network of familiar, range-overlapping neighbours rather than in a bonded group.

**Bimaturism** — The occurrence of two adult male forms — flanged and unflanged — with different appearances and mating strategies.

**Flanged male** — A mature male with wide cheek pads (flanges), a throat sac, long hair, and large size; typically dominant and long-calling.

**Unflanged male** — A fully fertile adult male that has not developed flanges, remaining smaller and more female-like while pursuing an opportunistic mating strategy.

**Long call** — The loud, booming vocalisation of a flanged male that carries far through forest, spacing rivals and advertising to females.

**Kiss-squeak** — A sharp sound made by drawing air through pursed lips, often given when disturbed and sometimes modified with leaves or a hand.

**Interbirth interval** — The time between one birth and the next; in orangutans the longest of any mammal, about six to eight years.

**Mast fruiting** — Irregular, community-wide production of large fruit crops by forest trees, separated by long lean periods.

**Frugivore** — An animal whose diet is based mainly on fruit; orangutans are specialist frugivores.

**Fallback food** — Lower-quality food (leaves, bark, pith, insects) eaten when preferred fruit is scarce.

**Nest-building** — The daily construction of a sleeping platform of bent and woven branches, made fresh each night.

**Cultural transmission** — The spread of learned behaviours (such as tool techniques) through a population by social learning rather than genetics.

**Dispersed community** — A loose group of individuals with overlapping home ranges who know one another but rarely travel together.

**Life history** — The pattern of an animal's growth, reproduction, and lifespan; orangutans have a notably slow life history.

**Quadrumanous locomotion** — Slow, deliberate climbing using all four limbs as grasping "hands" to move through the canopy.
