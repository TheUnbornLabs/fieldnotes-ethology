# Summary — Book 026: The Orca

The orca, or killer whale (*Orcinus orca*), is the largest member of the dolphin family, Delphinidae, and a global apex predator found in every ocean from the poles to the tropics. Despite the name "killer whale," it is a dolphin — the biggest one there is — and there are no confirmed records of a wild orca killing a human. Its true significance in ethology lies not in its power but in its social and cultural sophistication.

Orcas are divided into distinct populations, or ecotypes, that differ in diet, body form, dialect, and behaviour. In the North Pacific, fish-eating "residents" specialise on salmon and live in large, stable pods; mammal-eating "Bigg's" (transient) orcas hunt seals, sea lions, and dolphins in smaller, stealthier groups; and "offshore" orcas appear to specialise partly on sharks. These populations can share the same waters yet rarely interbreed, and their differences are largely learned rather than genetic. Because they remain distinct despite overlapping ranges, biologists increasingly regard the ecotypes as a case of incipient speciation — a striking example of culture potentially shaping evolution.

Orca society is among the most stable known in any mammal. Its core unit is the matriline: a mother and her descendants, who in many populations travel together for life. Related matrilines form pods, and pods that share a common dialect and ancestry form clans. Each matriline uses a learned, family-specific set of pulsed calls — a vocal dialect, transmitted like a spoken tradition. Female orcas undergo menopause and may live for decades afterward, and research indicates these older females act as ecological knowledge-keepers, leading their families to salmon during lean years and improving their descendants' survival — the "grandmother effect."

Orca hunting is specialised and learned. Different populations perform distinct cooperative techniques: wave-washing seals off Antarctic ice floes, carousel-feeding on herring off Norway, intentionally beaching to seize sea lion pups at Península Valdés, and coordinating attacks on much larger whales. Young orcas acquire these skills over years of practice, making orca feeding a leading example of cultural transmission and cooperative hunting in the wild.

The main threats to orcas are human-caused. Chemical pollutants such as PCBs accumulate in their blubber; underwater noise interferes with echolocation and communication; and prey depletion stresses fish-eating populations. Conservation status varies by population — the Southern Resident orcas of the northeastern Pacific are endangered from declining Chinook salmon, contaminants, and vessel noise. Captivity poses serious welfare problems for such large, wide-ranging, socially complex animals. The orca stands as a case study in proportionate science: an animal genuinely extraordinary — a bearer of culture and dialect at the top of the ocean's food web — without being either mythologised as a monster or sentimentalised.
