# Summary — The Komodo Dragon (*Varanus komodoensis*)

The Komodo dragon is the largest living lizard and a striking example of island gigantism. It is a monitor lizard (family Varanidae), not a dinosaur or a crocodilian, and it is endemic to a handful of small, dry islands in the Lesser Sunda chain of Indonesia — Komodo, Rinca, Flores, Gili Motang, and a few islets. Large adults reach roughly 2.5–3 meters and 70–90 kilograms. On these islands, with no large mammalian carnivores and a supply of big prey, natural selection favored a giant, patient, energy-efficient top predator. As an ectotherm, the dragon can hunt by ambush, survive on infrequent meals, and roam widely in search of food.

Feeding is the dragon's defining behavior, and it is where science has revised its story. Adults ambush and pursue large prey such as Timor deer, wild pigs, and even water buffalo, sprinting in short bursts far faster than their heavy build suggests. Serrated, blade-like teeth open deep, ragged wounds. For decades the animal was said to kill by infecting prey with filthy, bacteria-laden saliva — the "septic mouth" idea. That explanation has been largely overturned by the discovery of venom glands. Research led by Bryan Fry and colleagues describes venom containing anticoagulant and blood-pressure-lowering compounds. Large prey are now understood to be subdued by a combination of deep wounding, sustained blood loss, and shock, with venom rather than bacterial infection as the key chemical factor.

The dragon's most important sense is smell. Using a deeply forked tongue and the Jacobson's organ in the roof of the mouth, it reads the landscape as a map of scents and can locate carrion from kilometers away, making it a formidable scavenger as well as a hunter. Dragons are largely solitary, coming together mainly at carcasses and to mate, where interactions are governed by body size: the largest animals feed and breed first, and ritualized displays usually prevent serious fights.

Cannibalism shapes the whole life cycle. Adults will eat smaller dragons, so hatchlings are strongly arboreal, living in trees where heavy adults cannot follow, and young dragons even roll in feces to smell unappealing to potential attackers. There is essentially no parental care beyond the nest. Remarkably, captive females have reproduced through parthenogenesis — producing viable offspring from unfertilized eggs, without a male.

The species is listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List. Because the entire population lives in one small, low-lying region, it is unusually exposed to habitat loss, prey decline, and, increasingly, climate change and sea-level rise. The Komodo dragon teaches how ecology, physiology, and behavior interlock — and how careful research replaces a vivid legend with a more accurate account.
