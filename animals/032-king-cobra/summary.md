# Summary — The King Cobra (*Ophiophagus hannah*)

The king cobra is the world's longest venomous snake, commonly reaching 3 to 4 metres and occasionally exceeding 5. Despite its name it is not a true cobra of the genus *Naja*; it belongs to its own genus, *Ophiophagus*, within the elapid family of front-fanged venomous snakes. The genus name means "snake-eater," and it captures the animal's defining trait: the king cobra is a dietary specialist that preys mainly on other snakes, including rat snakes, other cobras, kraits, and even pythons, with the occasional monitor lizard.

Two features make the species stand out in ethology. The first is its parental behavior. Most snakes abandon their eggs, but the female king cobra rakes together leaf litter to build a mounded nest, lays her clutch inside, and coils on or beside it as a guard. The decaying vegetation helps hold warmth and humidity during the roughly two-to-three-month incubation. Crucially, she usually leaves before the eggs hatch, so the young -- fully venomous and boldly banded -- are independent from birth. The second is its distinctive defensive repertoire: a towering upright hood, formed by loose neck skin over elongated ribs, combined with a low-frequency "growl" produced with the help of tracheal diverticula that act as resonating chambers. Both are chiefly warnings meant to deter an intruder rather than automatic preludes to a strike.

The king cobra is a largely diurnal, solitary, active hunter. As an ectotherm it depends on external heat and basks to warm up. It finds prey primarily through chemoreception -- tongue-flicking scent particles to the Jacobson's (vomeronasal) organ -- supported by relatively good vision for a snake and by sensitivity to ground vibration. Its potent neurotoxic venom subdues struggling snake prey quickly, an advantage when the meal can bite back.

Contrary to its fearsome reputation, the king cobra is generally shy and prefers retreat to confrontation. Its defense runs in a graded sequence: flee first, then bluff with hood and growl, and only bite as a last resort. Bites to humans are medically serious but relatively uncommon, because the snake usually avoids people; a guarding female, with a clutch to protect, has a lower threshold for standing her ground.

The IUCN Red List classifies the king cobra as Vulnerable. It is threatened by habitat loss and forest fragmentation, by persecution rooted in fear, and by collection for its skin, the pet trade, and traditional medicine. Its ecological role as a predator of other snakes is one practical argument for protecting it. Studied honestly, the king cobra corrects its own caricature: a wide-ranging snake-eater with near-unique nesting behavior and a defensive system built to avoid a fight rather than start one.
