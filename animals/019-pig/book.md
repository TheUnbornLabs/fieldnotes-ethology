# Book 019 — The Pig: Rooting Intelligence and Social Nuance

## 1. Opening Summary

The domestic pig (*Sus domesticus*) is one of the most behaviorally misunderstood animals in the human world. Familiar as livestock and cultural shorthand for greed and filth, the pig is in reality a curious, socially attentive, and cognitively sophisticated mammal whose behavior repays close study. Ethologically, pigs matter because they combine three things rarely found together in a single farmed species: a rich natural behavioral repertoire inherited almost intact from a wild ancestor, a level of learning and problem-solving that rivals that of dogs and approaches that of some primates, and an economic ubiquity that places hundreds of millions of them in intensive systems where those capacities are routinely frustrated. Studying the pig forces a confrontation between what an animal can do and what its living conditions allow. This tension between capacity and circumstance makes the pig a keystone case in welfare science and a compelling subject for anyone interested in cognition, social organization, and the ethics of domestication.

## 2. Scientific Identity

The domestic pig is commonly called the pig, hog, or swine; a female is a sow, an intact male a boar, and a young pig a piglet. Its scientific name is usually given as *Sus domesticus* (also written *Sus scrofa domesticus*, reflecting its origin as a domesticated form of the Eurasian wild boar, *Sus scrofa*). It belongs to the order Artiodactyla (even-toed ungulates), the family Suidae, and the genus *Sus*. Pigs are not ruminants; they are omnivores with a simple, single-chambered stomach.

Domestic pigs are found worldwide wherever humans farm, from tropical smallholdings to industrial temperate operations, and free-ranging and feral populations occur on every inhabited continent. Their diet is broadly omnivorous: roots, tubers, seeds, fruit, fungi, invertebrates, eggs, and carrion, supplemented in farming by formulated grain-based feed. Lifespan under good conditions can reach 15–20 years, though commercial animals are typically slaughtered within the first year. As a domesticated animal, the domestic pig has no IUCN conservation status; its wild ancestor, the wild boar, is listed as Least Concern and is widespread.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

Pig behavior is shaped by the ecology of the wild boar, a generalist forager of Eurasian woodlands, wetlands, and scrub. Selection favored a mobile, opportunistic omnivore able to exploit patchy, seasonal, and hidden food — acorns one month, earthworms and roots the next. This produced the pig's defining adaptation: an investigative, ground-searching lifestyle built around the snout. Animals that could locate buried and cryptic food, remember where resources lay, and out-compete rivals were favored, laying an evolutionary foundation for spatial memory, exploratory drive, and social awareness.

Wild boar also evolved as social prey. Females form stable matrilineal groups (sounders); adult males are more solitary. Group living against predators such as wolves selected for individual recognition, coordinated vigilance, and a graded vocal system. Domestication, beginning independently in the Near East and East Asia roughly 9,000–10,000 years ago, altered body size, reproduction, coat, and tameness, but left the core behavioral toolkit — rooting, wallowing, nest-building, social ranking, and vocal signaling — remarkably intact. This is why domestic pigs released or feral revert quickly to boar-like behavior.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Pigs are naturally most active in the cooler hours around dawn and dusk (crepuscular tendency), reducing activity during midday heat, though captive routines shift their schedules toward whenever food and light are provided. A free-ranging pig's day is dominated by foraging: studies of pigs in semi-natural enclosures find they may spend well over half their waking hours rooting and exploring the ground, moving steadily across a home range.

Pigs are social and gregarious rather than territorial in a strict sense. Sows and their offspring form the stable social core, with associated animals forming loose groups. Around farrowing (giving birth), a sow separates from the group to build a nest and give birth in isolation, rejoining afterward. Pigs are also notably clean in their spatial organization: given enough room, they establish distinct areas for resting, feeding, and dunging, keeping their sleeping and eating zones free of excrement — a behavior with direct implications for how their intelligence is often underestimated.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

The pig's perceptual world is dominated by smell and touch, organized around the snout. The snout is a highly muscular, mobile organ tipped with the rostral (or "snout") disc, supported by a small prenasal bone, and packed with tactile receptors including sensitive touch-sensory structures. This makes the snout simultaneously a powerful digging tool and a precision sensory instrument, able to discriminate textures and manipulate small objects. Olfaction is exceptional: pigs detect and follow faint odors underground, a capacity exploited historically in truffle hunting.

Hearing is acute and pigs are highly responsive to vocal signals, which underpins their complex call system. Vision is comparatively less relied upon; pigs have laterally placed eyes giving a wide field of view useful for detecting movement, and evidence indicates dichromatic (two-color) vision rather than the fuller color perception of humans. Taste is well developed, consistent with omnivory and a strong preference for sweet and calorie-rich foods. Behaviorally, this sensory profile means pigs experience the world first through nose and snout, then ears, and rely on scent and sound for recognizing individuals and navigating space.

## 6. Feeding Behavior

Rooting is the central feeding behavior of the pig and the behavior around which most of its biology is organized. A pig lowers its snout and drives it into the substrate, levering and turning soil to expose roots, tubers, invertebrates, fungi, and seeds, then uses the sensitive snout disc and mouth to sort edible from inedible material. Rooting is not merely instrumental; it is a strongly motivated behavior that pigs perform even when fully fed on formulated diets, which is why its frustration in barren housing is a serious welfare concern.

Pigs are opportunistic omnivores and generalist foragers. In natural settings they range widely to exploit patchy food, drawing on good spatial memory to relocate productive sites. Foraging is also socially strategic. Because food is often found and monopolizable, pigs attend closely to what other pigs are doing. Experimental work on foraging competition — notably by Suzanne Held and colleagues — has explored how pigs use social information about food location and how a knowledgeable "informed" pig may adjust its behavior in the presence of a larger, exploitative rival that follows it to food, a line of research often discussed in terms of tactical use of information (see Sections 11 and 14).

## 7. Social Behavior

Pigs are fundamentally social animals that live in structured groups built on individual recognition. The natural unit is matrilineal: related sows and their young associate stably, while mature males are more peripheral. Within groups, pigs form dominance hierarchies that reduce repeated conflict over resources once established. Rank is negotiated through displays, pushing, and fighting, and is then largely maintained by learned recognition of individuals, so that stable groups fight far less than newly mixed ones.

This social structure has strong welfare implications. On farms, the routine practice of regrouping unfamiliar pigs — mixing at weaning, transport, or restocking — forces repeated re-establishment of rank and produces bouts of aggression and stress. Pigs recognize and remember many individual group-mates, discriminate familiar from unfamiliar animals, and can even learn to discriminate between individual humans. They also learn socially, acquiring information about food and the environment by observing others, which shapes group behavior and makes social context central to pig cognition.

## 8. Communication

Pigs communicate through a rich vocal repertoire combined with body posture and scent. Researchers have described a wide range of distinct call types — including short grunts, long grunts, squeals, and screams — that vary with context, arousal, and individual identity. Grunts function heavily in social contact and coordination: pigs grunt frequently during foraging and movement, and the rate and structure of calls change with situation and emotional state. Squeals and screams signal high arousal, distress, or pain, as when a piglet is separated or restrained.

Individual recognition is encoded vocally. Sows and piglets recognize one another's calls, and contact calls carry individual signatures that help maintain cohesion. During nursing, a sow gives characteristic rhythmic grunts that coordinate the litter's suckling. Beyond vocalization, pigs use body orientation, head movements, and nudging to negotiate social encounters, and scent — from urine, dung kept in defined latrine areas, and specialized glands — carries information about identity and reproductive state. This multimodal system supports the individually structured societies described above.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

Domestic pigs are highly fecund and, unlike their strongly seasonal wild ancestor, can breed year-round under domestication. Sows are polyestrous, cycling roughly every three weeks when not pregnant. Courtship centers on the boar's detection of a receptive sow through scent: boars produce salivary pheromones, and a sow in standing estrus adopts an immobile "standing" posture that permits mating. Boars also emit rhythmic courtship grunting ("chant") during mating behavior.

Mating systems in free-ranging conditions are polygynous, with dominant boars gaining more matings, and male competition can be intense; the tusks and thickened shoulder shield of boars reflect this history of male-male fighting. Gestation lasts approximately 114 days — memorably "three months, three weeks, and three days." Litters are large, commonly around 8–12 piglets and often more in modern breeds, reflecting a reproductive strategy of producing many relatively developed young. This high output is central to the pig's role in agriculture and to many of the welfare pressures placed on breeding sows.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

Pig parenting begins before birth with one of the species' most striking behaviors: pre-farrowing nest-building. In the day or so before giving birth, a sow becomes restless and, if given suitable material and space, roots, paws, and arranges straw, grass, or branches into a nest — a strongly motivated behavior driven partly by hormonal changes and still expressed even in animals whose ancestors were domesticated millennia ago. This nest thermoregulates and shelters the vulnerable litter.

Piglets are born relatively well developed, mobile, and sighted, and within minutes compete for access to the udder. They rapidly establish a "teat order," each piglet tending to claim and defend a particular teat, which stabilizes nursing and reduces conflict. The sow coordinates nursing with rhythmic grunts. Early life is high-risk: small piglets can be crushed by the sow, chilled, or out-competed at the udder. Piglets begin sampling solid food and rooting within weeks, learning foraging skills partly by observing the sow, and would naturally wean gradually over months — far longer than the abrupt early weaning imposed in commercial systems, which is itself a source of stress and abnormal behavior.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

Pigs are among the more cognitively capable farm animals, a point argued in accessible form by Lori Marino and Christina Colvin in their review of pig cognition, often referred to as the "Thinking Pigs" review. Pigs show robust learning across many domains. They readily learn operant tasks, associating actions with outcomes, and can be trained to perform specific responses for reward. In a notable line of research associated with Stanley Curtis, Candace Croney, and colleagues, pigs learned to operate a joystick-controlled cursor on a computer screen to hit targets — a task requiring them to connect a manipulated device with a symbolic on-screen goal, and one that demonstrates considerable behavioral flexibility.

Pigs also demonstrate strong spatial memory, relocating hidden food and remembering the layout of foraging areas, and they discriminate objects and individuals, distinguishing familiar from unfamiliar pigs and even individual people. A frequently cited experiment by Donald Broom and colleagues showed that pigs could use information from a mirror to locate a food bowl hidden behind a barrier but visible in the reflection, guiding their behavior toward the real food rather than searching behind the mirror. This is evidence of information-use, not the same as passing the mark test of mirror self-recognition, and the two should not be conflated. Work by Suzanne Held and colleagues on foraging competition suggests pigs can use social information tactically — for example, an "exploited" informed pig altering its foraging when a dominant follower is present. Claims about pig cognition are best stated carefully: evidence for flexible learning, memory, and social awareness is strong, while stronger claims such as self-awareness remain debated and should be flagged rather than asserted.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

Wild boar defense combines flight, group cohesion, and, when cornered, formidable aggression using tusks; sows with young are especially defensive. Domestic pigs retain much of this behavioral machinery. They are neophobic in some contexts, wary of novelty and sudden change, and respond to threat with alarm calls, fleeing, freezing, or bunching together. Piglets and adults emit loud squeals and screams under restraint or pain, functioning as distress signals.

In farming, chronic stress is a central welfare issue. Barren, crowded, or unpredictable environments, and the repeated mixing of unfamiliar animals, generate fear and social stress that can be measured physiologically and behaviorally. Frustration of strongly motivated behaviors — above all rooting and, in sows, nest-building — is linked to abnormal repetitive behaviors such as bar-biting and sham-chewing in confined sows, and to tail-biting outbreaks in growing pigs kept without adequate rooting material. These abnormal behaviors are widely interpreted as signals of thwarted motivation and poor welfare rather than random vice, and they are a major reason enrichment is emphasized in modern welfare science.

## 13. Human Interaction

Few animals are as entangled with humans as the pig. Domesticated independently in at least two regions around 9,000–10,000 years ago, pigs became a staple protein source across much of the world and today number in the hundreds of millions in production at any time. This relationship is deeply ambivalent. Pigs are central to some cuisines and cultures and prohibited in others; they appear in folklore as both symbols of prosperity and of filth and gluttony. Pot-bellied and miniature pigs are kept as companion animals, and pigs are used in biomedical research and as models in physiology, partly because of anatomical similarities to humans.

The dominant modern context is intensive farming, which raises acute ethical concerns given what is known about pig cognition and sociality. Practices under scrutiny include gestation (sow) stalls that confine pregnant sows so they cannot turn around, farrowing crates that prevent nest-building, barren housing that denies rooting, routine early weaning, and tail-docking used to reduce tail-biting that itself arises largely from barren conditions. Welfare science increasingly frames the solution in terms of environmental enrichment — rooting substrate, space, social stability, and nest material — and several jurisdictions have moved to phase out the most severe confinement. The pig thus sits at the center of ongoing debates about the ethics of animal agriculture.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

Myth 1: Pigs are dirty animals that love filth.
Scientific reality: Pigs are among the cleaner farm mammals when given room. They establish separate areas for resting, feeding, and dunging and avoid soiling their sleeping and eating zones. Wallowing in mud is not a preference for filth but a functional behavior (see Myth 3). The "dirty" reputation largely reflects cramped, barren housing that denies pigs the space to keep clean.

Myth 2: Pigs are unintelligent.
Scientific reality: Pigs are highly capable learners. They master operant tasks, have been trained to use a joystick to move an on-screen cursor to targets (work associated with Curtis, Croney, and colleagues), show strong spatial memory, discriminate objects and individuals, and can use a mirror to locate hidden food (Broom and colleagues). Reviews such as Marino and Colvin's "Thinking Pigs" place their cognition alongside that of dogs.

Myth 3: Pigs sweat, as in "sweating like a pig."
Scientific reality: Pigs have very few functional sweat glands and cannot effectively cool themselves by sweating. To lose heat they wallow in mud or water, which cools by evaporation and also protects the skin. The phrase is biologically false; if anything, the pig's inability to sweat is precisely why it must wallow.

Myth 4: "Teacup" or "micro" pigs stay tiny.
Scientific reality: There is no genuinely tiny pig breed that stays pocket-sized. "Teacup pig" is a marketing label; animals sold as such are typically young miniature pigs that grow to many tens of kilograms. Underfeeding to keep them small causes harm, and many are surrendered when they outgrow expectations.

Myth 5: Pigs are placid, simple animals with little social life.
Scientific reality: Pigs live in structured societies based on individual recognition, dominance hierarchies, and social learning. They recognize many group-mates and individual humans, communicate with a large repertoire of context-specific calls, and adjust foraging based on what rivals know — evidence of genuine social complexity, not passivity.

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

The most illuminating comparison is between the domestic pig and its own wild ancestor, the wild boar (*Sus scrofa*). Behaviorally the two are strikingly continuous, which is itself the key lesson: domestication reshaped the pig's body and reproduction far more than its core behavior. Wild boar are seasonal breeders with smaller litters, leaner bodies, longer snouts and legs, dense bristled coats, and prominent tusks in males; they are wary, ranging animals living in matrilineal sounders and covering large home ranges. Domestic pigs, by contrast, are larger and fatter in most breeds, breed year-round, produce larger litters, mature faster, and tolerate human proximity — the products of thousands of years of selection for productivity and tameness.

Yet the rooting, wallowing, nest-building, social ranking, individual recognition, and graded vocal communication of the domestic pig are essentially inherited from the boar and expressed whenever conditions allow. Feral domestic pigs revert rapidly toward a boar-like life. This continuity is central to welfare arguments: because the motivations behind rooting and nest-building were not bred out, denying their expression in intensive systems frustrates behaviors the animal is still strongly driven to perform.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

The pig teaches several broad lessons in animal behavior. First, it demonstrates how a single morphological adaptation — the muscular, sensitive snout — can organize an entire behavioral economy, linking feeding, exploration, sensory perception, and even communication around one investigative strategy. Second, the pig is a case study in the persistence of behavior under domestication: selection changed the animal's form and reproduction dramatically while leaving its motivational core intact, showing that "domesticated" does not mean "behaviorally blank."

Third, the pig sharpens how ethologists interpret cognition. Its mirror-and-food and joystick studies illustrate the importance of stating precisely what a result shows — information-use and flexible learning — without overclaiming self-awareness. Fourth, and most consequentially, the pig illustrates the welfare stakes of a gap between an animal's capacities and its environment. When a curious, socially aware, strongly motivated forager is placed in a barren crate, the predictable result is frustration expressed as abnormal behavior. The pig thus links pure ethology to applied ethics: understanding what an animal is drives conclusions about how it should be kept.

## 17. Glossary

- **Rooting** — the pig's core foraging behavior of pushing and levering the snout through soil to find and expose buried food.
- **Wild boar (*Sus scrofa*)** — the wild Eurasian ancestor of the domestic pig, whose behavior the domestic pig largely retains.
- **Farrowing** — the act of a sow giving birth to a litter.
- **Nest-building** — strongly motivated pre-farrowing behavior in which a sow arranges material into a nest before giving birth.
- **Wallowing** — rolling and lying in mud or water, primarily to lose heat, because pigs have few functional sweat glands.
- **Thermoregulation** — the maintenance of body temperature; in pigs achieved partly through wallowing rather than sweating.
- **Mirror food-finding task** — an experiment (Broom and colleagues) showing pigs can use reflected information to locate hidden food; a test of information-use, not of mirror self-recognition.
- **Operant conditioning** — learning in which an animal associates its own actions with consequences, the basis of joystick and lever tasks in pigs.
- **Contact call** — a vocalization used to maintain social cohesion and, in pigs, carrying individually recognizable signatures.
- **Dominance hierarchy** — a ranked social order that reduces repeated conflict once established through recognition of individuals.
- **Enrichment** — additions to an environment (rooting substrate, space, nest material, social stability) that allow natural behaviors and improve welfare.
- **Tactical use of information** — adjusting behavior based on what competitors know or can see, studied in pig foraging competition (Held and colleagues).
- **Sounder** — a matrilineal social group of sows and their young, the natural social unit in wild boar and pigs.
- **Teat order** — the stable assignment of piglets to particular teats, reducing conflict during nursing.
- **Gestation (sow) stall** — a narrow crate confining a pregnant sow, a central welfare concern in intensive farming.
