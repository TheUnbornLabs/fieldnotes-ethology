## Well-established sources (safe to name)

- Lori Marino and Christina Colvin — their review of pig cognition and behavior, commonly known as the "Thinking Pigs" review, is a widely cited synthesis of the evidence for pig learning, memory, and social complexity. (Cite the authors and review generally; verify the exact title, journal, and year before final publication.)
- Donald M. Broom — a leading figure in animal welfare science whose group reported that pigs could use information from a mirror to locate a hidden food bowl. (Safe to attribute the mirror-and-food finding to Broom and colleagues in general terms; verify the specific article details before final publication.)
- Suzanne (Suzy) Held — a researcher on farm-animal behavior and cognition, associated with experimental work on pig foraging cognition and the use of social information in foraging competition. (Attribute the general research program to Held and colleagues; verify specific study details before final publication.)
- IUCN Red List — species assessment for the wild boar (*Sus scrofa*), the wild ancestor of the domestic pig, listed as Least Concern. The domestic pig, as a domesticated animal, is not assessed for conservation status.
- Animal Diversity Web (University of Michigan Museum of Zoology) — species profiles for *Sus scrofa* and the domestic pig, useful for taxonomy, natural history, and general behavior.
- Standard animal-behavior and welfare textbooks (e.g. Alcock, *Animal Behavior*) for general ethological concepts such as domestication, communication, learning, dominance hierarchies, and welfare assessment.

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

The following were checked against the live IUCN Red List and Animal Diversity Web:
