# Summary — The Pig (*Sus domesticus*)

The domestic pig is one of the most behaviorally underestimated animals in the human world. Popularly linked with filth and stupidity, it is in reality a curious, socially sophisticated, and cognitively capable mammal — a domesticated form of the Eurasian wild boar (*Sus scrofa*) that retains almost the whole behavioral toolkit of its wild ancestor. Domesticated independently in the Near East and East Asia some 9,000–10,000 years ago, the pig kept its core behaviors — rooting, wallowing, nest-building, social ranking, and vocal communication — even as selection reshaped its body, reproduction, and tameness.

The pig's biology is organized around its snout, a muscular, highly sensitive organ that is both a powerful digging tool and a precise touch-and-smell instrument. Rooting — driving the snout through soil to find buried roots, invertebrates, and fungi — is the pig's central foraging behavior and remains strongly motivated even in well-fed animals, which is why barren housing that prevents it causes frustration and abnormal behavior. Smell and touch dominate the pig's perception, supported by acute hearing; vision is comparatively less important, and pigs appear to have two-color (dichromatic) vision.

Pigs live in structured, matrilineal societies based on individual recognition and dominance hierarchies that, once settled, reduce conflict. They communicate through a large repertoire of context-specific grunts, squeals, and screams, with contact calls carrying individual signatures that let sows and piglets recognize one another. Around birth, a sow separates to build a nest — a hormonally driven behavior still expressed despite millennia of domestication — and gives birth to a large, well-developed litter that quickly establishes a stable teat order.

Cognitively, pigs stand out among farm animals. They master operant tasks, show strong spatial memory, discriminate objects and individuals (including individual humans), and learn socially. In work associated with Curtis, Croney, and colleagues, pigs learned to use a joystick to move a cursor to on-screen targets. Donald Broom and colleagues showed that pigs can use information from a mirror to locate hidden food — a demonstration of information-use, not the same as passing the mirror mark test of self-recognition. Suzanne Held and colleagues' research suggests pigs can use social information tactically during foraging competition. Lori Marino and Christina Colvin's "Thinking Pigs" review gathers such findings and places pig cognition alongside that of dogs.

This capacity collides with how most pigs live. Intensive farming raises acute welfare concerns — gestation stalls, farrowing crates that block nest-building, barren rooting-free housing, early weaning, and tail-docking — because it frustrates behaviors pigs are still strongly driven to perform. The pig therefore sits at the center of animal-welfare science, illustrating a wider ethological lesson: understanding what an animal is drives conclusions about how it should be kept.
