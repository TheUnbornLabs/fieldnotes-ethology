# Glossary — Book 028: The Octopus

- **Cephalopod** — a class of marine molluscs (octopus, squid, cuttlefish, nautilus) characterized by arms or tentacles, a beak, and a well-developed nervous system.
- **Distributed nervous system** — a nervous organization in which neurons and processing are spread through the body (here, mostly the arms) rather than concentrated in a central brain.
- **Chromatophore** — a pigment-filled skin cell that expands or contracts under muscle control to change color and pattern.
- **Iridophore** — a reflective skin cell that produces iridescent and structural colors by reflecting light.
- **Leucophore** — a skin cell that scatters and reflects ambient light broadly, appearing white and helping match background brightness.
- **Papillae** — small muscular skin projections that can be raised or flattened to change the three-dimensional texture of the skin.
- **Dynamic camouflage** — the rapid, active matching of color, brightness, and texture to the surroundings for concealment.
- **Polarization vision** — sensitivity to the plane of vibration of light, a cue octopuses may use in place of color vision.
- **Semelparity** — a reproductive strategy of a single lifetime breeding event, after which the animal dies.
- **Jet propulsion** — locomotion by forcing water out through the muscular funnel to drive the body rapidly backward.
- **Den** — the crevice or cavity an octopus occupies as a home base and refuge.
- **RNA editing** — molecular alteration of RNA after transcription so that the protein produced differs from the strict DNA template; unusually extensive in cephalopods.
- **Hectocotylus** — the modified arm a male octopus uses to transfer sperm packets to a female.
- **Paralarva** — the small, planktonic early life stage of an octopus after hatching, before settling to the seafloor.
- **Radula** — a toothed feeding structure used, with the beak, to bore into and rasp prey such as shelled molluscs.
