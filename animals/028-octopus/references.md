# References / Further Reading — Book 028: The Octopus

Sources are separated into those safe to name and those that should be verified against a database before final publication. No citations have been invented.

## Well-established sources (safe to name)

- Peter Godfrey-Smith, *Other Minds: The Octopus, the Sea, and the Deep Origins of Consciousness* — a widely read, credible book on octopus and cephalopod minds and their philosophical implications.
- Jennifer Mather and Roland Anderson — recognized researchers whose published work on octopus behavior, cognition, and personality is a standard entry point (see also their popular book *Octopus: The Ocean's Intelligent Invertebrate*).
- Finn and colleagues — the peer-reviewed report describing coconut-shell carrying as tool use in the veined octopus (*Amphioctopus marginatus*). Named here in general terms; verify the exact citation before publication.
- IUCN and standard marine-science / fisheries references for the status and commercial exploitation of *Octopus vulgaris*.

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

- Scientific name *Octopus vulgaris*; Phylum Mollusca, Class Cephalopoda, Order Octopoda — confirmed.
- *Octopus vulgaris* species-complex claim — confirmed. The former cosmopolitan "*O. vulgaris*" is now recognised as a complex of several cryptic species; *O. vulgaris sensu stricto* is restricted to the NE Atlantic and Mediterranean, as the book states. Sources: Springer (O. americanus redescription, 2020); Frontiers in Marine Science (2022); bioRxiv 573493.
- Finn et al. coconut-carrying tool use in the veined octopus (*Amphioctopus marginatus*) — confirmed. Full citation now verified: Finn, J. K., Tregenza, T. & Norman, M. D. (2009), "Defensive tool use in a coconut-carrying octopus," *Current Biology* 19(23): R1069–R1070.
- NOTE (IUCN): *Octopus vulgaris* does not have a settled species-level IUCN Red List category (sources conflict between "not assessed" and "Least Concern"); the book text does not assert a specific IUCN status, so no correction is needed, but any future IUCN-status claim should be checked directly against iucnredlist.org.
