# Summary — The Nile Crocodile (*Crocodylus niloticus*)

The Nile crocodile is Africa's largest crocodilian and one of the most instructive animals in ethology because it combines extreme predatory power with genuinely elaborate parental care. It is a sit-and-wait ambush predator: rather than chasing prey, it positions itself where prey must come — a drinking spot, a river crossing, a fish run — and waits, showing little more than eyes and nostrils above the surface, before striking with a lunge powered by one of the strongest bite forces measured in any living animal. Because its conical teeth grip rather than cut, it dismembers large prey and subdues struggling animals with the "death roll," spinning its body along its long axis to twist off a manageable piece or to drown its catch.

As an ectotherm, the crocodile cannot generate its own body heat and organises its day around thermoregulation, basking in the morning sun, moving between sun, shade, and water, and gaping — holding its mouth open — to shed heat. This low-energy lifestyle makes patience affordable and lets the animal survive long fasts. Its senses are finely tuned for aquatic ambush: good eyesight with a reflective tapetum lucidum for night vision, acute hearing, and dome pressure receptors (integumentary sensory organs) scattered across the skin and concentrated around the jaws, which detect the ripples and pressure changes made by prey moving on the water, even in darkness or murky conditions.

The behavior that most overturns the "primitive reptile" stereotype is parental care. The female digs and guards a nest for the roughly three-month incubation, during which temperature-dependent sex determination — not chromosomes — sets the sex of the young. As hatching nears, the young chirp from inside their eggs, prompting the mother to open the nest; she then carries hatchlings gently to water in the same jaws that can crush bone, and guards a nursery, or crèche, for weeks. Adults also communicate through deep bellows, head-slaps, and infrasound that vibrates the water.

Crocodiles are broadly solitary yet aggregate at basking and feeding sites, where a size-based dominance order produces queuing at carcasses and occasional loosely coordinated feeding. Notably, crocodilians are archosaurs, more closely related to birds than to lizards or snakes, which helps explain their bird-like care of young. The species is a serious predator of humans, is exploited for its skin, and features in African and ancient Egyptian culture. Globally it is listed as Least Concern, though some regional populations remain depleted. The Nile crocodile is not an unchanged fossil or a mindless brute but a learning, adaptable, and surprisingly attentive apex predator.
