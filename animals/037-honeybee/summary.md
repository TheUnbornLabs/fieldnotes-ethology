# Summary — The Honeybee (*Apis mellifera*)

The Western honeybee is among the most intensively studied animals in behavioral science, and it earns that attention by challenging our idea of what a "behaving unit" is. A honeybee colony is not just a crowd of insects; it is a tightly integrated society of tens of thousands of individuals that forages, defends itself, regulates its temperature, reproduces, and makes decisions with no central controller. Biologists therefore describe the colony as a **superorganism** — order emerging from thousands of local interactions rather than from any plan or ruler.

The colony contains a single reproductive **queen**, tens of thousands of sterile female **workers**, and, seasonally, several hundred male **drones** whose only role is to mate. Workers divide labor by age (**temporal polyethism**): a young worker cleans cells, then nurses larvae, builds comb, guards the entrance, and finally forages — a schedule that flexes with colony need without any manager assigning jobs.

The honeybee's most famous behavior is the **waggle dance**, decoded by Karl von Frisch. A forager returning from a rich, distant patch performs a figure-eight on the vertical comb; the angle of the straight "waggle" run relative to vertical encodes the resource's direction relative to the sun, and the duration of the waggle encodes its distance. An internal clock lets bees compensate for the sun's movement across the sky. Other signals round out the colony's communication: the **round dance** for nearby food, the **tremble dance** to recruit nectar receivers, the **stop signal** that inhibits recruitment, and pheromones such as **queen mandibular pheromone** and **alarm pheromone**.

Colonies also make genuine group decisions. When a swarm must choose a new home, **scout bees** search independently and advertise good cavities by dancing; better sites earn stronger dances, scouts suppress rivals with the stop signal, and once support for one site passes a **quorum**, the swarm commits and flies there. Thomas Seeley called this "honeybee democracy." Colonies build wax comb, regulate nest temperature by clustering and shivering for warmth or fanning and evaporating water for cooling, and defend themselves collectively — even cooking predatory hornets in a heated ball of bees. Foraging bees are central-place foragers whose flower constancy makes them vital crop **pollinators**.

Several persistent myths deserve correction: not all bees make honey or die when they sting; the queen reproduces but does not command; bees are mainly defensive, not aggressive; and a normal hive has one queen. Today, honeybee colonies face serious pressure from the introduced *Varroa* mite, viruses, pesticides, and poor forage. The honeybee's enduring lesson for ethology is that sophisticated, adaptive behavior can arise from many simple agents following local rules — self-organization made visible.
