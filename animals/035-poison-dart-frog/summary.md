# Summary — The Poison Dart Frog (Family Dendrobatidae)

Poison dart frogs, the family Dendrobatidae, are small, often brilliantly coloured frogs of the tropical forests of Central and South America. Their vivid colours are not decoration but a warning: an example of aposematism, the strategy of advertising a genuine defence so that predators learn to stay away. The defence is real toxicity, held in the frogs' skin — yet, remarkably, the frogs do not make it themselves. They sequester defensive alkaloids from the small arthropods they eat, especially ants and mites. This diet-derived toxicity has a clear and well-documented consequence: frogs of the same species raised in captivity on alkaloid-free food such as fruit flies and crickets grow up essentially non-toxic, because the raw material is missing.

Because their chemical defence deters predators at a distance, dendrobatids can break the usual frog mould. Most are diurnal, moving and calling openly in daylight rather than hiding at night, and they rely on honest warning rather than camouflage — the opposite strategy to a dull, cryptic toad that survives by not being seen. Males defend territories containing calling perches and small breeding pools, advertising with species-specific calls that serve both to attract females and to repel rivals.

The family is also famous for parental care that is exceptional among amphibians. Eggs are typically laid on land, where a parent (often the male) guards the clutch and keeps it moist. After hatching, a parent carries the tadpoles on its back, sometimes one at a time, to small water bodies such as leaf axils, tree holes, or the pools held by bromeliads. In the strawberry poison frog (*Oophaga pumilio*), the mother goes further still: she deposits each tadpole in its own small pool and returns repeatedly over weeks to feed it unfertilised "nurse" eggs — an obligate egg-feeding system on which the tadpole entirely depends.

Dendrobatid warning colours have produced some of nature's clearest cases of mimicry, both Müllerian (defended species sharing a signal) and Batesian (harmless species copying it). Toxicity varies enormously: many species are only mildly unpalatable, while the golden poison frog (*Phyllobates terribilis*) is among the most toxic vertebrates known. Only a few highly toxic species were ever used by particular Indigenous peoples to poison blowgun darts — the source of the family's common name.

Today many poison dart frogs are threatened by habitat loss, the chytrid fungus, and collection for the pet trade. They remain one of ethology's richest lessons in honest signalling, the diet-dependence of defence, and the evolution of devoted parenting.
