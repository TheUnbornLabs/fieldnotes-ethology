## Well-established sources (safe to name)

- Ádám Miklósi, *Dog Behaviour, Evolution, and Cognition* — a standard scholarly reference on dog behavior, domestication, and human-directed social cognition.
- Raymond Coppinger and Lorna Coppinger, *Dogs: A New Understanding of Canine Origin, Behavior, and Evolution* — an influential book on the scavenging/commensal model of dog domestication and on breed diversity.
- American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior (AVSAB) — position statements on the use of dominance theory in behavior modification and on humane, reward-based training and puppy socialization. (Cite the organization and its position statements generally; verify the exact titles and dates before final publication.)
- IUCN Red List — species assessment for *Canis lupus* (the wolf lineage from which dogs descend). The domestic dog itself, as a domesticated animal, is not assessed for conservation status.
- Animal Diversity Web (University of Michigan Museum of Zoology) — species profile for the domestic dog.
- Standard animal-behavior textbooks (e.g. Alcock, *Animal Behavior*) for general ethological concepts such as domestication, communication, learning, and social behavior.

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

The following were checked against the live IUCN Red List and Animal Diversity Web:

- The domestic dog (family Canidae) is a **domesticated animal and is not assessed for conservation status** by the IUCN; the wolf lineage from which it descends, *Canis lupus*, is IUCN-assessed (Least Concern) — confirmed. The dog's scientific name is written *Canis familiaris* or, as a subspecies of the wolf, *Canis lupus familiaris*; both are accepted forms. Source: IUCN Red List (*Canis lupus*); Animal Diversity Web.
