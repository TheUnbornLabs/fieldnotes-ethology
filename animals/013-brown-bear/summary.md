# Summary — Book 013: The Brown Bear

The brown bear (*Ursus arctos*) is the most widely distributed bear species, ranging across northern Eurasia and western North America, where its interior form is known as the grizzly and its largest coastal form as the Kodiak bear. Globally it is listed as Least Concern by the IUCN, though many small, isolated southern and European populations remain threatened. As an ethological subject, the brown bear is valuable because it breaks the usual categories for large carnivores: it is a solitary, non-territorial omnivore whose behavior is organized less by daily rhythms than by the extreme demands of the seasons.

Brown bears live mostly alone across large home ranges that overlap extensively with those of neighbours; they defend no fixed territory. Their year turns on a single physiological gamble — feeding enough in a few months to survive winter without eating. This produces two dramatic behavioral states: hyperphagia, a period of intense pre-winter feeding that builds massive fat reserves, and winter denning, a prolonged dormancy. That dormancy is best described as torpor rather than true deep hibernation: metabolism drops but body temperature falls only moderately, bears can rouse if disturbed, and females give birth and nurse cubs mid-winter.

Feeding drives brown bear behavior. They are flexible omnivores that track seasonal foods — spring vegetation, roots, insects, berries, carrion, ungulates, and, where available, spawning salmon — with plant matter often dominating the diet. At rich, concentrated food sources such as salmon runs, normally solitary bears form temporary aggregations and sort access through an informal dominance queue based on size, age, and experience, with little serious fighting. Where bears carry salmon inland, they also transport marine-derived nutrients into forests, an important ecological role.

The brown bear's senses are led by an exceptional sense of smell, among the keenest of any land mammal, which guides foraging, mating, and navigation; hearing is acute and vision functional. A bear standing on its hind legs is usually gathering scent and information, not preparing to attack. Reproduction features delayed implantation, in which the embryo pauses and implants only if the mother is in good condition, so cubs are born in the den only when she can support them. Cubs stay with their mother for about two to three years, learning her foraging knowledge, and mothers defend them fiercely — in part against sexually selected infanticide by adult males, which can bring a female back into breeding condition sooner.

Human–bear relations center on coexistence rather than species survival. The key problem is food-conditioning: bears that obtain human food or garbage lose their wariness and enter conflict, often fatally for the bear. Most attacks on people are defensive and rare relative to encounters. Managed well, the brown bear remains a resilient, adaptable generalist and a model of how behavior and physiology together meet a demanding, variable world.
