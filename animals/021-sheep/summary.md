# Summary — Book 021: The Sheep

The domestic sheep (*Ovis aries*) is one of the most familiar and most misjudged animals on Earth. Everyday language treats "sheep" as a byword for mindless conformity, yet behavioural science reveals a socially intelligent grazer whose group-living strategy is an elegant answer to the problem of surviving as prey. Domesticated roughly ten thousand years ago from the wild mouflon (*Ovis orientalis / gmelini*) in the Near East, sheep are ruminants of the subfamily Caprinae — the same group as goats — and now number well over a billion animals worldwide.

The heart of sheep behaviour is obligate flocking. Living in a cohesive, coordinated group lowers each individual's risk of predation through safety in numbers, shared vigilance, and synchrony: an animal that grazes, moves, and flees in time with its neighbours is far harder for a predator to single out than a straggler. Follower behaviour and allelomimetic action — doing what neighbours do — keep the flock together and let it react to danger as one. This is adaptive design, not stupidity.

Beneath the flock sits a surprisingly capable mind. Research led by Keith Kendrick and colleagues showed that sheep can learn and remember the faces of many individual sheep, retain them for long periods (in some cases across about two years), and recognise human faces as well. Later work indicates sheep can even discriminate between emotional expressions and between calm and stressed states. Sheep also recognise one another by voice: ewes and lambs identify each other's calls, and a ewe further identifies her own lamb by scent, forming a specific, exclusive bond in the first hours after birth. Lambs are precocial, standing and following their mother soon after they are born.

Sheep perceive their world through wide, panoramic vision suited to scanning for predators, acute mobile hearing, and a keen sense of smell that underpins reproduction and the mother–lamb bond. Their defence is the group: a flight zone that handlers and herding dogs use to steer flocks, plus bunching and coordinated flight. Because they are so social, isolation is intensely stressful, and familiar companions measurably calm them — a central welfare point emphasised by researchers such as John Webster.

Compared with the goat, a close relative, the sheep is a tight-flocking grazer of open ground, where the goat is an independent, exploratory browser. Both are intelligent ruminants pursuing different survival strategies. The sheep's lesson for ethology is that conspicuous behaviour can hide a capable mind: the animal that follows the crowd also carries a detailed memory of faces and voices, forms real bonds, and has an emotional life worth taking seriously.
