# Book 003 — The Chimpanzee: Summary

The common chimpanzee (*Pan troglodytes*) is, with the bonobo, our closest living relative and one of the most influential subjects in the history of ethology. When Jane Goodall reported wild chimpanzees making and using tools at Gombe Stream in 1960, she overturned the assumption that tool manufacture was uniquely human. Decades of subsequent field research have shown that chimpanzees combine tool cultures, cooperative hunting, complex politics, and, in some populations, sustained lethal aggression between communities — a combination once thought to require human language and morality.

Chimpanzees are great apes, not monkeys, living across equatorial Africa in rainforest, woodland, and forest-savanna mosaic. Their society is organized as fission-fusion communities: a stable membership of a few dozen to over a hundred individuals shares and defends a territory but rarely gathers all at once, instead splitting into temporary parties that change through the day. Communities are marked by male philopatry — males stay in their birth group for life — and female dispersal, so males are typically lifelong, often related associates while adult females are usually immigrants. Males form dominance hierarchies in which rank frequently depends on political coalitions rather than strength alone, as popularized in Frans de Waal's *Chimpanzee Politics*. Grooming maintains the bonds and alliances that hold this fluid society together.

Their behavior is behaviorally rich. Chimpanzees fish for termites with modified plant probes, crack hard nuts using a hammer-and-anvil technique, and at Fongoli in Senegal fashion sharpened sticks to hunt bushbabies. Groups of males hunt red colobus monkeys and then share meat, often preferentially with allies and relatives. Different communities maintain different tool traditions transmitted through social learning, producing the cultural variation catalogued in the synthesis by Whiten and colleagues. Communication spans pant-hoots, gestures, facial expressions, charging displays, and buttress drumming.

Chimpanzee cognition is genuinely sophisticated, including planning, spatial memory, and social learning, but claims about it must remain proportionate. Whether chimpanzees possess a full human-like theory of mind is scientifically debated; they track what others see and know, yet the extent to which they represent others' beliefs is contested. Because they are so like us, chimpanzees expose how easily humans project their own minds onto animals — and how much discipline accurate description requires.

The comparison with the bonobo is instructive: the two *Pan* species diverge sharply, with bonobo society more influenced by females and generally less aggressive than the male-dominated chimpanzee. This warns against treating either as a simple mirror of humanity.

The IUCN classifies *Pan troglodytes* as Endangered, with the western subspecies Critically Endangered. Habitat loss, the bushmeat trade, the capture of infants for the pet and entertainment trades, and disease drive the decline. Past use in biomedical research and entertainment raises serious ethical questions given the species' complexity. Conservation, sanctuary, and rehabilitation efforts — including those of the Jane Goodall Institute — now focus on protecting habitat and confiscated individuals.
