# Summary: The Gorilla

Gorillas are the largest living primates and among the most misunderstood of our close relatives. This book focuses on the Western Gorilla (*Gorilla gorilla*), drawing on the well-studied Mountain Gorilla (*Gorilla beringei beringei*) where relevant. Popular culture painted gorillas as roaring monsters, but field study — beginning with George Schaller and Dian Fossey — shows predominantly peaceful, plant-eating apes living in stable family groups.

The core social unit is a cohesive group usually led by a single dominant male, the **silverback**, with several unrelated adult females, their offspring, and sometimes younger **blackback** males. Most groups are one-male/multi-female, though multi-male groups occur, especially among mountain gorillas. Female bonds are weak because females typically **transfer** out of their natal group to join a lone silverback or another group; cohesion comes from each female's bond with the protective male rather than from female kinship.

Gorillas are diurnal, terrestrial **knuckle-walkers**. Their day alternates feeding bouts with a long midday rest, and each evening they build fresh nests. They are bulk herbivores: western gorillas eat abundant fruit when available plus leaves, stems, pith, and bark, and take some invertebrates such as termites and ants, while mountain gorillas eat a more consistently leafy diet. A large **hindgut** ferments fibrous plants, the anatomical basis for surviving on low-quality forage and for their great size.

Communication is rich — soft grumbles and belch-vocalisations that keep groupmates in contact, hoots and roars, a feeding "hum" or song, and the famous **chest-beat display**. Rather than a prelude to attack, chest-beating advertises a male's size; research on mountain gorillas (Wright and colleagues) suggests the sound relates to body size, making it a candidate **honest signal** — a claim to verify against the primary source. Gorillas are **polygynous**; a dominant silverback fathers most offspring, and males compete for the loyalty of females. **Infanticide** by an incoming male is a real risk during takeovers, shaping female choice. Reproduction is slow: single infants, long nursing, interbirth intervals of about four years, and intense maternal care with learning by imitation.

Gorillas are intelligent great apes, but wild **tool use is rare** (though documented, such as using a stick to gauge water depth), and language claims based on the captive gorilla Koko remain scientifically contested and should be treated cautiously. Compared with chimpanzees, gorillas are larger, more herbivorous, organised around a single male, and generally less aggressive — chimpanzees hunt, live in fluid fission-fusion communities, and can be lethally territorial.

The Western Gorilla is **Critically Endangered** (IUCN), threatened by habitat loss, poaching, and Ebola. Yet intensive protection at Karisoke and by the Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund has driven a real recovery in mountain gorillas — proof that dedicated conservation can work even for a slow-breeding ape.
