# Book 027 — The Humpback Whale: Song, Bubble Nets, and Long Migrations

## 1. Opening Summary

Few animals have shaped human ideas about the ocean as powerfully as the humpback whale. In 1970, a recording of humpback song reached millions of people and helped launch the modern movement to protect whales. The reason the humpback matters so much to ethology, the study of animal behavior, is not only that it is large and charismatic. It is that this baleen whale combines three behaviors rarely found together in one species: an elaborate, ever-changing male song that spreads culturally between populations; cooperative feeding techniques, including the famous bubble net, that some groups pass to one another through social learning; and one of the longest migrations of any mammal on Earth.

The humpback is a filter feeder that strains small prey from seawater, yet it behaves in ways once thought reserved for the most social mammals. Its associations are fluid rather than fixed, its communication is rich, and its recovery from near-extermination by commercial whaling stands as one of the clearest conservation success stories of the past century. This book examines what humpbacks actually do, what the evidence shows, and where honest scientific uncertainty remains — especially around the true function of song and the widely reported cases of humpbacks disrupting killer whale hunts.

## 2. Scientific Identity

The humpback whale is *Megaptera novaeangliae*. The genus name means "big wing," a reference to the whale's extraordinarily long pectoral fins, and the species name means "of New England," where early specimens were described. It belongs to the baleen whales (suborder Mysticeti) and specifically to the rorqual family (Balaenopteridae), whales with pleated, expandable throat grooves.

Humpbacks live in every major ocean, from tropical breeding waters to cold, productive feeding grounds near the poles. Adults typically reach 12 to 16 meters in length and can weigh around 30 tonnes; females are slightly larger than males. Their diet consists of small schooling fish and krill, captured by filtering. Lifespan is estimated at several decades, commonly cited around 50 years or more, though precise figures are hard to confirm in the wild.

On the IUCN Red List, *Megaptera novaeangliae* is currently assessed as Least Concern at the global species level, reflecting broad recovery since the end of large-scale commercial whaling, although some distinct populations remain small and are recognized as separate conservation units.

## 3. Evolutionary Background

Humpbacks descend from land mammals whose lineage returned to the sea tens of millions of years ago. As baleen whales, they abandoned teeth in favor of baleen plates — fringed structures of keratin that act as a sieve. This filter-feeding strategy allowed early mysticetes to exploit dense concentrations of tiny prey, harvesting enormous biomass efficiently rather than pursuing individual large animals.

The rorqual body plan reflects lunge feeding. Expandable throat pleats let a humpback engulf a volume of water larger than its own body, then push it back out through the baleen. The long pectoral fins, unusual among whales, likely aid maneuverability, allowing the tight turns and coordinated movements used in cooperative feeding.

The species' long migrations are shaped by a mismatch between where food is abundant and where calving is safe. High-latitude waters offer rich summer feeding but are cold and predator-rich; warm tropical waters are food-poor but favorable for newborn calves. Selection has favored whales that exploit both, storing energy at the poles and breeding in the tropics. Male song, too, is a product of sexual selection, though exactly which pressures it serves remains an active question.

## 4. Daily Life and Activity Pattern

Humpbacks are active by day and night, structuring their year around a great annual cycle rather than a strict daily one. For roughly half the year they occupy cold, productive feeding grounds, spending long hours foraging to build blubber reserves. For the other half, many migrate to tropical or subtropical breeding grounds, where feeding largely stops and the focus shifts to mating and calving.

They are not territorial in the classic sense and do not defend fixed home ranges. Instead they are migratory and highly mobile, following predictable routes between well-defined feeding and breeding areas. Social groupings are loose and short-lived. A humpback may travel alone, in a temporary pair, or in a small unstable group that forms and dissolves within hours or days. The main durable bond is between a mother and her calf during the first year of life.

## 5. Brain, Senses, and Perception

Humpbacks perceive their world primarily through sound. Water carries acoustic energy far more efficiently than light, and the whale's hearing is its dominant sense. Low-frequency sounds, including components of male song, can travel great distances underwater, and humpbacks both produce and detect a wide range of frequencies. Unlike toothed whales, humpbacks are not known to use echolocation; they are listeners and broadcasters rather than biological sonar operators.

Vision is functional but secondary, useful at close range and near the surface. Touch matters in social contexts, including physical contact between mother and calf and during competitive interactions among males. The whale's large brain and complex behavior have prompted much interest in its cognition, but claims about intelligence should be grounded in observed behavior — social learning, flexible feeding, and vocal culture — rather than assumptions drawn from brain size alone.

## 6. Feeding Behavior

Humpbacks are lunge-feeding filter feeders. They target dense patches of krill and small schooling fish such as herring, sand lance, and capelin, engulfing huge mouthfuls of prey-laden water and then expelling the water through their baleen while retaining the food.

Their most celebrated technique is bubble-net feeding. One or more whales dive beneath a prey school and release a rising ring or curtain of bubbles that concentrates the fish into a tight ball. The whales then surge upward through the middle with mouths open. In some regions this is highly cooperative, with individuals taking consistent roles — one blowing the net, others vocalizing or herding — a striking example of coordinated foraging.

A particularly important case for ethology is "lobtail feeding," documented in the Gulf of Maine. Before bubble-netting, some whales strike the surface with the tail, a behavior thought to help manage prey. Long-term study by Allen and colleagues found that this innovation spread through the population in a pattern best explained by social learning, whale copying whale, offering rare evidence of a foraging tradition transmitted culturally in a wild baleen whale.

## 7. Social Behavior

Humpback social life is defined by fluidity. Rather than living in stable pods with lasting membership, humpbacks form short-term associations that assemble and break apart depending on context. Two whales feeding together one week may never associate again. This contrasts sharply with the stable, kin-based social groups of some toothed whales.

The clearest lasting bond is between mother and calf. On breeding grounds, temporary groupings form around receptive females, including competitive groups of males. On feeding grounds, cooperative aggregations can develop where prey is concentrated, and in some populations these feeding partnerships show a degree of consistency. Cooperation in humpbacks appears to be flexible and context-dependent, driven by immediate ecological advantage — such as the efficiency of group bubble-netting — rather than by permanent social structure.

## 8. Communication

Communication in humpbacks is largely acoustic, and their most famous signal is song. Male humpbacks produce long, structured sequences of sounds organized into repeating units, phrases, and themes. Roger Payne and Scott McVay first analyzed this structure scientifically, showing that humpback song has a hierarchical, patterned organization rather than being random noise. Within a population, males tend to sing broadly the same song at any given time.

Remarkably, the song is not fixed. It changes gradually through a season and can shift dramatically between years. Research in the South Pacific, notably by Michael Noad, Ellen Garland, and colleagues, documented song "revolutions": a new song type arising in one population and then sweeping eastward across the region as males in successive populations adopt it. This is one of the best-documented examples of cultural transmission on such a scale in any non-human animal.

Beyond song, humpbacks use non-song social sounds, especially on feeding grounds and among mothers and calves, which may communicate quietly to avoid detection. Physical displays — breaching, and slapping the surface with pectoral fins or tail — also carry information, though their precise meanings are still being studied.

## 9. Reproduction and Courtship

Humpbacks breed in warm, low-latitude waters during the cooler months, having migrated there from distant feeding grounds. The mating system is best described as one of intense male competition combined with female choice, without lasting pair bonds.

On breeding grounds, males form competitive groups around a female, jostling, displaying, and sometimes striking one another as they vie for position closest to her. Song plays a central but still-debated role in this system. The leading hypothesis is that song functions in sexual selection — attracting females, signaling to rival males, or both — but the evidence does not yet allow a single confident conclusion, and researchers remain appropriately cautious about its exact purpose. Females are selective, and the reproductive payoff of any male tactic is difficult to measure directly at sea.

## 10. Parenting and Early Development

A female humpback typically gives birth to a single calf, usually every two to three years, after roughly eleven to twelve months of gestation. Calves are born in the warm breeding grounds, where the absence of large predators and the mild water favor a vulnerable newborn.

The mother-calf bond is the strongest and most durable relationship in humpback society. The calf nurses on extremely rich milk and grows rapidly, building the reserves it will need for the long migration to the feeding grounds. Mothers and calves often communicate using quiet sounds, which may help them stay together while reducing the risk of drawing predators or harassing males. Sometimes a lone adult male, called an escort, accompanies a mother and calf. The calf remains dependent through its first year, gradually learning to feed and navigate the migratory route, after which it becomes independent.

## 11. Learning and Intelligence

The humpback provides some of the strongest evidence for culture in any baleen whale. Two lines of behavior stand out. First, the song revolutions of the South Pacific show that males acquire and modify complex vocal sequences by copying others, and that these learned songs spread across whole populations. Second, the spread of lobtail feeding in the Gulf of Maine, analyzed by Allen and colleagues, indicates that a novel foraging behavior can be transmitted socially through a population.

Together these show learning, memory, behavioral flexibility, and social transmission operating at a level once thought exclusive to a few highly social mammals. It is important, however, not to overstate the case. Evidence of social learning and cultural spread is not the same as evidence of human-like reasoning or self-awareness. The honest position is that humpbacks are behaviorally sophisticated cultural learners, with cognitive abilities that remain only partly understood.

## 12. Fear, Stress, and Defense

The main natural predator of the humpback is the killer whale, or orca, which most often targets calves and vulnerable individuals rather than healthy adults. Many humpbacks bear tooth-rake scars from orca encounters, evidence of attacks survived. Adult humpbacks are large and powerful, and their long pectoral fins and tail can be formidable weapons; a defending adult may position itself between an attacker and a calf and strike out.

Humpbacks are also frequently reported intervening in orca attacks on other species, sometimes charging toward hunting orcas and disrupting them even when the prey is a seal or another whale species. Pitman and colleagues compiled many such observations. These events are real and repeatable, but their interpretation is genuinely uncertain, and they are discussed further in the myths section below. Beyond predators, modern humpbacks face human-caused stressors including entanglement in fishing gear, ship strikes, and ocean noise.

## 13. Human Interaction

The humpback's relationship with humans is a story of near-destruction followed by recovery. Commercial whaling in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries killed vast numbers of humpbacks, driving many populations to a small fraction of their original size. International protection, beginning in the mid-twentieth century and strengthened by later whaling moratoria, allowed most populations to rebound substantially — one of the clearest large-scale conservation successes for any marine mammal.

Today humpbacks are central to whale-watching economies worldwide, generating public interest and revenue that support conservation. They are also cultural icons, their song embedded in music and environmental campaigns. Yet significant threats remain: entanglement in fishing lines, collisions with ships, chemical and noise pollution, and the shifting distribution of prey under climate change. The recovery is real but not complete, and it depends on continued management.

## 14. Common Myths vs Scientific Reality

Myth 1: We know exactly what whale song is "for."
Scientific reality: Humpback song is well described but its precise function is still debated. The strongest hypothesis is that it serves in sexual selection — a male display directed at females, rival males, or both — but the evidence does not yet support a single confident answer, and honest science leaves the question partly open.

Myth 2: When humpbacks disrupt orca hunts, they are acting from human-like morality.
Scientific reality: Humpbacks do interfere with killer whale attacks, sometimes protecting other species, and this is genuinely documented. But interpreting it as deliberate altruism or moral concern goes beyond the evidence. It may reflect a generalized anti-predator response toward orcas that sometimes incidentally helps other animals. The behavior is real; the human-like motive is unproven.

Myth 3: All whales sing.
Scientific reality: Long, structured song of the kind the humpback is famous for is characteristic of humpback males, not of whales in general. Many whale species produce sounds, but the elaborate, culturally evolving humpback song is a distinctive trait, not a universal one.

Myth 4: Whales are a kind of fish.
Scientific reality: Whales are mammals. They breathe air with lungs, are warm-blooded, give birth to live young, and nurse them with milk. The humpback's fish-like shape is convergent evolution for life in water, not evidence of a fish ancestry.

Myth 5: Humpbacks live in stable, lifelong pods like some other whales.
Scientific reality: Humpback associations are mostly fluid and short-term, forming and dissolving over hours or days. The durable bond is between mother and calf. Stable, lifelong matrilineal groups are a feature of certain toothed whales, not of humpbacks.

## 15. Comparison With Another Species

A revealing comparison is the humpback whale versus the killer whale, or orca (*Orcinus orca*). Both are large, intelligent, ocean-wide marine mammals, and the two interact directly as predator and, at times, defender — yet their biology and social lives differ profoundly.

The humpback is a baleen whale, a filter feeder that strains huge quantities of tiny prey through baleen plates. The orca is a toothed whale, an apex predator that hunts fish, seals, and other marine mammals, including humpback calves. In feeding, one harvests biomass in bulk; the other is a coordinated hunter of large prey.

Their social structures differ just as sharply. Humpbacks form loose, short-term associations, with the mother-calf bond as the main lasting tie and no stable pod membership. Many orca populations, by contrast, live in stable matrilines — tight, long-lasting groups organized around related females, sometimes staying together for life and maintaining group-specific vocal dialects. So while both species show culture and social learning, they express it through opposite social systems: the humpback through fluid associations and a shared, spreading song culture, the orca through enduring kin groups with their own traditions. The comparison highlights that intelligence and culture in whales are not tied to any single kind of society.

## 16. Ethology Lessons

The humpback teaches several broad lessons about animal behavior. First, culture is not unique to primates or to a handful of social mammals. The spread of song types across South Pacific populations and the transmission of lobtail feeding in the Gulf of Maine show that learned traditions can move through a wild whale population, reshaping our understanding of where culture can arise.

Second, complex, coordinated behavior does not require a complex, permanent society. Humpbacks cooperate impressively in bubble-net feeding while living in largely fluid associations, showing that flexible, situation-specific cooperation is a viable strategy.

Third, honest ethology means resisting easy stories. The humpback's interference with orca hunts is fascinating precisely because its cause is uncertain; labeling it "heroism" would replace evidence with narrative. Finally, the humpback's recovery from whaling demonstrates that behavioral and population resilience, given protection, can reverse even severe human damage — a lesson with direct conservation value.

## 17. Glossary

- **Baleen**: Fringed keratin plates in the mouth of mysticete whales, used to filter small prey from seawater.
- **Filter feeding**: A feeding mode in which an animal strains large quantities of small prey from water rather than capturing prey individually.
- **Bubble-net feeding**: A technique in which one or more humpbacks release rings of bubbles to concentrate prey before lunging through the school.
- **Lobtail feeding**: A feeding behavior involving striking the water surface with the tail before bubble-netting; documented to spread by social learning in the Gulf of Maine.
- **Song**: A long, hierarchically structured sequence of sounds produced by male humpbacks, organized into units, phrases, and themes.
- **Song revolution**: A rapid, wholesale change in the song type of a population, which can then spread to neighboring populations.
- **Cultural transmission**: The spread of learned behavior from one individual to another, and through populations, rather than by genetic inheritance.
- **Migration**: The regular, long-distance movement between distant regions — here, between high-latitude feeding grounds and tropical breeding grounds.
- **Breaching**: Leaping partly or fully out of the water and falling back, a conspicuous surface behavior.
- **Rorqual**: A member of the whale family Balaenopteridae, characterized by expandable throat pleats used in lunge feeding.
- **Pectoral fin**: The long forelimb-derived flipper of the humpback, exceptionally long relative to body size.
- **Breeding grounds**: Warm, low-latitude waters where humpbacks mate and give birth.
- **Feeding grounds**: Cold, productive high-latitude waters where humpbacks forage and build energy reserves.
- **Fluid association**: A social grouping that forms and dissolves over short timescales, rather than a stable, lasting group.
- **Mysticete**: A baleen whale; a member of the suborder Mysticeti.
