# Summary — The Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*)

The humpback whale is a large baleen whale — a filter feeder that strains small schooling fish and krill from seawater through fringed keratin plates — found in every major ocean. It belongs to the rorqual family, whose expandable throat pleats allow enormous mouthfuls of prey-laden water to be engulfed and then pushed back out through the baleen. Adults reach roughly 12 to 16 meters, and the species is famous for its exceptionally long pectoral fins, reflected in the genus name *Megaptera*, meaning "big wing."

Three behaviors make the humpback central to the study of animal behavior. The first is male song: long, hierarchically structured sequences of sound, first analyzed scientifically by Roger Payne and Scott McVay. This song is not fixed. It changes through a season and can change dramatically between years, and research in the South Pacific by Ellen Garland, Michael Noad, and colleagues has documented song "revolutions" that sweep across populations — one of the clearest large-scale examples of cultural transmission in any non-human animal. The likely function of song is sexual selection, a male display to females, rival males, or both, but the exact purpose remains genuinely debated.

The second is cooperative feeding. Humpbacks use bubble nets, releasing rising rings of bubbles to concentrate prey before lunging up through the school, sometimes in coordinated groups with consistent roles. In the Gulf of Maine, long-term study by Jenny Allen and colleagues showed that a feeding innovation called lobtail feeding spread through the population in a pattern best explained by social learning — rare evidence of a foraging tradition transmitted culturally in a wild baleen whale.

The third is migration. Humpbacks undertake one of the longest migrations of any mammal, traveling between cold, productive high-latitude feeding grounds and warm tropical breeding grounds where they mate and calve. Along the way they perform conspicuous surface behaviors — breaching, and slapping the surface with their pectoral fins or tail.

Humpback society is fluid. Rather than living in stable pods, humpbacks form short-term associations that assemble and dissolve over hours or days, with the mother-calf bond as the main lasting tie. Humpbacks are also frequently seen disrupting killer whale attacks, sometimes on other species; these events are real but should be interpreted cautiously, possibly as a generalized anti-predator response rather than proven altruism.

Once devastated by commercial whaling, most humpback populations have recovered substantially under international protection, and the species is now assessed as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, though entanglement, ship strikes, noise, and climate-driven shifts in prey remain real threats. The humpback teaches that culture, cooperation, and behavioral sophistication can arise even in a filter-feeding whale with a loose social structure.
