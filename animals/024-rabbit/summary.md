# Summary — The European Rabbit (*Oryctolagus cuniculus*)

The European rabbit is one of the most instructive prey animals in ethology and one of the most misunderstood companion animals. It is the only truly colonial lagomorph — the single rabbit or hare that lives in permanent, socially organized underground warrens — and it is the wild ancestor of every domestic rabbit breed. Native to the Iberian Peninsula and nearby areas, it has been carried by humans across much of the world, yet on the IUCN Red List it is assessed as Endangered (2019), driven by steep declines in its native Iberian range.

Almost every feature of the rabbit can be read as an answer to predation. Its eyes sit high and wide for a near-panoramic view of the sky, tuned to detect movement rather than fine detail, with a blind spot just in front of the nose. Large mobile ears locate faint sounds, and a keen nose reads a rich chemical world. The rabbit is crepuscular, feeding at dawn and dusk and retreating to the warren by day. Its defenses run in sequence: vigilance and freezing, a hind-foot thump that alarms the colony, then an explosive zig-zag sprint to the nearest bolt-hole, with the warren itself as the ultimate refuge.

As a grazer on tough grasses, the rabbit relies on cecotrophy: microbes in its large cecum ferment cellulose, and the rabbit reingests the resulting soft, nutrient-rich droppings, called cecotropes, to pass food through the gut a second time and absorb what the microbes made. This lets it crop grass quickly in the open and finish digestion safely underground.

Rabbit society is genuinely complex. Colonies are organized by dominance hierarchies in which rank decides access to the safest breeding burrows and to mates. Status is signaled largely by scent, especially chinning — rubbing the chin gland on objects — along with urine and communal latrines. Reproduction is built for volume: the rabbit is an induced ovulator, releasing eggs in response to mating, and with a short gestation it breeds prolifically. The doe raises altricial young — blind, naked kits — in a hidden nesting burrow, visiting to nurse only briefly, usually once a day, so as not to betray the nest.

The rabbit's relationship with humans is contradictory. It is a beloved but chronically misunderstood pet, often kept alone in cramped conditions despite being a social, active, long-lived species. Ecologically it is both a devastating invasive pest in Australia, where it was freed from natural controls, and a declining keystone prey in Iberia, where its collapse threatens specialist predators such as the Iberian lynx. The European rabbit shows how deeply predation shapes a mind and a society, and how easily people misread an animal.
