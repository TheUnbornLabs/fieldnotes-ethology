# Summary — The Dragonfly

The dragonfly (suborder **Anisoptera**, order Odonata) is one of the most accomplished aerial predators on Earth and one of the most instructive animals in ethology. With roughly 3,000 species worldwide, breeding wherever there is fresh water, dragonflies are obligate carnivores at every life stage. The adult is a short-lived (weeks to months) flying hunter; the aquatic **nymph** stage, which can last from months to several years, is a formidable predator in its own right.

What makes the dragonfly remarkable is *how* it hunts. Rather than chasing prey directly, it uses **predictive interception**: it steers toward the point where the prey will be, holding the target on a constant bearing so the two flight paths converge. Motion-capture studies (Leonardo, Mischiati, and colleagues) show that hunting dragonflies adjust their own body rotation and steering to keep prey fixed in a preferred part of the eye and to line up an intercept — behavior that implies an **internal model** of both the target and the dragonfly's own state. The payoff is a very high capture success rate, achieved by a control strategy engineers associate with guided missiles.

The dragonfly's vision is elite. Each **compound eye** can contain up to about 30,000 **ommatidia**, and in many species the two eyes wrap around the head for nearly panoramic coverage. The upper eye region is tuned to detect small dark targets against the sky, and dragonflies see across a very broad spectrum, including **ultraviolet** and **polarized** light. In the brain, small target motion detector neurons perform **selective attention**, locking onto one target and suppressing competitors in a cluttered scene (Wiederman & O'Carroll). Four **independently controlled wings** give hovering, backward flight, and sharp turns.

Before it flies, the dragonfly lives underwater as a nymph that ambushes aquatic prey — including tadpoles and small fish — by shooting out an extensible **labial mask**, and escapes by **jet propulsion**, expelling water from **rectal gills**. Development is gradual (**hemimetabolous**), with no pupal stage; the winged adult emerges from the final nymph.

Adult life centers on reproduction. Males are often **territorial**, defending shoreline or airspace, and mating involves the distinctive **wheel position** and intense **sperm competition**, with male genitalia shaped to remove rival sperm and males guarding females during egg-laying. Dragonflies also thermoregulate through posture, including the **obelisk** stance. Some, like the globe skimmer *Pantala flavescens*, are champion **migrants**, undertaking one of the longest known insect migrations.

Contrary to folklore, dragonflies do not sting, are not venomous, and are harmless to humans; they do not live only a day; and far from being weak fliers, they are among the most agile fliers alive. As predators sensitive to water quality, dragonflies are valuable indicators of freshwater ecosystem health.
