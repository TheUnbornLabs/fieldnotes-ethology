# Summary — Termites: Blind Architects and Fungus Farmers

Termites are eusocial insects that, despite the old nickname "white ants," are not ants at all. They are essentially social cockroaches, now placed within the order Blattodea, and their society evolved independently of the ants, bees, and wasps — a classic case of convergent evolution. About 3,000 species live across the world's tropics and warm regions, where most are quiet, essential decomposers and soil engineers rather than the destructive pests of popular imagination.

A termite colony is founded by a single pair — a king and a queen — who, unlike the short-lived males of bees and ants, both survive and reproduce together for years, sometimes decades. Because termites are ordinary diploid insects rather than haplodiploid like the Hymenoptera, their sterile worker and soldier castes include both males and females. Workers forage, build, tend brood, and cultivate fungus; soldiers defend the nest with armoured jaws or, in some lineages, a nozzle-like head that sprays defensive chemicals. Caste is often not fixed at birth: developmental flexibility lets the colony adjust its workforce to changing needs.

Termites are functionally blind and live in a world of chemistry, touch, and vibration. Pheromones organise foraging, reproduction, and caste; trophallaxis — the mouth-to-mouth and hindgut exchange of fluid — spreads food, chemical signals, and essential gut microbes through the colony; and vibrational head-banging carries urgent alarm signals through the galleries. Communication and social contact are so central that a young termite cannot even digest its food without acquiring gut microbes from nestmates.

That dependence points to one of the termites' great evolutionary tricks: they cannot digest wood alone. Lower termites rely on symbiotic protists and bacteria in the hindgut; higher termites depend on bacteria, and the fungus-growing Macrotermitinae cultivate *Termitomyces* fungus on spongy combs that break down tough plant material for them.

Perhaps the most famous termite achievement is architecture. The towering mounds of *Macrotermes* are not solid homes or the queen's palace but largely above-ground structures that manage gas exchange and buffer temperature and humidity for the nest below — a system whose ventilation mechanism has been refined by research such as J. Scott Turner's. No termite plans the mound. It self-organises through stigmergy: individuals respond to local cues left by earlier work, and complex structure emerges from simple rules with no blueprint and no foreman. This makes the nest a kind of "extended organism" and the colony a model of swarm intelligence.

Ecologically, termites are among the planet's great decomposers, recycling dead plant matter and engineering the soil and fertility of savannas and forests. Understood properly, they are less a pest than a keystone of tropical ecosystems.
