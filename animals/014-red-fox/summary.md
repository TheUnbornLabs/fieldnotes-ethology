# Summary — The Red Fox (*Vulpes vulpes*)

The red fox is the most widespread wild carnivore on Earth, native across the entire Northern Hemisphere and introduced to Australia, where it is a damaging invasive predator. A small member of the dog family — usually 3 to 7 kilograms, with a slender body, pointed muzzle, large ears, and a bushy tail — it thrives in an exceptional range of habitats: forest, tundra, desert, farmland, and, increasingly, the heart of major cities. On the IUCN Red List it is assessed as Least Concern, one of the few wild carnivores whose status is genuinely secure.

The fox's success rests on flexibility rather than specialisation. It is a dietary generalist, eating whatever is abundant: voles, mice, rabbits, birds, insects, earthworms, carrion, fruit, and human food waste. Unlike the wolf, which hunts large prey in a cooperative pack, the fox forages largely alone, taking small, scattered items one at a time. Its signature hunting move is the "mouse pounce" — locating hidden prey by sound, then springing high and driving down forepaws-first. A study by Cerveny and colleagues reported that foxes tend to align these pounces along the north-south magnetic axis, hinting at a magnetic sense; this is an intriguing but debated finding that awaits confirmation. When a fox catches more than it can eat, it caches the surplus by scatter-hoarding — burying food in many dispersed spots and later relocating it by memory and smell.

Foxes are not truly solitary. They organise into family groups that hold and defend territories, most often a breeding pair (a dog fox and a vixen), sometimes joined by one or more non-breeding adult females — frequently the pair's own daughters — that stay on as helpers and assist in rearing cubs. Communication is rich: scent-marking maps the territory, while a wide vocal repertoire includes soft contact calls, the eerie vixen "scream" of the winter breeding season, and rapid "gekkering" in close disputes. Foxes are chiefly crepuscular and nocturnal. Cubs are born blind and helpless in a den in spring, provisioned by adults and any helpers, and most disperse by autumn.

Among wild animals, the fox lives in unusually close contact with people. Its colonisation of towns and cities makes it a classic synanthrope, and many urban foxes are healthy residents rather than sick strays. The species is also central to the long-running Russian farm-fox experiment begun by Dmitry Belyaev and continued by Lyudmila Trut, in which selecting silver foxes for tameness alone produced a cascade of correlated changes — floppy ears, curled tails, patchy coats — known as "domestication syndrome," offering rare insight into how the wolf became the dog.

The red fox's central ethological lesson is that generalism and behavioural flexibility, not specialisation, can be the winning strategy — and that an animal can forage alone yet still live within a cooperative, territory-holding family.
