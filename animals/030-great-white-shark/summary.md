# Book 030 — The Great White Shark: Summary

The great white shark (*Carcharodon carcharias*) is a large mackerel shark (family Lamnidae) and one of the ocean's most studied apex predators. Despite a fearsome public image, its documented behavior is precise, exploratory, and flexible rather than mindlessly aggressive. It ranges nearly worldwide in temperate coastal and offshore seas, with well-known concentrations off South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, Mexico's Guadalupe Island, and both U.S. coasts. Adults commonly reach 4–6 metres, grow slowly, mature late, and can live for decades.

A defining trait is regional endothermy. Networks of blood vessels called the rete mirabile trap metabolic heat and keep the swimming muscles, gut, and brain warmer than the surrounding water. This warm-bodied physiology powers fast bursts, efficient digestion, and quick sensory processing in cold, prey-rich seas, and it explains why the white shark can specialize on agile, high-value marine mammals.

Its hunting is guided by a layered sensory suite. At long range, acute smell and low-frequency hearing draw the shark toward prey and carrion. At medium range, the lateral line detects water movement and vision refines the approach. At the moment of the strike, the ampullae of Lorenzini — jelly-filled pores around the snout — detect the weak electric fields of living animals, guiding the bite even when vision fails. The signature attack on a seal or sea lion is an ambush launched vertically from below, exploiting the prey's silhouette against the bright surface; off some coasts this produces dramatic breaching.

Bites on humans are rare and usually not completed predatory attacks. Many appear to be single exploratory "test" bites after which the shark departs, consistent with the finding that humans are not preferred prey. White sharks are largely solitary but tolerate one another at feeding sites, where a loose, size-based dominance order and give-way behavior help dangerous animals avoid injury. They undertake long migrations, including offshore aggregations such as the Pacific "White Shark Café," and they flee areas where killer whales appear — orcas being documented predators of white sharks that feed selectively on their livers.

Reproduction is slow: aplacental viviparity in which embryos are nourished by yolk and by oophagy (eating unfertilized eggs in the uterus), producing a few large, fully independent pups after a long gestation, with females reproducing only every few years. This low output, combined with late maturity, makes the species Vulnerable (IUCN) and slow to recover from finning, bycatch, and culls.

Several myths deserve correction: white sharks are not human-hunting man-eaters; not all sharks must swim to breathe (whites are ram ventilators, but many sharks can pump water while resting); sharks are not immune to cancer or disease; and they are not primitive, unintelligent fish. Ethologically, the white shark shows how sensory physiology shapes strategy, how a non-mammalian brain can still support learning and social nuance, and how apex predators can be both powerful and fragile.
