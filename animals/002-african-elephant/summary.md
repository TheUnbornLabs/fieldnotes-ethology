# Summary — The African Elephant (*Loxodonta africana*)

The African savanna elephant is the largest living land animal, but its scientific interest lies mostly in its behavior. It lives in a fluid, multi-tiered society organized around long-lived females, communicates partly below the range of human hearing, and carries a spatial and social memory that shapes how whole families survive on a changeable landscape. Decades of continuous field study, especially at Amboseli in Kenya, have made it one of the best-documented wild mammals.

The core of elephant society is the family unit: a matriarch — usually the oldest female — together with her daughters and their dependent calves. These units repeatedly split and merge with related families depending on food, water, danger, and season, a pattern called fission-fusion. The matriarch functions as a store of ecological and social knowledge; older, more experienced matriarchs have been linked to better group decisions in risky situations. Males take a different path, drifting away from the natal family as they mature to live alone or in loose male associations, joining females mainly to mate.

Communication is layered. Low-frequency rumbles, many with infrasonic components, carry over long distances, and elephants also detect seismic vibrations through sensitive receptors in the feet and trunk. Chemical signals from urine, dung, and glands — including the temporal gland — convey identity and reproductive state, while touch and posture handle close-range interaction. Smell is a dominant sense, supported by an unusually large set of functional olfactory genes, and the trunk unites touch, smell, and precise manipulation in a single organ.

Reproduction is slow and heavily invested. Gestation lasts about 22 months, the longest of any land mammal, and a single large calf remains dependent for years. Care is shared through allomothering, in which young females and other relatives help guard and retrieve calves. Adult bulls periodically enter musth, a hormonally driven state that shapes male mating success.

Elephants show strong long-term memory, flexible problem-solving, social learning, and some tool use; mirror self-recognition has been reported in elephants and is cited cautiously. Their responses to dead companions — investigating and touching bodies and bones — are real and repeatable but should be described without assuming human-style grief.

Humans dominate the elephant's modern story. Crop-raiding and other human-elephant conflict occur at the farming frontier, ivory poaching has driven major declines, and habitat loss fragments movement corridors. The IUCN Red List (2021 assessment, to be verified) treats the African savanna elephant as Endangered. As a keystone species and ecosystem engineer, the elephant reshapes vegetation and water access for many other species, tying its behavior directly to the health of African landscapes.
