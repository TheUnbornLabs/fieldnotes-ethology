# Summary — The Horse (*Equus caballus*)

The horse is a large grazing prey animal, and nearly everything about its behavior follows from that fact. Unlike a predator, it is not built to attack but to detect danger early and flee. This "flight response" — moving away first from anything sudden or unexpected, then assessing — is the horse's primary defense, supported by constant vigilance that is often shared across a group so not every animal must watch at once.

The horse's senses are those of an open-country prey animal. Its eyes, set on the sides of the head, give a field of view of about 350 degrees, close to panoramic, but with two blind spots: directly in front of the nose and directly behind the tail. Most of this field is monocular, giving wide coverage but poor depth perception; only a narrow zone straight ahead is binocular, which is why a horse turns its head to judge distance. Horses are dichromatic, seeing mainly blues and yellows — different from human vision, but not colorblind. Hearing is acute, and the large, independently mobile ears both locate sounds and serve as social signals. The horse's face is expressive enough that researchers built EquiFACS, a coding system for its facial muscle movements, now used in welfare and pain assessment.

Horses are intensely social and do not thrive alone. Their natural unit is the band: commonly a harem-type group of mares and their young accompanied by a stallion, with displaced young males forming bachelor bands. Their social life is widely misread through the language of a rigid "alpha" or "lead mare." In reality, dominance relationships (who yields over a resource) are fluid and are not the same as leadership (who influences where the group moves), and leadership can be shared rather than fixed in one individual. Bonds are maintained by proximity and by mutual grooming (allogrooming), which can lower heart rate.

As a hindgut fermenter, the horse digests fibrous grass slowly and must graze in many small meals across the day and night. Mismatches between this evolved pattern and modern feeding, housing, and weaning underlie several health and behavior problems.

The horse is also one of history's most important domesticated animals. Genetic research associated with Ludovic Orlando, Pablo Librado, and colleagues points to a lineage often called DOM2, arising on the western Eurasian steppes and spreading from roughly 4,200 years ago — a finding best presented cautiously as an active field. Modern equine science favors training built on how horses actually learn and on respecting their senses and fear system, rather than on "dominating" the animal. The horse's central lesson is how thoroughly a prey way of life can organize an animal from its eyes to its social bonds.
