# Summary — The Praying Mantis

The praying mantis is a common name for the insect order Mantodea, roughly 2,400 species of predatory insects found on every continent except Antarctica. Its most famous feature — the folded, prayer-like forelegs — is not a devotional or restful pose but the cocked, spring-loaded ready position of its raptorial forelegs, which snap shut on prey in a few hundredths of a second. The mantis is a sit-and-wait ambush predator: it conserves energy by staying hidden and motionless, then captures prey that wanders within reach with a single explosive strike. Its diet is broad, spanning flies, moths, bees, other insects, spiders, and occasionally small vertebrates.

The mantis is perceptually exceptional. It has two large, forward-facing compound eyes, each with a fovea for sharp inspection, set on a highly mobile head. Because these eyes share an overlapping field of view, the mantis can judge distance by comparing the images they receive — and it is the only insect experimentally shown to possess true stereoscopic three-dimensional vision. This was proven directly by Vivek Nityananda, Jenny Read, and colleagues, who fitted mantises with tiny "insect 3-D glasses" and showed them illusory prey; the mantises struck at empty space when the 3-D illusion placed a virtual target within range. Notably, this stereopsis appears tuned to motion, a computationally efficient strategy for catching moving prey.

The mantis also carries a single mid-line "cyclopean" ear tuned to the ultrasound of hunting bats. It cannot locate the direction of a sound, but simply detecting a bat is enough to trigger a rapid evasive dive in flight. Defensively, mantises rely first on crypsis — camouflage as leaves, bark, grass, or flowers — and, if discovered, on deimatic startle displays in which they rear up, spread their wings and forelegs, and flash bright colors. The orchid mantis takes camouflage further into aggressive mimicry: its flower-like form appears to actively attract pollinating insects as prey.

Mantis reproduction features the famously misunderstood behavior of sexual cannibalism, in which a female may eat the male during or after mating. The reality is nuanced: it is not universal or inevitable, varies by species, occurs mainly when the female is hungry or disturbed, was likely exaggerated by cramped laboratory conditions, and can boost egg output by providing nutrition. Females package their eggs in a frothy, hardened case called an ootheca and provide no further care.

Largely solitary, cognitively narrow, and harmless to humans, the mantis teaches enduring ethological lessons: that natural selection builds specialists, that perception can be the essence of behavior, and that dramatic single-cause stories about animals deserve careful scrutiny.
