# Summary — The Domestic Cat

The domestic cat (*Felis catus*) is one of the world's most abundant carnivores and one of ethology's most instructive cases of light domestication. It descends from the African wildcat (*Felis lybica*), a strictly solitary, crepuscular ambush hunter of rodents across North Africa and the Near East. The cat is widely thought to have largely self-domesticated: when early farming communities began storing grain around ten thousand years ago, they created dense rodent populations, and the boldest, least fearful wildcats exploited this food around settlements. This commensal relationship — rodent control for the humans, reliable prey for the cats — favored tameness without deliberately reshaping the animal. As a result, the domestic cat remains behaviorally very close to its wild ancestor.

At its core the cat is a small obligate carnivore, requiring nutrients such as taurine that only meat supplies. It hunts alone using a stalk-and-ambush sequence ending in a nape bite, taking many small prey rather than occasional large ones, and its hunting drive is partly independent of hunger — which is why play matters for indoor welfare. Its senses are those of a low-light predator: the tapetum lucidum enhances night vision, hearing extends into the ultrasonic range to detect rodent squeaks, and the vibrissae (whiskers) map close surroundings in darkness.

Socially, the cat is not a strict loner but facultatively social. Where food is dispersed it lives alone on a defended range; where food is concentrated it forms matrilineal colonies of related females that share space, groom, and sometimes nurse kittens communally. Much of its communication has been redirected toward humans: adult cats rarely meow at one another but do so at people, and they use the tail-up greeting, allorubbing and bunting, scent and spray marking, purring (which also signals stress or injury, not only contentment), and the affiliative slow-blink.

Cats are prolific breeders — seasonally polyestrous, promiscuous, and induced ovulators with no paternal care — allowing colonies to grow quickly where resources allow. Kittens are born helpless and pass through an early socialization window (roughly two to seven weeks) that shapes how tame and confident they become.

The cat's relationship with humans raises real questions. Free-ranging and feral cats are effective predators and a genuine conservation concern, having contributed to island extinctions. Indoor life protects wildlife and the cat but requires environmental enrichment to prevent stress. For unowned populations, trap-neuter-return is a widely used non-lethal management tool, though its effectiveness is debated. Studying the cat reveals how an animal can become tame and abundant in human company while remaining, at heart, a solitary hunter.
