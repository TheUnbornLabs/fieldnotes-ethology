## Well-established sources (safe to name)

- The Kalahari Meerkat Project — the long-term field study of wild, individually known meerkats associated with Tim Clutton-Brock and collaborators. A foundational body of research on meerkat cooperative breeding, reproductive conflict, and life history. Named here in general terms; specific findings should be traced to their individual publications and verified before final publication.
- Marta Manser and colleagues — research programme on meerkat vocal communication, including graded and functionally referential alarm calls and individual vocal recognition. Named generally; specific papers should be verified before final publication.
- Alex Thornton and colleagues — research on teaching in meerkats through graded, age-matched prey-provisioning of pups. Named generally; specific papers should be verified before final publication.
- IUCN Red List — species assessment for *Suricata suricatta* (global status: Least Concern). Consult the current Red List entry for range, population trend, and threats.

- Standard animal-behavior textbooks (e.g. Alcock, *Animal Behavior*) for general ethological concepts such as cooperative breeding, kin selection, alloparental care, and referential communication.

## Verified (checked 2026-07-02)

- Scientific name ***Suricata suricatta***; order Carnivora, family **Herpestidae** (mongooses); only member of genus *Suricata* — confirmed.
- Southern-African Kalahari range (South Africa, Botswana, Namibia, parts of Angola); arid/semi-arid habitat — confirmed.
- **IUCN Red List: Least Concern** — confirmed (wide range, stable populations).
- **Intraspecific lethal violence:** meerkats show the highest recorded rate among the mammals surveyed — about **19.4% of deaths caused by conspecifics** — in Gómez, Verdú, González-Megías & Méndez (2016), "The phylogenetic roots of human lethal violence," *Nature* 538:233–237. This exceeds lions (~13.3%) and gray wolves (~12.8%). Confirmed.
