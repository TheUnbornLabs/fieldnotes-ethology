# Summary — Book 034: The Green Sea Turtle

The green sea turtle (*Chelonia mydas*) is a long-lived marine reptile in the family Cheloniidae, found in tropical and subtropical seas worldwide and assessed globally as **Least Concern** on the IUCN Red List (downlisted from Endangered in 2025 after decades of recovery, though several regional subpopulations remain threatened). In ethology it is best known for **natal homing**: females that hatched on a particular beach may spend decades feeding far out across an ocean basin, then return as adults to nest on or very near the shore where they first entered the sea. Explaining how an animal remembers a beach across such distances and time is one of the field's most striking navigation puzzles.

The leading explanation involves **magnetoreception** and **geomagnetic imprinting**. Turtles can sense the Earth's magnetic field, apparently reading both its intensity and inclination, which vary with location and so provide a magnetic "map" and "compass." Kenneth Lohmann's laboratory research on sea turtles laid much of the groundwork for the idea that a hatchling imprints on the magnetic signature of its natal region and uses that memory to home as an adult. Near shore the turtle also relies on light, beach slope, chemical cues, and vision; the magnetic map guides the open-ocean legs in between.

The species has an unusual **ontogenetic diet shift**. Young pelagic juveniles are omnivorous, leaning carnivorous, taking jellyfish and small invertebrates, while adults become **largely herbivorous**, grazing seagrass and algae — the only sea turtle to do so extensively. This grazing helps keep seagrass meadows healthy, making the green turtle a keystone grazer in coastal ecosystems. (Its name comes from the greenish fat produced by this plant diet, not the shell colour.)

Reproduction is seasonal and offers no parental care. A female comes ashore at night, digs a chamber, lays a **clutch** of about a hundred eggs, camouflages the nest, and returns to the sea. Green turtles show **temperature-dependent sex determination**: incubation temperature sets each embryo's sex, with **warmer sand producing more females** — a serious concern as climate warming skews sex ratios. After about two months, hatchlings emerge together at night in a **frenzy** that swamps predators, orient to the sea using light and slope, then take up a magnetic map offshore. Strong **nest-site fidelity** brings females back to the same region to breed.

Green turtles carry armour that cannot be shed — the carapace is fused to the skeleton — and their vulnerable stages are the egg, hatchling, and juvenile. Major threats include fisheries bycatch, egg and meat poaching, coastal development and artificial lighting, plastic ingestion, climate change, and the tumour-causing disease **fibropapillomatosis**. Several persistent myths surround the species: that it is unintelligent, that it can leave its shell, that its salt-gland "tears" are emotional, and that sea turtles and land tortoises are interchangeable. In reality the green turtle is a specialised, ecologically important navigator whose slow life history leaves it slow to recover from human pressures.
