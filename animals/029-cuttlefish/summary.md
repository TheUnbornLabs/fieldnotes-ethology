# Summary — Book 029: The Cuttlefish

The common cuttlefish (*Sepia officinalis*) is a coastal cephalopod of the eastern Atlantic, Mediterranean, and North Sea — a mollusc, not a fish, in the same class as octopuses and squid. It is best known for carrying what may be the most sophisticated dynamic display system in nature in its skin. Three layers work together: chromatophores (muscle-controlled pigment sacs that switch colored spots on and off in a fraction of a second), leucophores (white-scattering cells that reflect ambient light), and iridophores (reflective plates that create iridescent, polarized structural colors). With these, a cuttlefish can match almost any background, raise fleshy papillae to add texture, and produce a rich vocabulary of signals.

The central paradox is that cuttlefish appear to be colorblind — the evidence points to a single type of retinal photoreceptor — yet they produce accurate color-matched camouflage. How they achieve this is an open question. They are highly sensitive to polarized light, and researchers have proposed that unusual pupil shape and strong lens chromatic aberration might let them extract some color information optically. These remain hypotheses.

Cuttlefish use their skin not only to hide but to deceive. Courtship is intensely competitive during the single spring spawning season of their short, roughly one-to-two-year life. Small "sneaker" males mimic female coloration and posture to slip past larger guarding males and mate. In a celebrated example of directed deception, a male can display two different messages at once — courtship stripes on the side facing a female and female-like colors on the side facing a rival. Such behavior shows that cuttlefish signals are targeted and tactical, not simple readouts of mood.

Cognition is genuine but should be described in proportion. In a marshmallow-style test (Schnell and colleagues), cuttlefish delayed gratification to obtain a preferred food, and better waiters performed better on a learning task. Work associated with Jozet-Alves, Clayton, and colleagues indicates cuttlefish have episodic-like memory — recalling what they ate, where, and when — that does not seem to decline with age. "Episodic-like" is the careful term.

Other hallmarks include the internal cuttlebone (a buoyancy device that is the internalized remnant of the ancestral shell), jet propulsion for escape, ambush hunting with a lightning tentacle strike often preceded by a hypnotic passing-cloud display, and a graded defense system running from camouflage through deimatic startle displays to ink and flight. The cuttlefish is semelparous, breeding once and then dying. Together these traits make it a powerful case study in how signalling, deception, and intelligence can evolve in a lineage utterly unlike our own.
