# Summary — The Lion (*Panthera leo*)

The lion is the only truly social member of the cat family. Where nearly all other cats live and hunt alone, lions organise their lives around the pride — a stable core of related females and their cubs, attended by a coalition of adult males that has usually immigrated from outside. This single exception to feline solitude makes the lion one of the most instructive species in animal behavior, because it lets biologists ask why sociality evolves in one cat and not the rest.

Prides are built on kinship among females. Sisters, mothers, and daughters typically remain together for life, hunting cooperatively, defending a shared territory, and rearing cubs communally. Males are the mobile element: a coalition competes to take over a pride and hold "tenure," the limited period during which its members father the pride's cubs. Coalitions of two or more males hold tenure longer than single males, which is the main payoff of male cooperation. Females are philopatric and stay; young males are eventually forced out to become nomads until they can win a pride of their own.

Lionesses do most of the hunting, stalking close and launching a short, powerful rush rather than a long chase. In group hunts, individuals often show consistent tendencies to take particular positions, but whether this reflects deliberate role assignment or each lioness simply responding to prey and companions is genuinely debated. Lions both hunt and scavenge, and the popular contrast between the noble lion and the thieving hyena is misleading, since both animals hunt and steal depending on circumstance.

Communication spans long and short ranges. The roar advertises presence, spaces out rivals, and helps group members locate one another; lions can even assess the number of roaring intruders and respond according to the odds. Scent-marking encodes identity and reproductive state across a territory, while close-range bonds are maintained by head-rubbing and grooming.

Reproduction is shaped by male turnover. Pride females often breed in synchrony and raise cubs together in a crèche, sharing guarding and even nursing. The gravest danger to cubs is infanticide: when a new coalition takes over, incoming males commonly kill existing cubs, which returns females to breeding condition sooner — disturbing behavior with a clear reproductive logic.

The lion is a powerful cultural symbol yet a species in decline. It is assessed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, threatened by habitat loss, prey depletion, and human–lion conflict, with trophy hunting a subject of ongoing scientific and ethical debate. Understood plainly, the lion teaches that sociality is an outcome of ecology rather than a fixed trait, and that cooperation and conflict can coexist within a single society.
