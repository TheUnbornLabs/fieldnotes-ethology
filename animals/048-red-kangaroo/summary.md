# Book 048 — The Red Kangaroo: Summary

The red kangaroo (*Osphranter rufus*, formerly *Macropus rufus*) is the largest living marsupial and the signature large mammal of the Australian arid interior. It is a macropod — a "big-footed" marsupial group that includes kangaroos, wallaroos, and wallabies — and lives across the dry inland plains, scrub, and grassland of mainland Australia. The IUCN Red List rates it Least Concern: it is abundant and widespread, with populations that swing dramatically with rainfall.

Its most famous feature is its locomotion. Instead of running on four legs, the red kangaroo hops (a gait called saltation), and the hop is unusually economical. Large tendons in the hind legs store elastic energy when the foot lands and return it on take-off, like a spring, so the energy cost of travelling a given distance stays roughly level, or even falls, as the animal speeds up over a wide range of speeds. Fast travel is therefore cheap — valuable for an animal that must cover long distances to reach the green feed that appears after patchy desert rain. At slow speeds it switches to a "pentapedal" walk in which its muscular tail plants on the ground and, with the forelimbs, bears and drives the body forward. The tail is a genuine fifth limb, not merely a balancing organ.

Red kangaroos are crepuscular and nocturnal grazers, feeding on green grasses and forbs in the cool hours and resting in shade through the heat of the day. They are strongly water-economical — subsisting on the moisture in green feed, concentrating their urine, and cooling themselves by licking their forearms — so they survive on far less water than a placental grazer of similar size. Socially they live in loose aggregations called mobs with fission-fusion membership: individuals join and leave freely, group composition changes constantly, and there is no rigid dominance hierarchy. The main benefit of grouping is shared vigilance in open country.

The species is markedly sexually dimorphic — males are much larger and more muscular than females — and males compete for access to receptive females in ritualised "boxing" contests, grappling with the forelimbs and kicking with the hind legs while balanced on the tail. Reproduction follows a distinctively marsupial plan. After a gestation of about a month the female gives birth to a bean-sized, embryo-like joey that crawls unaided to the pouch and completes its development there. Through embryonic diapause a female can hold a fertilised embryo in developmental pause, and she can support young at three stages at once — an embryo in waiting, a joey in the pouch, and an older joey at foot — even producing milk of different compositions for young of different ages. This staggered, pausable strategy lets breeding track an unpredictable environment with unusual precision.

The red kangaroo teaches that efficiency can be a whole way of life — in movement, in water use, and in reproduction — and that there is more than one good way to be a large grazing mammal.
