# Summary — The Fiddler Crab

The fiddler crab is one of ethology's clearest natural laboratories for studying sexual selection, animal communication, visual ecology, and biological timekeeping. Roughly 100 small species of the family Ocypodidae — long grouped as genus *Uca* and now split into genera such as *Leptuca*, *Austruca*, *Minuca*, and *Gelasimus* — live on tropical and warm-temperate intertidal mudflats, sandflats, salt marshes, and mangrove fringes worldwide. Their carapaces are only a centimetre or few across, but their behaviour is unusually rich and visible.

The species' defining feature is extreme, sex-limited claw asymmetry. A male carries one hugely enlarged claw (cheliped) that can reach a third of his body mass alongside a single tiny claw; females have two small claws. The giant claw cannot feed. It is a courtship signal and a weapon for ritualised male-male contests, while all feeding is done by the small claw. This makes waving an honest signal: it is costly to grow, costly to carry, conspicuous to predators, and it cuts the male's feeding rate, so only a fit male can wave vigorously and long. Different species have distinctive claw shapes and wave forms, letting females identify appropriate mates.

Fiddler crabs are deposit feeders. The small claw scoops sediment, mouthparts sort out microscopic algae, bacteria, and organic detritus, and the cleaned mineral leftovers are dropped as rounded pellets — pseudo-faeces, not true droppings. Their lives run on two internal clocks at once: a circadian (roughly 24-hour) clock and an endogenous tidal (roughly 12.4-hour) clock. Crabs emerge to feed and display at low tide, plug their burrows before the flood, and even show tidal colour-change rhythms that continue in constant laboratory conditions — evidence the timing is anticipated, not merely reactive.

Vision dominates their world. Stalked, near-panoramic compound eyes are tuned to the horizon of a flat habitat and are sensitive to movement, looming predators, and polarised light. Crabs navigate by path integration, keeping a running vector home so they can bolt straight to the burrow when a bird appears. In courtship, females assess wave rate, vigour, claw size, and burrow quality; some males build hoods or pillars that exploit a female's escape reflex. Neighbouring males sometimes synchronise their waves. Males regenerate a lost major claw as a slighter "leptochelous" claw over successive moults. Ecologically, their burrowing aerates coastal sediments (bioturbation), making them ecosystem engineers of wetlands whose main threat is habitat loss. The fiddler crab thus ties honest signalling, sensory ecology, and dual biological clocks together in one small, watchable animal.
