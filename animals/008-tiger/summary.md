# Summary — The Tiger (*Panthera tigris*)

The tiger, *Panthera tigris*, is the largest living cat and, for ethologists, the defining example of a solitary predator whose life is organised around space rather than society. Where a wolf pack makes cooperation visible, the tiger makes land tenure visible: an adult tiger lives mostly alone and holds an individual home range, using an elaborate system of scent-marking, ground scrapes, tree-raking, and long-carrying calls to keep neighbouring tigers informed and mostly apart. This signalling lets solitary animals share a landscape and find mates while avoiding costly fights. Tigers range across Asia, from tropical forests and grasslands to the cold forests of the Russian Far East, and are recognised as several subspecies and regional populations — Bengal, Amur (Siberian), Sumatran, Indochinese, and others — that differ in size, coat, and habitat. On the IUCN Red List the species is Endangered.

The tiger is a solitary ambush and stalk hunter. Unable to sustain a long chase, it relies on cover, patience, and a short explosive rush, usually killing large prey such as deer, wild pig, and wild cattle with a throat bite. Tiger numbers track prey numbers closely, so protecting tigers means protecting the animals they eat. The tiger perceives its world as a nocturnal hunter, weighted toward night vision and acute hearing, with smell serving mainly its social and territorial life through scent marks read with the flehmen response. It is also a strong, willing swimmer — unusual among cats — and its striped coat is both individually unique, used by researchers to identify individuals, and a camouflage tuned to dappled forest and grassland light.

Reproduction is polygynous and built on spatial overlap: a male's large range overlaps the smaller ranges of several females, and there is no lasting pair bond and no paternal care. A female raises her cubs entirely alone, hiding them, moving them between dens to avoid predators and infanticidal males, and teaching them to hunt over a dependency of roughly two years. Much of a tiger's competence as a predator is therefore learned, not innate.

Comparison with the pride-living lion shows how ecology drives sociality: from a shared big-cat template, open savanna favoured the social lion while dense cover favoured the solitary tiger. The two do not meet in the wild today, occupying separate continents. The tiger's decline is driven almost entirely by people — poaching for skins and body parts used in traditional medicine, depletion of wild prey, and habitat fragmentation, with tiger farms complicating enforcement. Man-eating is real but rare and context-specific, most associated with landscapes such as the Sundarbans. The tiger's enduring lessons are about spacing, the ecological roots of social structure, and the human dimension of conservation.
