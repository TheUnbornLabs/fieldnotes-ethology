# Citation Policy

Honest sourcing is a hard requirement for this collection. A confident-sounding but fabricated reference is worse than no reference. These rules are binding for every book.

## Acceptable source types

- Peer-reviewed scientific papers and review articles
- University and research-institution publications
- Natural history museum sources
- Recognized conservation organizations (e.g. IUCN, WWF, WCS)
- Government wildlife and environmental agencies
- Reputable field guides
- Standard animal-behavior and ethology textbooks

## The core rule: never invent citations

Do not fabricate authors, titles, journals, years, DOIs, page numbers, or URLs. Do not dress up a general recollection as a specific citation. If you cannot verify that a specific source exists as written, do not present it as verified.

## How to handle uncertainty

When a specific citation cannot be confirmed, use one of these honest forms instead of inventing detail:

- Cite the **type** of source and flag it: *"Peer-reviewed literature on [topic] — Further reading to verify before final publication."*
- Point to a **well-established, checkable resource** you are confident exists (e.g. the IUCN Red List species page, a museum's species profile, a widely used textbook by title and author) without inventing volume/page specifics.
- Add the standard flag line to any entry that needs checking: **"Further reading to verify before final publication."**

## Distinguishing verified from unverified

In each `references.md`, separate entries into:

1. **Well-established sources** — books, organizations, and databases that are safe to name (e.g. *Principles of Animal Communication* by Bradbury & Vehrencamp; the IUCN Red List; the Cornell Lab of Ornithology).
2. **To verify before publication** — specific studies or claims that should be checked against a database (PubMed, Google Scholar, JSTOR, the journal itself) before the book is published.

## What counts as "safe to name"

Long-standing, widely cited works and institutions with stable identities. Examples that are legitimate to reference by name and author/organization:

- Standard texts: Alcock, *Animal Behavior*; Bradbury & Vehrencamp, *Principles of Animal Communication*; Goodall, *The Chimpanzees of Gombe*.
- Institutions: IUCN Red List, Cornell Lab of Ornithology (All About Birds / Birds of the World), Smithsonian's National Zoo, AnimalDiversity Web (University of Michigan).

Even for these, do not invent specific edition numbers, page ranges, or article-level details you are unsure of.

## Reader-facing honesty

It is acceptable — and preferred — for a `references.md` to tell the reader plainly that some entries are suggested starting points requiring verification. Readers, teachers, and editors are better served by transparent uncertainty than by false precision.
